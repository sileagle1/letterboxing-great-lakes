From: Delirium (donaldson_home@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-11-28 06:05:27 UTC-08:00
Yes, I've been to some. "Squirrel's Stash", which used to be in West Bloomfield, MI, was like this. There are also quite a few like that in Rochester Hills, MI.
The only problem I've seen is that some geocachers who aren't familiar with letterboxing accidentally take the box's stamp, thinking it's a trinket. (Ask the placer of the "Stone Gulley" box how that goes...). My mother-in-law tells me that although the letterboxing prospects in the Memphis area are slim, geocaching is so big it was just recently covered by the local paper (again). Because of this, a local geocacher took pity on us 'boxers and planted a hybrid box.

As for my above mentioned new hometown, I find myself settling in nicely- as long as I don't come into contact with any locals. Their accents make me frantic, and my appearently quick speech cadence makes them just as alarmed. Two contacts have been marked with a lot of "I'm sorry?"s (me) and "Ma'am?"s. (them) It seems that "Ma'am?" roughly translates into "I didn't understand what you just said at the speed of light, could you please slow down, take a breath, and say it again slower?" I don't see myself as a fast speaker (and, as a former seventh grade teacher, I have been around the masters of fast talk- anyone who comes into contact with an excitable adolescent girl knows what I'm talking about), but my husband keeps telling me their going to put me in a nervous hospital if I don't calm down.

Also, it turns out that "hey" being directed at you is not, in fact, going to be followed by anything else. "Hey" equals "Hello". Now that I know this, I probably won't be shooting the greeters at Wal-Mart any more quizzical looks when they say this to me.

Ok, off to try and figure my way around town.
Cheers,
Delirium, still saying "pop", even though I've stopped ordering it at restaurants. Now I just stick to sweet tea.

We found a hybrid box (Explorer Park) in Indiana, but it was a little
different than you described. We found it listed on the LBNA site and it
had "normal" letterboxing clues but with a weird paragraph about not
exchanging stamps that made absolutely no sense to us until we found the
box and realized it was also a geocache. The stamp was well labeled and
it was neat to look through the trinkets in addition to seeing the stamps
of other people. I've also seen clues for a few in Ohio that start with
coordinates and then have lb clues from that starting point - but I
haven't found those boxes yet.

Re: Hybrid LB

From: Katelyn Burrill (wench116@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-11-29 19:39:44 UTC

We drove down near Scott Air Force Base near St. Louis this
Thanksgiving weekend, and I hoped to catch a couple boxes on the
way. However, most of the clues for St. Clair and Madison counties
are hybrid boxes. I was able to get letterbox clues for 5 of the 7 I
hoped to get to (I pass one of them each time I drive to visit my
parents!), and even met a geocacher on the way (he was willing to
lead us to one of the hybrids with only the GPS coordinates - thanks
Blue Bead Man!). These had 2 separate sections - one put-and-take
bag and one stamp and logbook bag. As these were the oldest
letterboxes I had found (the logbooks go back to 1999 and 2000), the
stamps were in pretty poor shape (likely from the extreme temperature
and humidity changes). But as they are listed on LbNA, I'll add them
to my find count.

The St. Louis area geocaches (and hybrids) seem to be geared towards
families with smaller children. The boxes are found fairly quickly
(for those with shorter attention spans and/or patience) and have
lots of neat toys (that I would have loved to trade when I was a kid!)
But the parks are just as nice to wander through and we had a fun,
but slightly chilly time finding them!

-- KissMeKate
