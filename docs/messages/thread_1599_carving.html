Oh I couldn't believe how much a lighted magnifying lens helped!! I'd
been wearing reading glassses to carve, but last Christmas Martini Man
got me a large lens with a circular light around the lens that lights
below the lens. The whole contraption mounts on a desk edge and has
extendible arms so the lens can easily be repositioned to wherever you
want. It is wonderful! (I believe he bought it at an art store)

For some reason, when I started using the lens, I also quit using the
Speedball carving tools (I had primarily used #1 and to a lesser extent
#4) and went to using only an xacto blade. I use the blade for tiny
areas and for large. Somehow I just seemed to figure out how to
control the blade at the same time.

From: azobox (jparkerg@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-07-14 16:30:58 UTC
Tiptoe,
I became an x-acto convert several months ago when I was working on a
series of stamps with more detail than I often attempt. Now I use the
no. 11 knife in most all but big areas that need "clearing" of lots of
material. I do have a very small veiner blade that I occasionally use
for very fine lines.
But to your question of MAGNIFICATION......

I use super-powerful reading glasses (from the drugstore) and
occasionally use them in conjunction with my regular glasses for
carving. Lots of other carvers use stationary magnifiers that sit on
stands or ones that hang around their neck. I actually have a
jeweler's loupe that attaches to my glasses' frame for super-duper
close-up views.
As others will likely attest, wearing the magnifiers can make you
dizzy if you look away from your work or try to get up and walk around
the room.


Good luck.

azobox

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2005-07-14 12:32:55 UTC-04:00
I purchased reading glasses from the $$ store that are about 3.0 strength and I wear these while I am carving. They work great and don't get in my way, although they make me very nauseous if I forget to take them off before get up and walk around. There are also some very nifty magnifying stands with and without lights that people use -- although I'm sure they are not from the $$ store.

As far carving with an Exacto knife... that's just how I learned to do it. I hold it in my hand like a pencil, at about a 45 degree angle to the carving material. First I carve along the line and then I come back about 1/8" away for the return cut, thus forming a "V" shaped piece of material that comes out. In this respect it is a lot like the "V" tool, except I can much more easily cary the angle with the knife. The main difference is that I feel I have much more control in varying the depth depending on the detail of what I am carving. Also I find it easier to get a sharp, clean, tight corner using the Exacto knife. I have a couple of specialty Exacto-type knives that have very small blades -- one even uses a needle type end. A set of these were sent to me by Legerdemaine, a truly awesome carver. The others I have found at craft and hardware stores.

But it really is just what you get used to. I have seen some of ScoutDogs carvings and they are amazing, and she carves almost exclusively with the tools (and modified versions of them). It is more a steady hand and patience than the tools you are using, although if you are having trouble with one method, it is certainly worth it to try another.

SpringChick

From: Kyle Klein (armadillo1111@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2005-07-14 12:02:55 UTC-07:00
If you are going to squeze the #1 so that you get a finer blade, it is best to put something in the middle and then squeeze. For example, one carver I know heats the cutter over a flame and then inserts a thin knife into the slot of the #1 and uses pliars to squeeze the edges up against the blade. This makes a cutter for finer cuts.

I've also heard that magnifying is a good idea also. It helps with the detail and keeps you from cutting too deep, as even small depths look like canyons.
