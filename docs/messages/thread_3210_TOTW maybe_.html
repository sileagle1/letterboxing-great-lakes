The kids and I went on a day-long boxing trip in early January of
this year--right before they had to go back to school after
Christmas break. We headed up to Lake County and did alot of boxes
by Beetle and a few others as well. It was a wonderful day! There
was a little bit of snow on the ground so everything looked pretty,
but it wasn't that cold (for January). We had our boots and mittens
and plenty of layers on. I packed lots of snacks and a big thermos
of hot chocolate and we were ready to head out. We would do a few
boxes and then have some snacks and hot chocolate (and also warm up
as we drove to the next area where boxes were). The kids had a
great time climbing on the giant rocks at one of the beaches we went
to in search of a box. And I saw a common goldeneye duck out on
Lake Michigan (a new find for me).

I think it worked because we stayed warm, we alternated boxing trips
from more urban ones to ones out in the woods, because there were
plenty of snacks and hot chocolate, and probably because the kids
were getting tired of Christmas break and were ready for an
adventure. The only downside to the trip was driving back home in
rush hour traffic all the way from Lake County(but we just had to
get those last few boxes!)

From: ontario_cacher (ontario_cacher@yahoo.ca) | Date: 2006-10-24 16:57:40 UTC
A sunny windless winter day is a nice time to be out. The thing I
don't like about winter tupperware hunts is road and parking
conditions. I've slid into a ditch once while trying to park on a
country road where the ditches were filled with snow. I know people
who have been stuck in trailhead parking lots that are not plowed. I
highly recommend a cell phone and an automobile association card. The
other thing that can be irksome are frozen boxes - some of my boxes
are guilty of getting frozen-in. I put a note in my clue pages to let
people know that it's likely that certain boxes will be frozen in the
winter.

Absolutely! I love winter boxing. I am big on boxing
when most others aren't i.e. I do ALOT of my boxing
during the wee hours of the morning. My most memorable
winter boxing escapade was last winter here in
Minneapolis. I was looking for "Bridging the River 2"
by The Red Cat on the banks of the Mississippi river.
I had just finished work and it was about 1:30 a.m.
(try combining winter and late night boxing for a real
treat). It was about 12 degrees outside and the river
was maybe 15 feet from the boxes hiding spot. My work
shoes were completely devoid of any traction, think
racing slicks. I walked around the side of the tree
that was facing the river and before I knew what
happened I was sliding towards an icy, dirty river. I
was able to stop myself 5 feet from the rivers edge
after sliding for 10 feet. My hands were cut up from
trying to grab branches and hold on which is hard to
do when it's 12 degrees outside. Even harder though
was climbing back up the embankment with freezing
bleeding hands and then finding the box and stamping
in. Ah yes, the things we do to find a piece of carved
rubber : ) Good times people, good times.

I learned about letterboxing in February 2005, and since I didn't
want to wait to get started, all of our first boxes were found in the
winter. Probably my favorite letterboxing memory is of my first box,
"Red Cedar Box" on MSU's campus. It was snowing very hard but I was
determined to go anyway since I had spent the weekend getting my
stamp and logbook ready. My husband and I found it (I remember how
amazed I was that it was right where it was supposed to be) and went
back to the car to stamp in. We got a few other boxes that winter
including "Patriarche Park" (it's a wonder we found it since the
trail referenced in the clue was not visible due to snow cover),
"Burchfield's Bounty," and "Fitzgerald's Find."

A more recent winter letterboxing experience was less successful, but
makes for a good story. Last March we went to the Traverse City area
for a few days. On the way up we tried to get a box that I had long
wanted to get -- the Clare Roadside Park box. What we didn't realize
is that the park is not maintained in the winter. We pulled in and
then immediately realized that it was a mistake because it was
completely covered with snow. We then got stuck and spent a while
trying to dig out before a guy in a big pickup pulled in. "Need some
help?" he said. I didn't know how to explain why we had thought it
was a good idea to pull a Honda Civic into a snow-covered roadside
park. I said, "I guess you're probably wondering how we were stupid
enough to get into this situation." He just said, "It happens," and
then helped push us back out.

I was disappointed that we weren't able to look for the box and hoped
to get it on my next trip up north. Unfortunately, by the time we
next headed up that way, the box was reported missing.


Heather

From: lesann847 (Acorn@fools-errand.org) | Date: 2006-10-26 11:56:40 UTC
That's where you have to be clever. I've tried just about every strategy I've seen in the
moviesand some I haven't. Here are a few:

Bring a big dog and let her confuse the tracks. Maybe a horse would work?
On snowshoes, stop at the box, but don't move around as you open the box and stamp
in. Then, keep on going in the same direction as if you never paused. Same strategy
would work on foot.
Bring a little bottle of yellow food coloring and camouflage your activity.

I've also had to pass by boxes hidden in more open areas where camouflaging my
approach would be difficult. I simply returned in the spring.

Of course, it's frustrating when you've been oh so clever and discreet and the darned box
is frozen tight in place.

Nevertheless, winter boxing runs a close second to autumn boxing in my book because I
seldom see anyone on the trail. The solitude, quiet and beauty is priceless.

From: Silent Doug (silentdoug@letterboxing.info) | Date: 2006-10-26 11:14:23 UTC-04:00
At 01:50 PM 10/25/2006, you wrote:
>If you go letterboxing in the winter, wouldn't you leave a trail of
>footprints in the snow leading right to the hiding place?

No. We'd leave a trail of snowshoe prints....

:-)

This is really no different than in the summer -- if you're not
careful, you can leave traces of your footsteps as you trample ferns
or other plants as you head from the trail to a box hiding place,
which can develop into a social trail over time. This is why you
should *never* walk from a trail directly to an off-trail hiding
place, and always find a non-direct route. When we letterbox in the
snow, we always look for a roundabout route to the hiding place,
finding a place where our footprints aren't going to be obvious and
hike over to the hiding place via the back way.

Since there are so relatively fewer hikers in the winter, there's
much less risk of being seen or having your footprints be seen, too.
And it's easy to use a branch to sweep snow into your footprints and
cover up your trail completely as you back away from the box.....
