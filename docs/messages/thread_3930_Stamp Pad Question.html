From: bindlestiff_boxer (bindlestiff_boxer@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-04-07 14:53:09 UTC
I just added a new folder to the files section of the group on inks
and stamp pads. There are three files in the folder that I copied
over from my old Letterboxing_Bindlestiff yahoogroup. I think you'll
find some helpful information in the files. Here's a link to the
files area (note, you'll have to be signed in to yahoo to access it)

http://groups.yahoo.com/group/LbGLK/files/

As a general rule, pigment inks dry out less quickly than dye or
hybrid types do and slovent based inks dry out the fastest.

Personally, I wouldn't want a Petal Point in a letterboxing pack. For
one thing, even though the individual pads are small, the overall
size isn't. Also, the lid doesn't have a positive latching system so
it's possible it may one day open up inside the bag and make a mess
of everything around it and finally, pigment ink takes time to dry so
it's possible that it will smear in logbooks.

Hope my two cets (and the files) are helpful.

From: xxxxxxxx (BrighidFarm@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-04-08 10:04:10 UTC-05:00
Pigment ink in general should be just fine. I'm an artist and often use
stamping in my artwork, and it's usually using my own hand-carved stamps,
and both pigment inks or dye inks work fine. Pigment inks are slower drying
and thus easier to clean off a stamp if a person blots it off quickly, but
pigment inks also don't hold up nearly as well to the weather elements that
might attack a logbook since most letterboxes are outdoors.

I steer clear of chalk inks because those can leave small particles behind,
particularly as the stamp pad gets older and begins to dry out.

I also steer clear of any solvent based inks like Stazon or alcohol inks for
either hand-carved or "clear" cling-type stamps. Eventually the solvent
will harm the stamp and the colors tend to be very permanent, making it
often difficult to switch colors without harming the stamp because it's so
difficult to clean off the previous color enough so that it doesn't corrupt
the next color. Solvent based stamp pads also tend to dry out much quicker
than other stamp pads, so if a person doesn't use them often, they might not
consider it to be worth the price.

You might also want to consider just carving a quickie stamp or two just to
use to experiment on with inks and markers to see what you like. In the
past couple of years, there's really been sort of an explosion of available
inks, everybody and their brother and sister is coming up with new inks,
particularly if a person buys online, and so much of the literature out
there dealing with ink comparisons is often outdated.

Has anyone ever considered not stamping into a letterbox's logbook itself?
I was thinking of making some ATCs which would include my stamped image of
my sig stamp and putting one in the logbook when I find a box. That way I
could make my own backgrounds, do some embellishing, etc, plus laminate the
ATC at home before I set out, and just tack it into the logbook.
Obviously, this isn't something that's going to interest anyone who likes to
find a ton of boxes in a day and anyone who isn't an artist. Altho it's
easy to make a lot of ATCs at once as far as background goes -- designing
and making one large background sheet and then cutting it up. But Ancient
took my idea and made a bunch of ATCs using her sig stamp with some
background papers Ancient had made last summer but hadn't found a use for
yet, and they look terrific. But to stand up to the elements in a logbook,
the ATCs would have to be laminated. Or possibly use acetate with an acohol
ink background but then something like Stazon would have to be used for the
sig stamp which I wouldn't want to do to my sig stamp.

That's what I've thought about doing with boxes I find this year. Making a
pre-made background on cardstock before I leave home based on what the theme
of the stamp or box is or seems to be, stamping onto that cardstock, and
then laminating it when I get home and cutting it down to an ATC size. Not
to be traded, of course, but just to go in my letterboxing scrapbook.
Again, not something that's going to be done by someone who's out to find a
lot of boxes in a set time frame.

I wish I'd thought to do that with the Edgar Allan Poe box here in Villa
Park. Edgar Allan Poe is great ATC material.
