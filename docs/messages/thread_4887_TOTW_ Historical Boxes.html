TOTW: Historical Boxes

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2008-03-30 14:33:15 UTC
I was having an interesting conversation with some fellow letterboxers
yesterday and the topic of historical boxes came up (i.e. boxes that
have been in place for several years). It got me to thinking about
how these boxes that have survived the test of time are viewed by
other letterboxers. Is the opinion the same for seasoned letterboxers
as it is for newer letterboxers?

When choosing the boxes you look for, is the age of the box a factor
in your decision? Positive or negative? Do you consider it more of a
*find* to track down a box that has been hiding out for 5 years versus
a box that was placed a month ago? Do you look forward to finding a
bulging logbook, filled with stamps from letterboxers from across the
country or do you worry that an older box may be missing or in bad
repair? Or perhaps is the age of the box not really a factor to you
at all, rather you make choices based primarily on other things --
clues, hike distance, location, planter, etc.

As time goes on, there are many boxes within the GL area that have
stood the test of time and are aging nicely, but from my own personal
experience, they don't see a lot of traffic. One reason is that many
seasoned letterboxers have already found these boxes. Another is that
there are now so many boxes for a person to choose from that any one
individual box holds much less probability of being the box of choice.
This is where I wonder if the historical factor matters or not.

Is there a value to maintaining these older boxes? Or would it make
sense to breathe fresh life into some of these areas by putting out
new boxes?

SpringChick


Re: [LbGLK] TOTW: Historical Boxes

From: lcoil79@ameritech.net smokey (ainawgsd@yahoo.com) | Date: 2008-03-30 08:37:46 UTC-07:00
You know, I hadn't really given this topic much thought. Age is not really a factor in determining whether I go after a particular box or not. My decisions are based on (in this order) location (is it nearby someplace I am traveling? are there other boxes in the area? is it someplace I can bring the dogs?), hike distance (am I on a time schedule with limited time to box or am I on a daytrip and looking for an all-day hike to make my drive worth while?), clues (can I figure them out or get someone to help me decipher them? or am I feeling lazy that week?), and lastly planter (this is really a low priority...but some carvers are just plain amazing and I have run across the rare planter whose boxes are consistently in poor repair or missing and it isn't usually worth it to me to go after a box I *know* probably isn't there). For me, the age of a box doesn't really give me much information about the likely condition of the box. I do enjoy looking at logbooks with lots of
other boxer's stamps in them. There is a thrill to being a first finder, but I really enjoy looking at the carving work of everyone who was there before me even more than being the first to put my stamp in a book....especially if we are boxing on a trip out of our home territory.

I think there is a certain value to maintaining at least some of the older boxes. While I don't necessarily see age as an incentive to look for a box, there is a certain satisfaction of finding a box that has been in place longer than I've been letterboxing...which is going on 4 years this summer. For a box to stay in place for that long it means that a certain level of care and planning and some small amount of luck had to go into placing it. Especially around here, where the wide-open landscape and flood plains tend to make finding a really good hiding spot that won't rot away in a few years very difficult. As my own boxes age I do remove and replace some of them...especially if the stamp wasn't something I was particularly proud of or the amount of traffic was particularly low. Sometimes I re-use the hiding place, using new clues and/or a new route to take boxers to the location. Other times, I determine that I had enough trouble with that box and decide that the
location just isn't a good one.

From: ruhlette (ruhlette@yahoo.com) | Date: 2008-03-30 16:08:49 UTC-07:00
I definitely have the "older, historical" letterboxes on my radar - even
if they are reported as status unknown. When I started I think there were
a total of 46 in my entire state and I was determined to get them all.
When I managed to whittle the list down to about 15 to go, the new box
numbers were climbing faster than I could get to them. Now I don't even
try to get every one, but I still want to find all the active boxes that
were out there when I started in April, 2004.

I haven't heard an update on a number of my older boxes, and I have been
wondering if I should go and pick them up and retire them. I believe if I
haven't received an attempted-but-not-found report, that the boxes are
most likely still in place. If I get a report of -ewww, it's all wet and
moldy - then I will probably retire that type.

Interestingly enough my Dora series has not been recorded for several
months, and I put 4 new logbooks out last summer. It will be FOUR years
old in August. Many new boxers tell me that this was their first
experience and they really enjoyed them. They are also in spots I think
would be easy to get muggled. But they are surviving! I never expected
them to last this long, and since I have several logbooks from the past,
if they go missing I will not be sad.

With the price of gas, we cannot letterbox every weekend. I study the
clues for every letterbox and then hand-pick a few, but not all.

I will be in the Milwaukee area at the end of June for another commitment
and I have prioritized my list -- I must have Triangles, Triangles,
Triangles Everywhere (1&2) and Milwaukee County Parks Series. I don't
know if Acorn's Happiness is still out there, but I would like to get that
one too. If I can manage a trip in August, I want to visit the RenFaire.
Oh, there are more to find, but they will have to wait. I look at the
titles as much as I do the actual hunt; I am easily amused.

I posted a note on the OHIO board at AQ. We live about 1 hour from
Cincinnati and SW OHIO, an early letterboxing hot spot. I was shocked to
be the First Finder on 4 great boxes in Hamilton that were placed more
than 4 months ago. I think there are just too many new boxes to choose.
I am not a prolific planter, but I am ruminating over some major project
ideas. I don't want to get distracted anymore than I already am.

I don't think it's necessarily a good idea to reseed/saturate an area with
an historic box just to increase traffic because I fear the historic box
could become unduly jeopardized.

Very good TOTW!

~speedsquare

For B and me, how long a box has been out is rarely a factor. We

and "The Source of the Sacramento" carved on site by dvn2r ckr and hidden in
2002.

Please keep these wonderful boxes out as long as possible, nee forever as I
hope to someday find them all.

One thing to keep in mind about those numbers - that is only boxes that remain active on the letterboxing.org website. There may have been more boxes planted in those early years, that have since gone missing or been retired, and so wouldn't show up in the current totals. But, no question about the exponential increase in planted boxes, year by year.

Personally I think there's an extra bit of intrigue with the boxes that have been in place for 4 - 5 years, or more... I think maybe it's just because I've seen the clues out there for so long, and have had them on a list to find, or have plotted them out on a map, or whatever it might be, that once I finally have the opportunity to find them, it's like making a long-awaited visit to a famous landmark. Also, how can you not get a kick out of seeing a logbook that's stamped "The Dragon - P13 F30" or "Wisconsin Hiker & Martini Man - F10" or the like???

According to LBNA only 36 boxes were planted in 1999, 43 boxes in 2000, 205
boxes in 2001, 771 boxes in 2002 and an explosion of 2,127 boxes in 2003.

I then searched for boxes planted during my first year of boxing (Nov. 25,
2006 - Nov. 25, 2007) and there are 8,506 plants listed!

Before you know it there will be 10,000 planted boxes per year.

I've found 11 boxes planted in 2003 and before, and with each of them I got
a special thrill . . . "It's still there!!" My happy dance when clutching one
of these boxes surpasses any other happy dance I have.

Two of my favorites historic boxes were DMS's "Mt. Tabor" planted in 1999
and "The Source of the Sacramento" carved on site by dvn2r ckr and hidden in
2002.

Please keep these wonderful boxes out as long as possible, nee forever as I
hope to someday find them all.

the thrill that it gave us to see that only Pete and Wanda and a few local folks had found it
in all those years. It was so much more satisfying to ink our little boots into that log than
the hundreds previous. I know the placer is not local, and it is debatable that they were

feedback. But, WOW! Was it sweet to find it after obsessing over it for so long.

From: wood thrush (wood_thrush@mac.com) | Date: 2008-04-07 15:05:34 UTC
Hello GL friends, this is Wood Thrush over in Maryland. A few weeks
ago I spent four lovely (rainy, chilly) days in the area between
Cincinnati and Columbus, and thought I'd share my thoughts here on
historic boxes and my "vacation" boxing.

I usually print out all the clues on the two main sites plus any clues
I know about on private sites. Since it's vacation boxing I do a rough
sift and drop boxes that have multiple attempts. (If I were local I
would not care, but I don't tend to take as many chances with vacation
boxing.) I take all remaining clues along with me, just in case I wind
up in a place I had not thought I'd be visiting.

I do prioritize some "must-visit" boxes and try to get to those if at
all possible. In my case the factors are (in no particular order)
historicity, which I like; cool clues; cool location/hike (as best I
can tell); and boxers I've heard of (either because of their
longevity, their historical importance, their rep for good maintenance
of their plants, their carving ability--this is pretty broad).

Historic boxes are definitely a factor for me when I am vacation
boxing. I had an absolute blast on my trip, and had it not been
flooding, there are several other historic boxes I would have tried
for. I hope they survived the waters intact.

I found 25 boxes and attempted another 8 or so that I did not come up
with. My favorite "miss" was Ft. Ancient. I conclude I misinterpreted
the clues on that one. Went in what I believe was the "correct"
direction first for about a mile--but did not go far enough, I now
believe. Retraced my steps and went about 3 more miles in the WRONG
direction, finally concluding I had been right to start with. Of
course, it was dark by then, LOL. But I had a blast and met some
interesting local pottery/artist folks on the trail, and had a nice
long hike in the process. Great location too.

My favorite box I actually found was The Wisconsin Gas Flame. This box
had it all. It was an older box with awesome stamps in the log, and
the instructions inside the box on "how" to stamp in were a hoot. So
much fun to stamp in the correct manner and then read the log entries.
Excellent, creative carve, fun clues, what could be better?

Love the older boxes.

wood thrush
