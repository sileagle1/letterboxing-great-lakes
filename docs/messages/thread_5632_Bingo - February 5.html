You know--I was a little bummed today when I saw that I had the
word. With my competitive nature--if I'm going to lose, then I want
to lose big! Plus, I had spent all of my carpooling downtime
thinking of a wonderful little story. Since I went through the
thinking effort, I thought I would share it anyway. So here goes--
BirdWoman/Waltzing Pigs
=========================

I was sitting in the break room with a cup of joe when Pete came in
with an assignment: a distraught letterboxer was in need of
immediate attention and I should get right on it. This was my chance
to prove myself! As I reviewed her file, I was quite concerned when
I saw the location; it was a town FULL of cemeteries. I hoped that
wasn't a bad omen.

As I headed down to earth, I could see a woman standing on a bridge
overlooking the Eisenhower. "Oh no, I hope I'm not too late", I
thought. I quickly circled downwardyes, in a circle (it's hard to
go straight when you don't have wings). As I approached, I realized
that she wasn't going to jump, but she was holding a bag over the
expressway. I introduced myself and said that I was here to help.
Then I asked her what she was doing. She sobbed "I wish I had never
become a letterboxer! Nothing is going rightespecially in BINGO.
I'm throwing it all awaymy logbook, my stamp, my ink and markers,
and most of all my BINGO card! This card is definitely NOT bleeping
golden!" She dangled her bag over the cars rushing by down below.

I couldn't let her drop that bag! Thinking quickly, I
said, "Remember the time when you were winter boxing by that lake. A
little girl on a sled zoomed by you and crashed through the ice. If
you hadn't been thereif you hadn't been letterboxingshe would have
drowned or frozen to death".

The woman said "Yeah, I do remember that", and pulled her bag down by
her side. But she remained upset, so there was still work to do. I
asked her to walk with me. We came down the hill. I steered her
towards a little table in a gazebo and pulled out another tidbit from
her file.

"Think back to last spring when you were on the trails", I
said. "Remember when you came across that family new to
letterboxing. The grandma was just about to put her hand down that
hole to find the letterbox, and you grabbed her hand away. You told
her you should always probe a crevice like that with a stick. When
you put the stick in, a poisonous snake slithered out. If you hadn't
been thereif you hadn't been letterboxingshe might have been
seriously injured."

The woman nodded her head. I was making progress! We stood up and
headed down the streetwe walked away from the sunrise because I
needed to keep a close eye on her and didn't want to be blinded by
the light. One more memory might do it. I said, "I want you to
remember the gathering you were at a few years ago. There was one
couple there who were hoping to hit a real milestone there100,000
finds. And then disaster struck. They were so engrossed with the
hunt that they didn't notice that they had stumbled into an art
studio and damaged a vase. The angry potter grabbed all their
supplies and threw them into his kiln. But you were there to save
the day. You gave them your clue booklet, along with some other
supplies. You made it possible for them to fulfill their dreams."

"I'm still not sure I want to go on", she said. Just then, I saw her
friend Thomas by the side of the road. "Let's go sit by him", I
said. Thomas said, "We know you've been through a lot lately, so
everyone pitched in to get you this pile of letterboxing booty".

The woman seemed overwhelmed by the gesture. Then she broke into a
big smile and exclaimed, "I DO want to go letterboxing again, right
here and right now! It's a wonderful hobby!"

My job was done, so I headed back up to HQ to report in. Pete
said, "Clarence, that was a job well doneyou've earned your wings".

My last words to you all are: "Remember that every time a stamp is
inked, a letterboxing angel earns their wings."
