A Nice LB story...

From: jlizner (jlizner@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-01-18 02:56:27 UTC
I don't normally post anything, but I wanted to share this story
with everyone. It may qualify for the 'You know your obsessed
when...' topic, or it may (I hope) be a nice story.

This past Sunday, I was gathered in Illinois with my extended family
(I'm from Michigan) to celebrate my Grandma's life. She was a really
special lady, a major part of my life, an incredible woman, and she
passed away on Thursday, January 12th.

My mom had suggested I bring my letterboxing equipment with me, as
you never know what may happen. She knew I was on box #99, just
waiting for #100.

As it happened, my Grandpa (a wonderful man who is very set in his
ways), was a little tired and overwhelmed from all the activity and
people who had decended into his life since Grandma's passing. On
Sunday afternoon, it became clear that he needed some quiet time (to
watch college basketball, do the crossword puzzzle, and take his
Sunday afternoon nap). So I went out with two of my sisters and
three of my female cousins (who had heard about LB-ing and were
anxious to try it) to find a series that was planted in Champaign.
If you live in that area of Illinois, you may remember the sunshine
and warm temperatures of Sunday and Monday.

Now, that's the part of this story that could sound a little selfish
and obsessive (and my friends will tell you that I do have a little
bit of an obsessive mind!); but what happened was a really lovely
experience.

In the process of searching for this 6 box series, 6 family members
from the age of 10 to 36, from two different states, and in several
different phases of life, were able to gather together and spend an
afternoon together. We caught up on what was happening in each
other's lives, remembered Grandma (remembering stories that we all
knew, and sharing some stories for the first time). We initiated the
youngest (Gracie) into the odd society that is the women in our
family (starting with our mothers and their cousins - the original
Moo Mau Chorus Girls!) and grew closer together as sisters and
cousins.

I know it's exactly what Grandma would have wanted - all of us
gathered together having fun (not sitting around being sad and
upsetting Grandpa) and being important to each other. If Grandma was
nothing else, she was family. She lived for her family and left a
huge legacy (16 grandchildren and 4 greats).

And now, we all (who sometimes tend to be too busy, too involved in
our own lives, too fill-in-the-blank) have a really special memory
of time spent together in laughter, joy, and celebration in the
midst of our sadness.

I loved my Grandma dearly, and wasn't sure how I would make it
through this weekend (which was a long time coming), and here this
odd little hobby of looking for plastic in the woods brought
together the elements of comfort and family that provided the
strength I needed (and didn't think I had within myself). What an
unexpected but welcome gift!


P.S. I found my 100th box (am up to 104) and have a special page
dedicated to this day and my Grandma Cromlich, complete with inked
thumbprints of all my cousins and sisters who shared the search with
me! There's even been talk of planting a box for Grandma - no for
sure plans yet, but the wheels are turning!
