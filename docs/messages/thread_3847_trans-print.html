From: Wisconsin Hiker (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2007-03-15 16:40:42 UTC
My understanding is that in order for the acetone to work well, it is
best to have an image that was made with "toner". This is the media
that is in many photocopy machines (although I hear some new copy
machines don't use toner). Laser printers also use toner, so you
could print an image on a laser printer, then use the acetone
approach. You can get a big bottle of acetone for not much $ at most
drugstores. Cheaper than buying little bottles of nail polish
remover. I place the image from the photocopy machine upside down on
the PZKut, then use an old washcloth to put a small amount of acetone
on. I then press FIRMLY on portions of the imagage, rewetting with
more acetone as required. It is best not to rub the image, just move
around pressing down firmly in each area. You can then slowly peel a
corner back to be sure it has transferred well. If not, just
continue with more acetone and pressing hard.

Other people have good luck with an iron for transferring images, but
I never could get it to work for me. I think Merlin uses a combo
approach of acetone AND heat.

Wisconsin Hiker

This is the method I used for The Rose Gardeners. I had to make a couple
copies of my image to test each side. I got the first try to wet and so
the acetone blurred the image. Second try was much improved. Added to
the mix was a nice magnifying lamp and exacto knife. I might add it was
my first attempt on PZKut, first carving with exacto, and first with the
magnifying lamp.

If you goof up, the acetone will wash the toner/pencil away. Just try
again.

I use the combo of acetone and heat and it is amazing how clearly the
image is on the PZ Cut--now if I could just carve it exactly as I see
it. I use an old copying machine to make a copy (I too have discovered
that some new machines don't use the toner to make this work). I use a
cottonball to rub acetone on the surface of the PZ Cut. Then I
immediately place the image face-down and use a hot iron to really
transfer the image. It transfers so well that the paper is almost
sticking to the PZ Cut. I put a piece of paper between the iron and
the paper with the image--I ironed the PZ Cut itself once and that
isn't the best thing for my iron (just gives me another excuse not to
iron!). The only drawback I have noticed is that the dark ink of the
toner transfers a little when I first start inking the stamp--esp. with
light-colored inks. It doesn't seem to last, but does anyone have any
suggestions for dealing with that?

It is not harmless to the carving material, but if used carefully it's
still an excellent method.
The acetone will dissolve/melt the carving medium but you'd have to
use way more than necessary or to scrub on the block to cause a
serious problem.
So it's just the opposite of alcohol. The alcohol dries the stuff; the
acetone can make it squishy
Someone in an earlier post suggested using a bit of well placed
acetone to remove toner that clings to the stamp when you're finished.
However if there is *very* fine detail on the stamp -- crosshatch for
example -- the final dose of acetone may blurr the detail.
It's just a case of using care.
