MN state park experience

From: jimanellie63 (jimanellie63@yahoo.com) | Date: 2008-07-10 21:44:07 UTC
One of my first boxes placed was in Glendalough State Park. At that
time, I was unaware of the state park rules--my bad. I had heard from
another boxer that one of theirs had been taken by the manager, but now
the rules were changed, and it was allowed, so I placed mine.

After about 6 months, I got an e-mail from the manager that I had not
filed an application, and if I didn't contact him by July 1, it would
be confiscated. I immediately wrote an apology e-mail, asking what the
next step was. He never bothered to answer. In the meantime, I found
the DNR site and printed an application, intending to submit it and
have the box approved. Then I thought better, and pulled it from the
park entirely. Evidently, letterboxes are not welcome. (I do notice,
though, that there is at least one Geocache there.)

I thought I could help draw people to the state park system, and
particularly to this gem of a park, but I guess I'll be placing this
box elsewhere, as well as more in the planning.

Re:MN state park experience

From: Marty Sell (m_sell31@yahoo.com) | Date: 2008-07-11 17:15:38 UTC-07:00
I work in a Michigan state park and i also enjoy letteroxing. Here is the problem, unlike geocashing, which has gone through the process of getting a state wide land use permit for DNR land, letterboxing has no permit. I'm not sure what the process would be but letterboxing should do the same. Other wise you have to go through the process of getting permits on a park by park basis, and to be honest, most people working in the field don't like stuff being hidden in the parks. People on the government end, in michigan thats the DNR folk who work in Lansing, often see things differently and would probably grant a statewide permit. I know that if my manager knew there was one in my park it would be removed. It would be a great way to draw business to the parks but there is not enough letterboxers that its going to make a huge difference either way. The bottom line is that if you want to hide boxes on state land it would be to the benifit of all
letterboxers for a group to work out a deal with individual state governments to get a statewide land use permit.

My personal experience in the Michigan State Parks has been quite to the contrary. I have generally found them to be quite accepting and cooperative. There was actually one park that had the clues available at the office and suggested it to people who were setting out on a hike.

I am curious however... why do letterboxers/letterboxes seem to get lumped in with geocachers/geocaches for purpose of restrictions and rules (i.e. most places where letterboxes are prohibited do not have a policy specifically referring to letterboxes, rather they have a policy regarding geocaches and letterboxers are just get held to the same policy), but when the geocachers have a blanket permit, letterboxers aren't just covered by that?
