First time out -- I'm not quite a letterboxer yet!

From: trixiemixie (mix30@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-01-07 22:42:56 UTC
Well, my family set out to find our first box today. I spent 90
minutes hiking a trail only to realize that the box's clue intended
to lead me to a location not 75 feet from where the car was parked!
Anyway, I will add to my essentials kit the following: mace, for all
the dogs that were off leash and approached/rushed me with varying
degrees of happy doggy-ness and rage in their eyes; food, yes, food,
because I was out there for a long time (fam stayed in the car,
they're so smart) and I got cold and hungry; a compass (this
particular enclosed location and its clue did not require a compass
to find, but it would have been nice to tell the rescuers where I was
in case I got stranded!); better shoes (I had no idea the differing
terrains there were on this trail).

So, while we did not find anything to stamp, I feel better prepared
to go out a second time.

When we did realize that the box was planted so close to us, though,
our poking around yielded nothing -- perhaps due to the densely
packed vegetation. We'll try again in the spring when the plants
are standing up and the surrounding area isn't as crowded with
tangled brush stuff.

In the meantime, I am secretly thanking the box's placer for giving
me the opportunity to get out there today and earn 5 activity points
(for the unecessary hike) for my Weight Watchers effort!

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-01-07 23:33:30 UTC
Fact: The shortest route to the letterbox is not always the path
outlined in the clue.

As you do more letterboxes you will learn to spot the clues that do
this -- take you round about back to the box which is very near
where you started. Boxers often do this because it makes maintenance
easier, but yet provides a challenging hike for the finder. You will
also learn to read the clues and look for shortcuts using the trail
maps, etc. (except in the case of some planters who take steps to
make it very difficult for you to do this).

I am with you on the dog issue -- this is one of my pet peeves. I
am not a dog-hater, I own dogs, I just don't like strange dogs
approaching me unexpectedly and it really annoys me that some pet
owners are thoughtless in this way when signs clearly state that
pets must be on a leash. As for the food -- just make sure it is
something that will not leave food smell on your fingers as handling
a letterbox and its contents with smelly fingers is an open
invitation to critters once you have left the box.

Sorry you didn't find the box in the end, but kudos to you on the 5
AP you earned!

SpringChick

From: (katy@dsa132.org) | Date: 2007-01-08 00:15:39 UTC
Sounds like you have a good attitude, though, about not finding the box.
Although I do get discouraged when boxes aren't in place or I'm just too
inept to find them, I have to say that some of the best letterboxing
experiences, for me, have very little to do with the boxes.

For example, we stopped at this awesome trail in Ohio while traveling
home for Christmas. The trail actually goes under the interstate, a spot
I'd often admired on my treks to/from my parents' house. There was a
paved trail and a parallel mulch trail and a parallel series of fields,
some with playground equipment or soccer fields or just open areas.
Although we only found one of the four boxes, hiking to find them worked
all the stiffness and restlessness from driving out of my limbs. The
trail was so beautiful and I understand why the Olentangy has a "scenic
river" designation. It's a spot that I will visit again when I'm
traveling that direction, whether the boxes are still there or not.

On the dog note, I have to agree with you about strange dogs approaching.
I have actually started claiming that Claire is a vicious pit bull just
to make people keep their crazy dogs away. One of the things that really
impressed me about our Christmas letterboxing experience was that all the
dogs we saw were off leash (probably close to a dozen in our 2 miles of
hiking) but not one of them came within 20 feet of us; they were all so
very well-behaved and under control.

Hopefully your next trip will be more fruitful!
Katy

That has happened to me before! In fact, pretty nearly happened today!
But, a good deal of the point of this odd hobby is the getting out
and doing the physical part. It's not just finding the box itself.

Actually, we did two boxes today, the first had a parking lot right
next to where we ended up, but we were instructed to park a mile or
so away. This was kind of an "urban" box and we pretty much knew we
could find it from the car and park closer. But, rain and cold
weather be damned, we parked where we were supposed to and did the
box as the planter intended. It was fun to see how and where they
came up with the clues. The used all the store and restaurant names
as puns to lead us on the way.

The second box pretty much had us start about 100 feet from the box
and took us in a big circle around it. We didn't have to hike for 90
minutes, but the clues definitely went the indirect way! Not to
mention I'm an idiot and we started from the way wrong place and
probably added a mile onto our walk before we got on the right
track. LOL That's the fun, right? Getting into the placers head to
see what they were thinking. We saw a whole bunch of really cool
trees that we wouldn't have seen by the direct route.

I'm sorry you didn't find the box, but hope you had a great time, anyway!

From: xxxxxxxx (BrighidFarm@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-01-09 08:05:56 UTC-06:00
I love that site. The closer ya can zoom in, the more accurate the mileage
is. For normal city streets I generally click the "map" style in the upper
right corner, no need really to go to a satellite view. For forest
preserves, I click on "hybrid" in the upper right corner which gives a
satellite view plus the street names. How much ya can see with that view
depends on how clear the satellite got the image and what time of year it
was. If there's a lot of foliage, then it's often hard to see smaller
trails and paths. But it's amazing how much can be seen at some preserves.
There was one letterbox I found last year where I could even see the
"landmarks" in the clues on the satellite map that's how clear the satellite
view was close-up.

I do 5ks and 10ks -- workdays are for racewalk training and Sunday
afternoons and days off are for fast hiking walks -- no moseying on those
walks :-). Every January I change my "routes" for the new year and enter
the new routes at the USATF.com site so that I've got my distances figured
out right off the bat. If you search under Darien IL, you'll see my 2
Waterfall Glen walks and under Glen Ellyn IL is my Churchill Woods walk.
The Waterfall Glen 3k is deceiving because right after the 1 mile mark comes
a rather nasty incline, especially since the path is gravel and not that
easy on footing for this old lady. I know I'm starting to be in good shape
for the season when I can do that loop twice in a row and go 6k without
feeling this overwhelming urge to check to see if I can get enough cell
phone reception to call 9-1-1 if needed. :-)

This year I want to try planting a letterbox using the USATF site for the
clues -- except for the very end location of the box. This way, anyone who
goes looking for it will know exactly how far their muscles are walking
since it's probably going to be a long hike. Tho flat. No serious
elevation rises/falls in that area, since the mounds have been badly
excavated by thieves in decades past from what I understand.

But right now, with 1 1/2 more weeks to go with this cast, all I can do is
map my routes. Now watch -- as soon as my cast is off, we'll get more snow
than Denver. Everybody in the Chicago area can plan on either 10 feet of
snow or 40 below zero on or shortly after the 17th.
