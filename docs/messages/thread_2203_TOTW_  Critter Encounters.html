On one of my first letterboxing adventures, I was hiking alone to the
top of a mountain in New Jersey when I met up with three hikers
coming quickly down the trail. They told me that they had just seen a
bear off the trail. We discussed the sighting, and since the bear was
a hundred yards or so off the trail, I decided to continue up the
mountain, on full-alert, singing and making all sorts of noise so
that the bear wouldn't be surprised when I got into his neighborhood.
Of course, I was walking quite briskly up the slope, jumping every
time a chipmunk dove into the leaves as I passed by, and finally I
had to kneel down on a rock by the side of the trail and take a
breather. Then I noticed something on the other side of the path --
it was a rattlesnake making his way down the trail! I froze in place
until he got well past me, and then continued on my way up the
mountain. I was still hiking at a good pace, and then I encountered a
fat black rat snake also coming down the trail right where I was
about to step. Again, I froze in place, thinking hard about whether
or not my snake identification skills were up to snuff. I continued
up the mountain once again, and as I neared the summit, a large
commotion in the woods off to my left got my attention -- it was a
flock of wild turkeys scrambling at my appearance. Finally, I arrived
at the summit, only to find a large buck waiting for me.

I figured that I had wanted to get out and enjoy nature, so I
certainly got my fill on that hike!

SD

This is reiteration of a message I shared with Doodle n Deedle quite
awhile back.
This is the story of my very first letterbox
placement. Some background I do NOT like snakes.
Hmmm, let me repeat, I DO NOT care at all for the very
IDEA of snakes. It is deeply within me to avoid the
presence of the slithery creatures.
So I'm off to plant my first letterbox oh, about
three and a half years ago (but I remember it as though it were
yesterday and Artistamp will agree that the story has
not changed over the years).
I found the PERFECT place. A short sidepath, down to
the water's edge, a fallen tree with a large open,
decayed knothole. Yes, I thought, this is IT. So I
reached in to slightly enlarge the hole and then
NEARLY TOUCHING my hand, out crawled a snake. It must
have been six feet long!!.okay maybe six inchesbut
it scared the heck out of me. I ran away like a baby.
But then I thought, holdit, it WAS the perfect place
and the snake was probably just on his way somewhere
else to bask in the sunshine. So (what was I
thinking?!) I went back. I looked into the hole and,
lo and behold, no snake. Cool. So I gently settled the
box into its new home, AND THE SNAKE CAME BACK OUT!!
I may be a bit slow on the uptake, but that settled it
for me. I don't even walk down that little path
anymore and my first box was put elsewhere.

I also had an interesting encounter with a deer while placing a box.
That's another story.

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2006-01-24 05:21:37 UTC-05:00
Wow, great topic! A couple of years back I had a little run in with a mink down near Lake Michigan. There are several places along the lake where piled large rock boulders have been used to create jetties. I was walking out on this pier to take a photo for a box I had planted in the area and along comes this little mink. These things are beautiful, but can be vicious, and there he stood, between me and the shore, watching every move that I made, looking as though he had no intentions of letting me go back the way I had come. Apparently he lived somewhere down in the rocks and he was either not too happy, or a little too curious as I passed by. Now let me say that I am not the only person who walks this way -- their is clearly a path worn from fishermen who head out to the end of the pier every day. The difference is, they head out to the end, they do not stop halfway, I imagine very near the vicinity of the mink's abode.

I am not a complete baby when it comes to animals (well, okay snakes), but having never had a run-in with a mink before and having few escape options that didn't include jumping into Lake Michigan fully dressed with camera in hand, panic was not far from mind. But I composed myself and took my photos, thinking if I just ignored him, he would go away. No, he actually inched closer. At this point, I decided that since these fishermen do this every day, the best bet was to walk further out on the pier (away from the territory protected by the mink). He followed me a few steps and then retreated back down into the rocks.

Meanwhile, I am out at the end of the pier in Lake Michigan, backpack, camera... no fishing pole. I looked sorely out of place and felt somewhat foolish for having let a stupid little animal chase me out to the end of the jetty. You can bet I was shaking as I walked back to shore... okay, I cheated. I waited until a group of fishermen headed back and I followed them.

But not one to miss a letterboxing opportunity, the story of the mink has now been preserved in a letterbox clue -- the Mystery Mink.

SpringChick

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2006-01-24 05:35:39 UTC-05:00
Oh, reading these posts about snakes, I just remembered another one... this is an excerpt from a post I made after returning home from Florida last January.

I was down in FL for a few days, in the panhandle, Panama
City Beach Area. Before I went I checked for boxes because
I knew I would have a day to explore and do a little boxing.
There were 6 boxes in the surrounding counties, 4 of which
indicated they had been washed away by the hurricanes and
not yet replaced. I headed down with clues to the remaining
2 boxes.

On Monday, I headed out in shorts and Birkenstocks (not
my usual letterboxing garb, but hey, it was sunny and 77
degrees there -- a record high for this time of year -- it was
freezing rain and very cold where I had come from).



Off to box #2... This one was inside a ritzy, glitzy resort.
I tell the guard I am there to visit the Village and he gives
me a pass, no questions asked. I drive around the golf
course and into the Village, parking near the footbridge to
this tropical-paradisey island. Very cool. I follow the clues
around the island and eventually come out at the bay... turn
left and there ahead is the swing I am looking for. Oops,
there laying in the sand under the swing is pair of sleeping
bodies, half intertwined with one another, half intertwined
with a sleeping bag, half naked -- and sleeping, or at least
pretending to be sleeping.



After about an hour and a half (yes I waited this long for a
silly letterbox -- good thing I had a book in my pack), they
arose, dressed, wadded up the sleeping bag and left. I
made my way back to the swing... there was the pine tree
and the little bush thingie next to it. Supposedly the box
should be under this bush/tree thing in some native grass
and pine cones. Don't see any pine cones, just a lot of
poison ivy and some briars. Carefully stepping over the
briars and around the poison ivy (remember... shorts and
sandals), I looked under every nearby tree. Then kind of
behind one of them, I see a pile of grass and a couple of
pine cones... Hmm, maybe the box lies within. So, I begin
clearing away the grass and what do I find hiding there?
One very large copper-colored snake who did not appear
to be happy I had upset his world. Back through the briars
and poison ivy I went, this time with less care than on the
way in... and I'll be, but that darn snake followed me! So,
up onto the swing goes me, my backpack and my scraped
legs. Once I decided he wasn't coming any closer, I left.
No box. I did however bring home a souvenir -- little red
poison ivy bumps all over my feet and ankles.

SpringChick
