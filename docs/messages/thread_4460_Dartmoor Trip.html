From: Silent Doug (silentdoug@letterboxing.info) | Date: 2007-09-26 16:26:56 UTC-04:00
At 02:41 PM 9/26/2007, you wrote:

>Has anyone out there been to Dartmoor. Do you have any tips/trick on
>planning a trip, when is the best time to go, what to bring, how to get
>there etc. Any advice would be helpful. I want to plan a trip there
>but it's a bit intimidating.

Just back from our second trip, so here's my take:

It's rainy all year round on Dartmoor (except
when it's snowy), so you'll need solid rain gear
from head to toe to fingertips, and will probably
want to avoid the winter season altogether. The
wind atop the tors can be brutal -- I have a
photo atop Brat Tor of Clueless's long tresses
sticking literally straight up and out -- so make
sure you're prepared for high winds, as well, or
you'll likely loose your hat. The weather changes
rapidly and a sunny day can turn into pea soup
with alarming speed, so dress in layers and
always be prepared for any kind of weather. An
Ordnance Survey OL-28 map is essential, along
with a compass (and the knowledge of how to use
it). Almost all the UK letterboxers we went out
hiking with had GPS units, since once the mist
rolls in, a map and compass isn't going to do you
much good. If you step into a bog, it could
easily reach past your knees, so waterproof
boots, gaiters, and rain pants should be worn
much of the time. A trekking pole will help you
poke under rocks, test for boggy ground, find
"plugged" boxes, and (potentially) alert you to
any adders hiding in the rocks. Leave any
aversion to scat at home, since sheep, cattle,
and ponies roam freely on the moors and leave
evidence behind in quantities that you might have
thought improbable. Gloves are good for turning
over rocks or for reaching into gorse; the thorns
on the gorse bushes are nasty and can tear
through heavy nylon. There's no real bus or train
service that will get you anywhere near most of
Dartmoor, so you'll have to fly into London, hire
a car, and drive 3-4 hours to Dartmoor (or,
alternatively, do the same in Bristol, which is
about 2 hours from the southern end of the
moors). Brush up on the UK driving regulations
and signage schemes and be sure to specifically
reserve an automatic transmission car, since the
standard is standard and driving a stick while
shifting with your left hand and sitting in the
right side of the car and keeping on the left
side of the road might be a challenge
(apparently, I had enough trouble staying on the
correct side of the road while driving an
automatic transmission minivan). Pack lightly,
too, since cars are much smaller in the UK and
two large suitcases might not fit in the boot of
an English auto. Give up now on any chance of
visiting on a budget -- the pound to dollar
exchange rate means that everything is about
twice as much as it would be in the states after
you convert from pounds sterling to dollars
American. Gas is presently about 0.97/litre, and
even though vehicles may get 40 mpg, fuel is
still pricey, which you'll notice when you fill
up your tank for 55 and convert that amount to
dollars. A pub lunch might typically be 8.95 --
before you add on the cost of a pint -- so
dropping more than $20 on lunch per person isn't
uncommon. You won't find any American-style
motels (thank God) -- accommodations are
typically English country hotels or inns (similar
to what we would call "bed and breakfast") and
generally include breakfast. Self-catering
cottages are also available, usually for rent by
the week only. With a self-catering option, you'd
do your own grocery shopping and cooking in a
room or cottage with an equipped kitchen. There
are some larger towns just outside the boundary
of the Dartmoor National Park (which, unlike
national parks in the US, is largely
privately-owned), and most of the villages inside
Dartmoor have lodgings of one kind or another.
Princetown/Two Bridges is pretty close to the
center of Dartmoor; Plymouth is the closest city
and is to the southwest of Dartmoor. Okehampton
is a large town and a good base for exploring the
north of Dartmoor; while Ivybridge is the same at
the southern end and Tavistock on the western
end. Dartmoor is about 368 square miles, so you
can get from one end to the other without too
much trouble, though it may take time since the
roads inside Dartmoor are almost all 2-lanes
(except when they're just 1 lane). ATMs are
common and US credit cards are accepted with no
trouble at most establishments, but it's always
good to check first since some establishments do accept only cash.

As far as letterboxing goes, you could buy a copy
of the catalogue and use your siting compass and
map to triangulate the location of the
letterboxes contained therein, or you could
simply scavenge. Every Dartmoor letterboxer will
give you a different answer about which tors are
best on which to scavenge, and sometimes they've
been known to lead newbies astray, so pick a
popular one and start hiking up the track and
look under every rock on the way up and see what
you find. Letterboxes are commonly hidden in pill
pots, and are either commercially-made or
store-bought (with the rare hand-cut stamp). If
you can get your hands on a charity walk
cluesheet, you might have better luck that way,
but you'll have to come up with some way to remit
the 5 or so to the charity sponsoring the walk
in order to get the clues. Letterboxes are
*never* hidden in or near any stone wall, ancient
ruin, or remains of a structure, so check the OS
map first to be sure that the pile of stones that
you're picking through isn't of archaeological
importance. Letterboxers are not typically hidden
in the actual tors, though this rule is not
observed so thoroughly in practice. The northern
end of the moor is used as an artillery range by
the army, so check the firing schedule in advance
before planning a trip to the north. The
boundaries of the firing range are marked on the
OS map, and red flags are flown on the perimeter
on days when firing is underway. Certainly don't
touch any mortars that you might come across in
this area; they've been known to sink in the bogs
and then resurface later after several
freezing/thawing cycles, and some are found that
still have live rounds. Dartmoor letterboxers are
exceptionally secretive and don't believe in
letting anyone know what they're doing. They
won't approach you -- and in fact will most
likely avoid you -- and they believe that all
letterboxers should adhere to the code of
secrecy. Box thievery is rampant and a concern to
many, and many remember that Dartmoor
letterboxing was banned at one time, so there is
a tradition of trying to keep the activity "under
the radar." In any event, I'd suggest that you
don't obsess with the letterboxing to the point
where you miss enjoying the beauty of the moors
and tors. Humans have been on Dartmoor for 8,000
years in rugged and inspiring terrain, and there
is history everywhere you turn, from the leats
and clapper bridges, to the roundhouses and
blowing houses, to the stone crosses and boundary
stones. You'll make the most of your trip if you
take the time to appreciate the timelessness of Dartmoor.

SD
