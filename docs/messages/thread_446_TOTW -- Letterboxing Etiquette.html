TOTW -- Letterboxing Etiquette

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-07-26 16:43:33 UTC
We have all heard a hundred times that there are "no rules" in
letterboxing (perhaps that is part of the intrigue?). However, most
of us have also discovered that it may be more accurate to
say "there are no written, hard-and-fast rules," as there are plenty
of unwritten "guidelines" that are for the most part practiced and
expected by other letterboxers.

Since the TIME magazine story last week (and upcoming CNN piece?)
will no doubt bring an increase in newcomers and participation, and
since there have been a few posts of late from boxers frustrated by
boxes not rehidden well or HH not moved along, etc., I thought a
good TOTW would be letterboxing etiquette... the unwritten rules
that have become widely accepted.

Very helpful for newcomers and "oldies" alike.

SpringChick


Re: TOTW -- Letterboxing Etiquette

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-07-26 23:11:33 UTC
Okay, so I will get the ball rolling on this one... A few things
that immediately come to mind:

- Replacing and rehiding boxes better than when you found them and
treating each box you visit as if it were your own. 'Nuff said.

- It is considerate to check for other boxes in the area/park
before posting clues to your box to be certain your clues do not
give away part of somebody else's mystery clues and your box is not
located directly on top of the other one.

- It is a good idea to search the LbNA clue site and the HH list
for duplicate box names before naming a box... It is not such a
big deal with regular boxes that have the same name, especially if
they are in different areas, but it does get very confusing when HH
have the same name, as people tend to report that they found
HH, not even realizing there are more than one.

- Letterboxing and eating -- critters are very sensitive to the
slightest smell of food, and food oils from your hands transfer very
easily to letterbox contents, making the box a target for a hungry
critter after you leave.

- The underlying philosophy of HitchHikers and Cuckoo Clues is to
move them on as quickly as possible. If you find one and do not
think you will be boxing for a while, it is okay to take a stamp
image, count the find and leave the HH for the next person rather
than keeping it at the bottom of your pack for 6 months.

- It is important to be environmentally conscious both when hunting
AND planting letterboxes... I recently visited a box that was about
8 feet up the side of a very steep embankment in the woods. There
were many small plants growing between the trail and the hiding spot
and there was no way to scale the side of the hill to reach the box
without considerable backsliding and uprooting of plants. This also
is true of some parks that may be habitat for spring wildflowers and
show no sign of them in other seasons. This is why it is important
to check on your boxes periodically to assess the impact to the area
in different conditions and seasons.

SpringChick

From: debrocks54 (deb.maertens@stpaul.com) | Date: 2004-07-27 17:42:51 UTC
It's always a good idea to take a look around before you take your
final steps to a box's resting place. If there are others around, I
like to make up some likely excuse as to why I'm rustling around in
the bushes, such as Jo and Tuck have run off into the bushes and I
need to go after them...

One thing I think is really a nice thing to do is not something done
while letterboxing itself but, when posting on the letterboxing
groups. I find this especially important on the national group but,
as we add more and more members to this group, I think it's a nice
idea to get in the habit of when posting something about a specific
letterbox (especially new ones or box reports) to include the two
digit state abbreviation for the box's location, in the subject line
of the posting.

What this does for me is; I'm from Minnesota, if I see something with
MN in the subject line, I might be more likely to open it than if I
see something with MI. I do hope to eventualy get to MI to hunt down
boxes there and I'd probably go back to some of those postings at
that time. If I see something with IL in the subject line, I make
note of it for when I'm visiting my brother in Chicago. You get the
idea...

By the way, I hardly ever take the time to read the postings on the
national site any more (too much about what's going on, on the
coasts, and not enough about what's happening in the Midwest/Great
Lakes area). I'm so glad SpringChick decided to start this group!

DebRocks
