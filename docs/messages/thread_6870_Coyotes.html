Coyotes

From: Beetle (cjosada@hotmail.com) | Date: 2011-01-08 20:32:55 UTC
More than once, I have come in contact with coyotes while searching in urban wooded areas for letterboxes. Here is some information I received about what to do if you and your dog are approached by a coyote.

Lake County Health Department Senior Biologist Michael Adam said: "From a distance, try to scare the coyote away by being loud and throwing something or spraying water if you've got it. Coyotes will normally avoid a confrontation but they are unpredictable and quick in their movements. Always give these animals an avenue for escape; don't corner the animal. Do not run. That will trigger an instinctive attack. Simply back away without turning your back on the animal. Do all you can to look bigger and make noise. Don't hide or crouch down."

Safe and Happy Letterboxing.

This is a good reminder, Beetle. I helped my brother move to Las Vegas at the end of August and we went letterboxing in a newer, man-made park near the airport. At first I thought a small child was terribly injured, but we quickly realized it was a pack of coyotes chasing another coyote with dinner. Luckily we could see them from above. We scampered back to the car and left. I didn't even go back to attempt finding "Chef" a second time. I think how apropos that letterbox title was.

Trail Rider - the coyotes moved into the Chicago area on their own, they
were not introduced by humans. But after they moved in, Cook County started
a program to track their movements, so each coyote is tagged and returned to
where it was caught. Their movements can then be tracked. If you want more
information, check out the website at http://urbancoyoteresearch.com/

Apparently the coyotes do a very effective job of keeping rodent populations
low and mostly avoid humans and their pets.

From: Sara Byron (sarabyron@att.net) | Date: 2011-01-10 14:49:08 UTC-06:00
I live in Hinsdale, IL - I's just south of Oak Brook, west of Chicago. My
next door neighbor is an elderly (90's) woman with macular degeneraion. She
has opted to let her backyard go wild and overgrown. Last time my husband
ventured over there to trim some of her bushes that have grown up through
our fence, he discovered a den of coyotes with three littl baby coyotes.
Since then, we've heard pack skirmishes and there have been coyote sitings
all around the area. we have a small 8 pound dog and I NEVER let her out
without a human - even with a solid 8 foot privacy fence around the yard.
While they are not a serious danger to humans, the little dogs in the
neighbor hood are as risk. But they must be getting enough food somewhere,
becasue we still have rabbits and squirrels and other critters in the
neighborhood.
Local officials take no stand on coyote packs, other than we have to give
them a wide berth and be careful. Even if we trap and relocate them, we've
been told that they will return. Evidently, they have been known to return
from being released 100 miles away.
One neighbor tried to take up a collecion to have a bow-hunter come in and
kill all of them, but we've been told that anoher pack will jsut re-take
over the territory.
That said, we live very near Graue Mill, and Bemis Woods. If you're
letterboxing over there, be aware that there are tons of coyoes over there
too.
JeffersClan
