We have placed one indoor box. I wrote down a little description of
letterboxing, both to help me explain it and also to leave with the
owners so they could look it over. When we went in to talk to one of
the owners, we brought our logbooks in to show them some of the
stamps. I also pointed a few stamps from indoor locations/businesses
(thanks FungusWoman! for those boxes). They OK'd it and they put the
letterbox behind the counter--people have to give a password to get the
box. We were just in that store last week, and the owner said she
really likes having the letterbox there--she enjoys hearing where
people are from and what other letterboxes they have been at nearby.

I too have placed a letterbox indoors, in a business. When I asked permission of the business owner, and explained a little about letterboxing, he was intrigued and immediately grasped the fact that it would bring some new customers into his shop. The two of us discussed possible hiding places in the shop, but ended up having the box be kept behind the counter. My clues tell the seeker what to ask for.

I have received a couple emails from successful finders, telling me they liked the idea and had bought some of the shop's goods, and one boxer was even inspired to do the same and plant a box in a friend's business herself. Of course hubby and I had to run right out and find THAT one :)

I have several indoor boxes in local library branches. I did NOT seek
permission before planting them as I was afraid it would be denied. I trusted that
letterboxers would be discreet and rehide them without issue since that is
what we do.

I got an email from one of the library branch managers telling of how the
box had been left out on a table and was brought to the desk (Busted!) but she
thought it was such a neat idea she asked if I could email her the clues so
she could put it back in place. I think the fact that it was based on a
children's book character helped her see it as something that was acceptable to
have in the library, and she no doubt also realized it could bring in additional
patrons. I just got another FOUND email on that box so apparently she did
a good job rehiding it and all is well.

My indoor boxes are in little metal tins (think Altoids) with a business
card magnet attached to the box bottom. I usually hide them under the next to
bottom shelf and always toward the back. I have not been back to check on any
of them as I didn't want to draw attention to them. I put some basics on
letterboxing onto a business card, trimmed the corners and taped it inside the
lid. Then I have another business card that tells about LbNA and Atlas Quest
and shows their logos and it is taped into the bottom of the tin. I also
included some little 1" x 3" slips of paper with the URLs for both sites so
visitors could take them with them. The log books are of course rather limited in
size and the stamps should be of thinner material. I also bend the front
center lip of the box bottom out a bit so the latch holds tighter as they will be
upside down on the shelves. Hope that helps!

We had a blast in Metamora, IN looking for Jabber & JoBob's indoor boxes.
One was in a candy store, so of course we had to sample to treats there! They
were so cute and I loved the great choices of containers too. That is one
thing about the boxes being indoors...it opens up a whole new plethora of
containers that will work! I just love seeing how creative some LBers are when the
boxes don't have to be hidden outside in the wild!

We also found several by dream a dream in libraries and asked her permission
to use the idea for our own before we planted them. Then there was another
great box by Toadfrogs in a coffee shop in a cut out book...very nice! I
have a book and the stamp is even carved to go with the book title, but have yet
to cut out the book or figure out where to plant that one, so must get back
to that. With this cold weather and all that is yet to come indoor boxes are
likely to be the ONLY ones we find for a while!

I have placed a number of indoor boxes. They are in retail establishments (cafe, candy store, stationery store). The management is aware of their existance; in fact you must ask for them with a key word or phrase. For example, at the cafe you ask for a Spinach Lattee (eww!).
