From: bindlestiff_boxer (bindlestiff_boxer@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-10-07 11:41:11 UTC

I agree with SpringChick, what is used for the logbook is a totally
personal decision. I've never make negative assumptions about any
logbook I've come across. Good heavens, unless it's part of the
clues or written in the logbook itself, how could I know what the
story might be behind the reason the placer used that particular
logbook?

I've seen some beautiful hand-crafted books (Keltic Kara has some),
some hand-crafted books with wonderfully unique covers
(SpringChick's Indian Bay box comes to mind), some interesting
twists on logbooks (the box I brought back from the West Coast for
Maiden1976), plenty of those sketch books - some with modified
covers, a good share of other utilitarian style logbooks; including
a fair number of those little lined composition books.

My own boxes have all different kinds of logbooks; sketch pads with
and without altered covers, little hand-crafted books with simple
bindings, strips of paper cut to size and covered then stapled
together, and one is a journal with a coptic stitch suede cover.
Some of my logbooks have a story behind them, which means something
to me and I usually don't share it. For example, one of my former
students, who now lives semi-independantly in an AFC home, gave me a
pack of those little composition books from the dollar store. Enders
Angel and I cut them down to tiny size and used them for the
logbooks in our Bonus Eggs series (which are now running around
loose as HH's)

Lately, I find myself wanting my letterboxes to be smaller than what
one of those sketch books would fit into, so I've been coming up
with quickie logbooks made to size. I love bookmaking/bookbinding,
but I'd never get any boxes planted if I had to wait to endulge in
creating each logbook by hand! lol

Enjoy!
---PJ
aka Bindle Babe


"SpringChick" wrote:
> What you use for the log books in your boxes is a totally a
personal decision -- as simple or elaborate as you want. The most
important consideration is the size of your box, and paper without
lines is nice, but again that is more a preference than a
requirement.
>
> I have seen boxes that use the little 3"x5" spiral pads of basic
lined paper, others that use strips of adding machine tape stapled
together at one end, index cards (blank or lined) -- spiral bound or
held together in a variety of other ways, a wide assortment of
purchased unlined paper pads/books and then handmade journals. I
enjoy bookmaking and can never seem to find the right size book for
my boxes, so that is why I make most of mine, but it is certainly
not necessary. As you can see from the list above, even art quality
paper is not a requirement, although some of the thinner papers tend
to allow ink bleed-through..
>
> A lot of people buy the small sized Strathmore spiral-bound sketch
pads and then recover or embellishment the cover a bit -- adds a
touch of creativity and gives a nice quality paper without having to
start from scratch. A few options:
>
> 400 series 4"x6" spiral-bound pad (brown cover) - 24 sheets/80 lb.
> Mister Art (www.misterart.com) for about 1.50 each
> 400 series recycled 3.5"x5" spiral-bound pad (green cover) - 100
sheets/60 lb. Art City (www.artcity.com) for about 1.80 each
> 300 series 3.5"x5" spiral-bound pad (yellow cover) - 100 sheets/50
lb. Art City (www.artcity.com) for about 1.80 each.

From: Timbertoes (toomey@genevaonline.com) | Date: 2004-10-07 13:17:01 UTC

Here's another option for making logbooks that works for me. A
couple office supply stores near me have pads of paper they make from
printing scraps and sell real cheap. (They're privately-owned shops,
not chains like Office Max or Office Depot - but they might have them
too.) Since both stores are printers along with selling office
supplies, I give them the measurements that I need to fit my box and
they use their big die-cut machine to cut the pads down to size. At
the same time they punch two holes near the binding. I separate the
pads into the page count I want, make a cover and use brads to hold
them together. I can be as creative as I like with the cover or just
use plain cardstock. Since I use one of the printers for my projects
at work, they don't charge me for this service, but I can't imagine
it would cost too much.

Timbertoes
