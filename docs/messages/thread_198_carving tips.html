Oh my...
the mistakes I've made would fill a book, but (hopefully) I've
learned from them.
Okay a couple of tips:
First, I use *strong* magnifying glasses.
Next, I raise the work on a wooden block until it's about 5-6 inches
from my eyes.

Towards the end of the process, I use a light colored ink to test the
stamp. That way I can still see the dark parts of the transferred
image which remain on the block and still need to be removed.
Only when I think it's almost finished do I use black ink to test.

Anxiously awaiting others' contributions...

I used to use dark ink to test the stamp while I was carving. Then,
after carving more away, when I wanted to clean up the stamp I'd wash
the ink off with acetone... until I realized that the acetone
actually dissolves a very thin layer of the stamp material! At
least, it does on the "pink stuff." It's probably only noticeable if
you have very shallow, lightly carved lines in your image, but still
something to be aware of. The use of light colored ink is a much
better idea; I'll have to try that instead.

Also, we ordered a large magnifier glass on an adjustable stand -- I
think we found it on a science/hobby site. There are many different
styles available in a wide range of prices. Ours was under $20.
This can make it much easier to see what you're working on, although
sometimes I prefer working without it.
