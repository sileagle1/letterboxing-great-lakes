Worse than poison ivy??

From: Doodle & Deedle Bug (doodle_n_deedle@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-08-08 11:44:06 UTC-07:00
The Cleveland Plain Dealer had an article today about
Hogsweed, a plant that's showing up in Ohio and is,
per the article, "100 times worse than poison ivy."
Evidently if you touch hogsweed, you can get
"second-degree burns, painful blisters and
long-lasting scars" because "its stalks bear a sap
that allows the sun's ultraviolet rays to severely
burn skin and can even cause temporary blindness if it
gets into the eyes." The link to the full article is
here:
http://www.cleveland.com/ohio/plaindealer/index.ssf?/base/news/112349356298842.xml&coll=2


The link doens't include the nice little graphic where
pretty much every great lake state except for Illinios
is highlighted as having hogsweed in some part of the
state. Just what we need - another letterboxing whoa.
Per the article, it looks a lot like Queen Anne's
Lace. Searching for information on the web, I also
found this link, which seems very helpful for
determining where one might find this plant, as well
as photos:
http://dnr.metrokc.gov/wlr/LANDS/weeds/hogweed.htm

Has anyone heard of this plant before? That article
was the first I heard of it.

Here's to staying happy, healthy, informed
letterboxers!

Doodle Bug.

Re: Worse than poison ivy??

From: (katy@dsa132.org) | Date: 2005-08-08 22:11:58 UTC
My lab has been studying the plant chemicals (furanocoumarins) that cause
this reaction for over twenty years and well, we all got a kick out of
this article. We've all had the photo-dermatitis described and not one
of us would describe it as worse than poison ivy. If you've ever burned
yourself on the toaster oven so that you got a shiny blister - imagine
that without the initial burning sensation. It's mildly painful and you
will have ugly brown scars for a few years, but that's about it. Of
course, just like with poison ivy, some people react more than others, so
that my one labmate can handle the plants without gloves and be fine and
another gets burns from rubbing up against the table in the insectary.
You can also get this from wild parsnip (Pastinaca sativa) and cow
parsnip (Heracleum sphondylium) - both of which are found in Illinois.
It's good for people to be aware of these plants, but I think the paper
over did it a bit.

Katy

PS Our informal (bc we aren't cleared for vertebrate testing and just did
it on ourselves) trials in lab indicate that wearing sunscreen will help
protect you from burns from these plants.
