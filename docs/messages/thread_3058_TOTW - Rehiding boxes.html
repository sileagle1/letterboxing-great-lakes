From: Barb Funk (mmfunk99@greenapple.com) | Date: 2006-09-11 13:39:37 UTC-04:00
Hey Cindi and all,
The very same thing is happening in central and southern Ohio after a
couple of magazine articles about letterboxing were published. I just
walked in my house from taking a hike in a nature preserve where I have
7 boxes placed and every single one of them was left in the open or very
close to it. In our area, we are finding that folks are going out in
large groups (10-18 ppl?!) and the last one is perhaps being left
behind?? and not taking the time to rehide well. We are also seeing
that some of the very best stamps are being stolen. We are seeing
geocachers are taking the stamp (and in my case leaving a quilting
brochure in it's place??) and we are seeing that some parents (not all)
are perhaps letting the kids toss the box back on the ground, not
covering with the original rocks or logs etc. as well.

To answer your question, I personally would like to have someone let me
know....if I didn't do a good job and would not be offended, but I also
know that when I have given some VERY gentle feedback about clues, I
have gotten some mighty strong feedback. I look back at this point and
wonder how well I put boxes back when I first started. What I read
constantly was to be discrete and so I was very worried about that far
more than anything else and so I may have compromised a bit in the
beginning. It wasn't until I carved and put my first box out there that
I began to understand that a box is best if it is sort of anchored down
etc. Some placers ONLY use leaves to cover and I just cringe knowing
there will be problems after I have stamped in and recovered with only
leaves because there were no rocks in the area they chose. This IS
when I have gone back to let them know I found their box, the clues and
stamp were fantastic, but that an animal or bad storm will blow their
box right out into the open. This is where I really learned from others
about better placement choices--when others gave ME feedback.

I have been trying to let new letterboxers in my area know these things
via email and encouraging them to come to an event. This way they may
be able to pick up some of these things including "leave no trace" habits.

I don't know that my comments have solved anything, but I'm hoping to
hear how others are handling this as well! Thanks for letting us know
Cindi!

Trailtracker~Barb

It may be there to hide or in some cases to keep animals from carring it away.
One of my boxes hidden along an old highway behind and tucked under a rock with a historical marker was left on top of the rock in plain view. From: Becky (imdurangogirl@aol.com) | Date: 2006-09-11 21:13:03 UTC
I would want to know If I rehid a box poorly. I have always tried to
go by the guideline of leave the box in as good if not better condition
then it was found. With that in mind when we have found boxes hid only
by leaves we try to find bark, rocks, log, anything at all to hide it
better and look natural. I would say though when contacting someone,
be a diplomatic as possible about it. Boxes out in the open are not
always the fault of the previous placer. I have one box in paticular
that every time I'd check on it, it would be out of the log it was
hidden in. I couldn't figure out what was up, until I started to
notice some teeth marks in the box. I guess the critter that lived in
the log did not like sharing his space.

From: Kathi Krankoski (krank@ameritech.net) | Date: 2006-09-12 22:23:49 UTC-07:00
Because of the way I have my clues posted on LbNA ("contact me to obtain the rest of the clues"), I have been getting a LOT of messages from new boxers. It is interesting to "see" their thought processes, which may give some insight as to what everyone is mentioning about poorly hidden boxes.

It sounds like many of them can't wait to create and hide their first box, often without the experience of finding a good number of boxes first. For example, someone wanted to find one of my indoor "password protected" boxes before placing their first. I warned them that that particular box was not a typical letterbox and would not give their children a good idea of what the sport entailed. Perhaps we are seeing the letterboxing version of the "telephone game", where the examples the new boxers are following are not the best ones, as the hiding techniques have gotten lax over time???

We must all remember that letterboxing is a four-season activity - the fall leaves will be gone come spring, and the spring-hidden landmarks may become overgrown by summer. And snow - I won't even go there...!
