Re: outer bags

From: Tom and Jill (jillnick72@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-04-14 12:59:57 UTC
We've put all our boxes in outer bags, if I'm remembering
correctly. After checking on several of them last weekend, I'm glad
we did :). Many were very wet and dirty on the outside, but the box
inside was completely dry. We had put the box in an outer bag
because we've been concerned with melting snow or heavy rains
getting into the box. Generally our boxes have been ones with a
good seal, but we've noticed that a few boxes hadn't been sealed
tightly when we've checked on them. The outer bag seems to lend one
extra layer of protection, particularly if the cover of the box
isn't resealed well.

That being said, one of our boxes *did* migrate a few yards away
from the hiding spot, most likely by a critter, so I suppose it's a
matter of opinion/personal choice either way.

Jill


Re: outer bags

From: Deb (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-04-14 16:12:34 UTC
Yes, I agree there are pros and cons. Sometimes I use an outer bag,
sometimes I don't -- depends on the area, the hiding spot and the
container. I do think the outer bag gives critters an advantage --
something to latch onto and drag it away, and I have seen several of
my outer bags that were punctured with teeth marks.

So if the container seals tightly and the area is not prone to
wetness I tend to leave off the outer bag in favor of placing all
inner contents inside a large ziploc baggie, in addition to their
smaller individual bags. But I do still use the outer bags in some
places, especially where wetness is a factor and if the container
does not seal well or is too small for an inner large bag.

SpringChick

From: Silent Doug (silentdoug@letterboxing.info) | Date: 2004-04-14 12:29:08 UTC-04:00
Personally, I don't see much value in outer bags. They are usually torn
quite quickly after being pulled out from under a rock or tree stump, and
once they're punctured, all they do is serve as a holding place for
moisture close to the letterbox. I'd rather see a sturdier container used
instead, and I also use the "burp test" to check containers to see how
watertight they actually are. Close the container, and then press firmly on
the lid. If air is expelled, then the container isn't likely to be
watertight and water will eventually work itself into the box. For
instance, the small sandwich sized Rubbermaid containers aren't watertight
at all, and neither are most of the cheaper varieties of plastic containers
you find at dollar stores. Water is a significant enemy of a letterbox,
particularly when they may be exposed to freezing temperatures and snow,
and a torn outer bag won't do any good.

Doug
