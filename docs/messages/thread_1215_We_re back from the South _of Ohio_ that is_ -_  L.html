Sounds like you had a wonderful time all in all. Thanks for the recap! It
made today enjoyable as I look out my window and wonder what I am doing
inside working!

At 11:35 AM 4/25/2005, you wrote:
>For a month we'd planned this trip to the Pike County
>area of Ohio to letterbox. It was going to be April,
>the weather was supposed to be nice, on Wednesday
>weather.com said 60-degree weather. And then, as I
>lamented last week, the forecast changed to cold and
>rain and snow! But we still persevered. For anyone
>who wishes to hear of our adventures, here's the
>recap:
>
>Once we got south of Columbus, the rain let up for the
>most part. It was still drizzely, but the temps
>weren't too bad. Here's a tip we discovered for
>'boxing in the rain. If you forget to bring a sheet
>protector for your clues, a gallon-sized storage
>baggie works beautifully for keeping those clues dry
>and mud-free. I don't think we'll ever use a sheet
>protector again....
>
>We first stopped in Circleville (Pickaway county)
>where we found both the letterboxes hidden in Ted
>Lewis Park. This was around lunchtime on Friday. We
>started searching for the Ted Lewis Park box, and then
>two women showed up to spend their lunch hour right in
>the area we needed to be. So we played on the
>playground equipment for a bit, found Torch of
>Freedom, and then hung out under another picnic
>pavilion until they finally left. The clues for Ted
>Lewis were driving us crazy, though, and we were about
>to give up and headed back to the car when I started
>being nosey and found that here we'd parked right in
>front of the hiding place.
>
>So with our count being F2 and the drizzle pretty much
>having stopped, we headed to AW Marion to look for
>Drew Landmark & Pandamonium. We found Drew, and then
>started out on the path to find Pandamonium. Dragon,
>you're in good company - just like you wrote in the
>Drew logbook, we walked the entire 5 mile path around
>the lake, too. The clues to find Pandamonium say that
>it's an easy find, and it is - if you take the path in
>the right direction! Sadly, this logbook was soaked,
>so we did what we could in the way of First Aid and
>we're going to try to contact the placer to let them
>know.
>
>At least, with walking 5 miles, we wouldn't feel
>guilty about the Elephant Ears we were planning on
>eating at the Pike County Dogwood Festival the next
>day. And at least it hadn't been raining as we hiked
>around the lake.
>
>After AW Marion, we continued south, stopping at the
>Logan Elm Memorial to eat dinner. What a quiet,
>peaceful spot! And, by now the sun was also actually
>shining! We only found 1 of the 3 boxes that had been
>planted at Logan Elm, though (that being Jack).
>Someone had given us a head's up that they couldn't
>find Untie The Ribbon this past fall, and sure enough,
>its hiding place had at least 5 inches of standing
>water. More contacting the placer to do...
>
>We by-passed Chillicothe, saving that for on the way
>home on Sunday. We stopped at Canal Park in Waverly
>to look for Rocky, but couldn't find that one. It was
>almost 7 PM now, and after much sweet talking I
>convinced Deedle that we needed to go to the rest area
>on Route 23 just past the Pike County border to look
>for the 2 boxes hidden there before the sun set around
>8. We went 1 for 2 there, finding Jester but not
>Passenger Pigeon (although it's listed that someone
>found it on April 4th of this year, so hmmmm).
>
>Alas, the sun that had visited us briefly that day
>departed and during the night the thunderstorm came.
>The next day it did rain the whole entire day, but
>we'd travelled so far to search for boxes, we weren't
>going to let cold rain stop us. And most of these
>sounded like they'd be fairly easy finds. To those of
>you who hide boxes close to picnic pavillions, we
>thank you for giving us dry places to spread out and
>stamp in! :) Makes it much easier than trying to hold
>an umbrella...
>
>First stop of the day was a little town called Jasper
>to look for a box hidden around this sad, abandonded
>church on a hill. We climbed the steps to the top,
>looked around, and just as we were ready to begin
>searching, we heard barking. We looked up. The
>property next to the church, which was down a slope
>and a decent distance away, had 2 dogs in the yard.
>As Deedle put it, 2 junkyard dogs that he named Kujo
>and Black Shuck once we were back in the safety of our
>Jeep. They started barking, and then they started
>running towards us. Now, we're from the city, where
>dogs are supposed to be chained up, but this is the
>big, wide open country where their post office was
>literally a trailer. As soon as we saw those dogs
>start running and that they didn't have chains to hold
>them back, we flew back down those stairs, jumped in
>the jeep, and took off. I've created a new stats
>category, M, for Mission Aborted, which we will use
>when our letterboxing efforts were abruptly ended for
>fear of life and limbs. (Hopefully this will have
>been the only time we'll need to use that category.)
>
>To add insult to injury (ok, avoiding an injury from
>dog bites), I checked letterboxing.org just now and
>the box we'd been searching (Morgan's Raid #1) for by
>that church is listed as missing. Was that listed
>when I printed off the clues?? Luckily, our second
>venture for the Gingko box was much more succesful
>(and dog-less). Next stop was Pike Lake State Park,
>where we found Autumn Leaves and were the First
>Finders of Munch, Munch! (Of course, this was after
>our 8-mile round trip side trip up a narrow mountain
>road because the clues as to where we needed to start
>the search for Autumn Leaves were misinterpretted.)
>
>We then drove to Lake White State Park to find Rubber
>Duckie, and ended the day in Beaver, Ohio, where we
>found Morgan's Raid #3 (with another very soaked
>logbook). We then wanted to find Ohio's Most Perfect
>Tree (which also has a letterbox hidden there). Yes,
>our government has declared this tree the Most Perfect
>Tree in the state. Evidently it's so perfect, though,
>that mere mortals cannot find it, or at least, we
>couldn't. Per the clues, it was 3 & 1/2 miles south
>of the Route 335 & Route 32 intersection, on Route
>335. Sounds simple enough, a sign was supposed to
>mark its location. We drove up and down that stretch
>of road twice and although we saw some fairly perfect
>trees to our eyes, we couldn't find THE perfect tree!
>Deedle kept grumbling that there should be an address
>for it, to which I replied, "It's a TREE! Trees don't
>have addresses!" The only thing we could think of was
>that Govenor Taft had the tree cut down and sold for
>firewood to help solve Ohio's budget woes....
>
>When we got back to the hotel, we asked about The Most
>Perfect Tree at the front desk. The woman had no idea
>what we were talking about.
>
>It was now dinner time, and our plan was to eat fair
>food at the Dogwood Festival. Poor festival - it was
>almost as deserted as that old abandoned church. Half
>the booths had packed up and left; of what was still
>there, only a handful of food booths were open. We
>did get our elephant ears, though.
>
>The next day we woke up to snow. At least it wasn't
>really accumlating, though. We started to drive back
>north and stopped in Chillicothe. Of those 50-some
>boxes hidden in the city, thanks to the weather we
>were only able to search for 2. We found Lonely Rock
>and Pooh Trail #1 (both with wet logbooks :( ) before
>Deedle declared that if we searched for any more he
>would get pneumonia.
>
>So thus ended our weekend down "south." Evidently we
>drove into some time machine that took us back to
>February once we reached Cleveland. Ick, ick, ick!!
>
>Hope everyone is keeping warm,
>
>Doodle Bug.
