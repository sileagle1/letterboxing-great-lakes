I don't think everyone realizes that it is actually very easy to make
your own personal stamp at any local stamp shop. Just bring in
whatever image or text you like and they will create a personal stamp
for you for under $10. These are the kind of stamps you are talking
about in Dartmoor.

I wouldn't call these "commercial" stamps, because they are not the
mass-produced stamps you find at the craft store.

If anyone has found any of my boxes, you have seen one of these rubber
stamps created from a drawing. I also love hand-carved stamps, but if
you don't feel like carving one, just bring a black & white image to a
rubber stamp shop to make a unique stamp for yourself or for a
letterbox you are hiding. Once you see how easy it is to make a stamp
from a drawing, you won't feel tempted to use one of those oft-scorned
commercial stamps.

Matt the Rat

Yep, doubtless this might have been what they were referring to as regards
"commercial" stamps. I literally read close to 60 or 70 websites giving the
history of letterboxing, and I'm not going to spend the time looking thru them
all again to find exactly what site I read it on. IIRC, it was a UK site where I
read that commercial stamps had been preferred to hand-made ones -- a
relatively plain site with a font size that my eyes could just barely read.

But what you're talking about is still a commercial stamp in my opinion, and
I can't see any difference between having one of those made and buying an
already-made one that a person really likes at a store. There *is* the artistry
involved I guess in that the person did make their own drawing, but then that's
not even necessary either. There are so many amateur software programs for
working with photos and images done by others to reduce things down to what can
be done to commercially produce a stamp, that it basically can come down to
having a simple printshop type software program that will combine a drawing, a
desired font, and a design. The individual is doing none of the "sculpture"
work that would be required to produce that stamp.

But I think to me it would be more desirable just because, even tho it's
commercial, at least it can be individualized to the person, if a personal stamp,
or it can be made to securely relate to the scene, if it's a stamp going into
a letterbox. So it would follow more the traditional "I was here & I can
prove it" line of thinking.

In addition to Matt's great advice, let me add that there are places
that will provide you with a whole sheet (7"x9") full of your images
*in rubber* which leaves you to just cut them apart and mount them.
They're just like store-bought, only it's your image.
This is just an option if carving is not your deal.
You can still design your signature stamp or box images and send them
off for reproduction.
We have used these sources for 'art stamps' but not for letterbox
stamps, so far.
Although as Matt can probably attest, replacing a commercially
produced stamp for a missing box would be a whole lot easier than
recarving an image.
Here's a link.

http://www.polyclay.com/ready.htm
