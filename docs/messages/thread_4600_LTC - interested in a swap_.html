From: baliddle (baliddle@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2007-12-02 01:23:51 UTC-05:00
Hi there!
I was wondering if anyone has seen or read about the latest "thing"
called LTCs or Letterbox Trading Cards. It's a derivative of Artist
Trading Cards with the main difference being that at least one
component of the cards has to be a hand carved stamp image. The
cards must be 3.5x2.5 inches. Those are about the only
requirements. From there, you can decorate, embellish, art up or do
whatever you want to the cards. You make a bunch and trade other
people for their cards. I've posted a bunch of web sites below with
more information.

Anyway, my point is, I was wondering if there's enough interest here
to do a Great Lakes regional LTC swap. I was thinking we could have
the theme be "My home state/town". You'd make a series of cards, all
relatively the same, that represents your state or town in some
way. You'd have to carve a stamp to match this theme and design your
cards around that theme.

You can get 10 cards from one piece of 8.5 x 11 cardstock (see
mirkwood designs link below). So, I was thinking we could do either
1 or 2 groups of 10 participants or 1 large group of 20, if there's
enough interest. It'd be awesome if we could get at least one person
from each state in the region.

I'd be willing to host this. I think we could have the due date be
the end of January. That should give everyone plenty of time to do the cards.

What do you think? If you want to participate, would you rather do
sets of 10 or 20?

BNL Bali Grl

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-12-02 09:08:00 UTC-05:00
Bali, I would be interested in participating in the ATC swap. Sounds like fun! I am open to sets of 10 or 20. My preference would be the larger group because you get more in return, and to me the work is in the concept and carving the stamp -- once you start assembling them, the quantity is not such a big deal. But if a large number is a hindrance to some people, the smaller groups are okay too.

We did a variation of this a few years ago on another letterboxing-related group using our signature stamps as the basis for a design on ATC's. I think it might have been an idea before its time as not many people were familiar with ATC's at the time and there were only about 8-10 people who participated. It was fun though -- you can see my contribution here: http://www.confessionsofdecember.com/art_atcs.asp (the one on the right).

The only thing I would caution though, and I know you didn't make up the name -- what exactly is it that makes these "letterbox" trading cards? What do they have to do with letterboxing? I once played around with using an ATC format as the basis of a hand-delivered letterbox clue, which I would think could have been considered a letterbox trading card since it was about a letterbox, but I ended up going with another format that allowed more room for the clues. But I don't really see the connection between letterboxing and ATC's just because they include a hand-carved image. Rubber stamp carving, while prolific among letterboxers, is an art form that has been around independent of letterboxing long before the first box was ever placed in the U.S. It is not really about letterboxing at all. I understand the name as a derivitive from one of the things we do in this hobby, but it is really no more about letterboxing than a Saturday afternoon hike without a box (would anyone call that a letterbox hike then?) and it is bound to cause dissention among some of the purists just as the term "postal letterboxing" did. But that is all semantics and life is too short to diddle over the details... especially when there are stamps to be carved ;-)

On the other hand, it does get the wheels turning as to several possibilities for a true letterbox trading card -- using the ATC format as means to distribute/trade WOM type clues... perhaps hand-carved images that are in themselves clues and used as the basis of a trading card design... oh the possibilities there!

As an FYI, if there are those interested in more swaps like this involving hand-carved art, but not necessarily pertaining to letterboxing, you might be interested in checking out either the Carving Consortium (http://groups.yahoo.com/group/CarvingConsortium/) or the Eraser Cuts (http://groups.yahoo.com/group/erasercuts/) groups. There are usually several different swap projects going on there (ATC's, postcards, etc.) involving hand-carved images.

SpringChick

At 09:08 AM 12/2/2007, you wrote:
>The only thing I would caution though, and I know you didn't make up
>the name -- what exactly is it that makes these "letterbox" trading
>cards? What do they have to do with letterboxing?

Not much, really. You're right. I'm new to the party on this. But,
reading through to the old posts on AQ, it seems like someone asked
if anyone was interested in trading ATC's. Then, someone suggested
they have hand carved images. Then someone coined the phrase. I
think they decided on that because it's sort of a combo of ATC's and
postal letterboxing. (which as you noted, in itself is a whole other
debate about whether it's letterboxing). I think they thought this
was a good way to trade images without the expense of sending logs
and stamps through the mail. They just called them LTCs because they
wanted to add the requirement that one component of the card be a
hand carved image.

I've never done a postal ring, but this idea really intrigues me
because it combines the carving aspect of letterboxing with my other
love of paper crafting. Maybe a better term would be "Letterboxers'
Trading Cards", since they're created by letterboxers.

Anyway, thanks for your interest and other information about similar
swapping. I'll add you to the list and keep you posted. Right now,
I have 5 people and 3 states. I'd really like to have all the states
in our region represented, if possible. (need Ohio, Indiana, Minnesota)

If you know any letterboxers that may be interested in this, but
don't read the message boards, please feel free to pass on the info!

Thanks,
Bali

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-12-02 14:34:16 UTC-05:00
Yes... what a fun twist this could be indeed! I like the idea of trading them to complete a series or even trading them to get all of the necessary clues for a particular box. I am wondering though if people would be willing to let them go, even once they have the info they need from them? Not only would they serve as clues to ultra-rare letterboxes, but the cards would be collector's pieces themselves -- like being sent a WOM clue from a boxer for whom you have a lot of respect that is so far from you that you may never get there, but still it is a great thing to add to your letterboxing memorabilia collection. But I suppose if trading were the way to get others -- clues to boxes you don't yet have, then it would be a worthwhile exchange. Remember the old card game, Pit, where you traded commodities to get a run on one particular suite? Sometimes it was hard to let go of a couple of Rye, but necessary to get a full set of Wheat.

I think this is definitely worth some more thought... It would probably take a while for it to catch on, but once there were a fair number of people playing, it would be great fun!

In the meanwhile, the hand-carved ATC cards will be a wonderful winter diversion.

SpringChick

I agree with you, SC, this isn't really "letterboxing". Nor would I say
it was "invented" by Mama Cache as AQ says. Several years before I
started letterboxing, I was involved in ATC's, and there were those who
carved their own stamps to create the cards. Whatever. Like you say,
semantics. Bring on the PZKut and let's carve more stamps.

Which brings me to another permutation of the idea. Back in those days,
another mail art group I belonged to did "one-to-one" exchanges, AKA
"1-2-1"s. We had a database of those interested in participating and
you'd just mail one to someone on the list and they'd mail one back to
you. No particular theme (usually, though seasonal themes often
emerged). No time restraints on the return. No large expenses (though
postage was cheaper in those days!). (A way around the expense could be
to outline the 2.5x3.5" size on a postcard sized piece of cardstock,
create the ATC within the outline, and mail at the reduced postcard rate
(assuming no ephemera or add-ons that could be lost to the USPS
machinery). The recipient could then cut it to size.)

Too many fun ideas, too little time!! (-:

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-12-02 19:49:52 UTC-05:00
I read the AQ wiki and have a better understanding of why they are called Letterbox Trading Cards -- because the way it sounds, they started out using sig stamps, although that may have expanded to just hand-carved stamps in general. I would agree about the reference to it being "invented" by Mama Cache. It is definitely not a new idea -- it is exactly the same as we did back in 2003 at the IALC (International Artgirls Letterboxing Cabal) swap... The idea of trading ATC's with our letterboxing signature stamps on them was originated by the IALC group in general and the first swap was hosted by Kimberly from OH. I guess because there was no AQ back then and we didn't write up official instructions or officially name them (we just considered them ATC's with a theme of hand-carved letterboxing signature stamps, which actually they are), it doesn't count as the official inception of the idea.

Oh well, it's nice to see people extending their carving skills for some other artsy and fun kinds of things.

SpringChick
