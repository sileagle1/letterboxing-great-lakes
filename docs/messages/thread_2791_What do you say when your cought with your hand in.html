I enjoy birdwatching (guess that explains my trail name) so I often
have my binoculars when we are out boxing. If someone seems to be
looking at us as we are searching, I conspicuosly pull out my
binoculars to provide a distraction while the others are searching
for the box.

pretend that they are using their journals to "take notes" about

Oh yes, the Letterboxing Murphy's Law. As Deedle
says, you can be letterboxing on Mount Everest and as
soon as you find the box and spread all your supplies
out, along will come someone walking their dog.
Luckily we've never actually had anyone ask us what we
were doing. Usually, if we're hunting for a box where
we know there are other people lurking around, we'll
prepare a cover story in advance that's apropos to the
situation. IE, pull the camera out, if someone gets
too close start taking lots of photos. I forget how
many photos we have of me posing in front of the tree
or sitting on the ledge where the box is hidden as
people walked by us.

If we're in a state where there was no one, no one, no
one around and they suddenly materialized from thin
air and we've got all our stuff spread around us, we
gather it up into our bag as quickly as we can and
either pass around the water bottle, act winded like
we need to rest from the hiking, pretend we're
searching for something in our pack, stoop to tie our
shoes, pretend I'm searching for my contact, pretend
we've stopped for a snack (how do they know there
isn't really food in that tupperware container, right?
:). So far no one's questioned us...

Doodle Bug.

We have become great Thespians in the woods:

We've used the camera decoy because we really do take photos of
fungi!

We've used the birdwatching decoy...with our secret call-out to
alert the other of an "intruder".."I think I hear a
Woodpecker!"..Although, this usually doesn't work well at a beach
where woodpeckers are scarce!

And like Doodle & Deedle, I've actually acted like I was chewing a
stamp as if it was a cookie just pulled from a ziplock baggie as
someone passed by! I think that if that occasion had been captured
on film an Oscar would have been mine for the over-the-top
performance!

But, the best, was recently when there was an elderly woman acually
sitting on the tree where the box was located. We simply told her
that we were letterboxing (like a scavenger hunt) and if she didn't
mind moving we'd fetch the box and be on our way. We explained all
about letterboxing to her as we went through the process. She was
keenly interested and then asked if she could continue the hike out
with us and hitch a ride into town! Our first human hitchiker! It
ended up her sister met up with her and we didn't actually have to
take her to the next box! Sometimes with a little discernment you
can win new letterboxers right out on the trail!
Tiptoe & Tonto

From: Gooble Gobble (gooble_gobble_3@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-07-20 13:52:06 UTC-07:00
I've never been "caught in the act" by anyone, but if
someone asked me I wouldn't lie to them. I'd tell them
exactly what I was doing and even show them some of my
stamps. Lying is just plain wrong. I wouldn't however
let them watch me re-hide the box, of find it, but
once everything is out in the open and I'm ready to
stamp in it seems childish to try pretend you're doing
something else. What is everyone afraid of ? No one is
going to call the cops on you for writing in a logbook
and stamping. If the person you've shared the
aforementioned info with decides to take the box or
works for the park service or something, OH WELL.
That's part of the risk of hiding a box, isn't it ? It
might last 2 weeks, it might last 10 years, who knows.


I'm not trying to pass judgement on any of you so
please don't take this post the wrong way, but
seriously, we're all adults here. DON'T LIE, ABOUT
ANYTHING! What if your children are with ? Would you
lie then ? What would that teach them ?


Gooble Gobble
