From: Armadillo Jo (jes7o@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-09-20 05:41:22 UTC-07:00
Great topic, as I was just going to mention something
about this after a letterboxing experiencing I had
yesterday. One should not hide boxes in a place where
someone could get seriously injured trying to obtain
the box. I'm not talking about up a mountain or
anything, as most people who go up a mountain do so at
their own risk. I'm not even talking about sand dunes,
as everyone knows how fond I am of those. The scenario
i'm talking about involves hiking on a flat, paved
trail. Okay, so you get the clue and head out on the
paved trail. You eventually find the spot where the
box is, the clue is very specific....and it apparently
is on the underside of a bridge support. Now, there is
no way to get to the spot to even have a look without
crossing the safety rail and sliding down a very short
and steep embankment of gravel. One false move, and
you're heading about 15 feet down into a creek. You
try to see if the box *might* be hidden as such where
you can get it from the main trail. Nope. This is a
bad hiding spot, you declare! I tried to get down the
embankment (foolishly) to have a look, but gave
up....again...I would rather live than have box in
hand and be dead. Placers should keep in mind the
general safety of the finders. If your box is placed
in a rugged area (mountain, dune, cliff) fine, warn
the finder in the clue. As long as the powers that be
say you can be there, it's game. However, don't make a
find unnecessarily difficult by placing it somewhere
that humans should not be in the first place. A box
should never be on the other side of safety rails,
guard rails, or anywhere already sectioned off by
rangers due to safety concerns.

--Armadillo Jo

From: Timbertoes (toomey@genevaonline.com) | Date: 2004-09-20 19:41:50 UTC
My favorite hiding places are under rocks. There is a low risk of
passers-by finding a box hidden under one (unless there is a well-
worn social trail to it) and it seems really secretive and safe. One
of the other things I look for when planting a box of my own and
appreciate when finding someone else's box is that there's a good
place to stamp in not too far from the hiding place. I like to be
able to take my time about stamping in and looking over the previous
visitors' messages and there's nothing worse than feeling rushed
because you're afraid of being seen or are getting eaten alive by
mosquitoes. Also high on the list are deep notches in trees.

Re: TOTW - Good/Bad Hiding Spots

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-09-21 15:44:23 UTC
Good hiding places -- In the woods, not on main trails, not off trail
in areas where dense undergrowth would facilitate a social trail.
Interesting historical sites are great, but not right at the place,
rather off on a side trail or at the edge of the property.

Bad hiding places -- Very busy public areas are a bad idea both for
the longevity of the box and also for potential finders. I once
found a box located at a very busy public place and had to wait over
2 hours to replace the box because the coast was never clear. Being
from out of town, I knew I wouldn't be back and didn't want to take
the box with me. The placer also was not from the area, so mailing
it back to them for replacement was also not a viable option. I
would have much rather spent that 2 hours hiking a 4-5 mile round-
trip letterbox than sitting on a bench waiting for an opportune time
to replace the box. Another box I found was located along an outdoor
stairway at a busy public place. The finder was clearly in view to
people both at the top of the stairway and at the bottom of the
stairway. Once I spotted the box in its hiding spot, I was hoping to
be able to non-chalantly bend down and tie my shoe and swoop up the
box. No such luck -- the reach was more than my arms could get to.
I actually ended up having to climb over onto the other side of the
stairway railing to get the box. Again, there was significant time
waiting for a time where people were not within eyesight -- both to
retrieve the box and replace it.

It is true that sometimes there is a scenic or interesting place you
want to bring people to with a letterbox that doesn't have a trail
system or a good out-of-the-way hiding spot -- those are hard, but
easier to understand why the planter chose the place they did. What
I don't understand is why a person would choose a park with a great
set of hiking trails, and then place the box in an open area within
plain view of the picnic area, rather than off on one of the trails.

People are by nature suspicious of anyone who doesn't quite fit in.
Even with a camera in hand, there are some places where it is almost
impossible to not look out of place just because you are lingering or
searching (no matter how discreet you try to be). Especially in this
day and age where security is heightened and everyone is more on
guard, I am not sure why it would be worth the risk. Not only is
there potential for losing the box or the finder and/or placer
getting in trouble, but it also gives letterboxing a bad name.

Another place where letterboxes don't belong is private property --
unless the clues very clearly state that it is private property and
that permission has been obtained. Likewise, instructing finders to
walk over private property to get to the box, even if the box is not
on private land, is a bad idea. Following clues to a letterbox on
private property in cases where permission has not been obtained is
trespassing. I recall hunting a box that required one to walk across
private property along Lake Michigan. A property owner's rights to
the beach extend about 3 feet into the water. I guess in summer it
would be possible to get there without trespassing by swimming or
walking in the water, but any other time of year, walking across the
beach is trespassing.

I personally do not care for urban boxes or boxes at roadside rest
areas, although I admit there is a time and a place -- urban boxes
can provide great historical and architectural exposure and roadside
stops are nice leg-stretchers on long expanses of highway. I guess
the reason these do not appeal to me so much is not having the
element of nature.

SpringChick

From: Doodle & Deedle Bug (doodle_n_deedle@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-09-21 09:18:07 UTC-07:00
Deedle is a very big fan of the "round trip letterbox" - a box that sends you one way to find it, and then provides an alternate route to get back to your car rather than retracing your steps. Of course, our first plant Kumbaya doens't necessarily fit this description, but that box was mainly my idea and plant, so he can deal with that.

I love it when the box takes us to a very scenic place, or historic place, some little gem we never knew existed. Esp. if it's along Lake Erie. But, like SpringChick said, you've got to make sure there's then a place off-the-beaten path (that won't create a new beaten path) to hide the box. If the search for the box takes you past an ice cream stand, that's always a bonus for me ;) Someone else mentioned having a good place to stamp in close by, yes, that's always a good thing, esp. if the box is hidden in a more secluded area where you can take your time to read through the logbook, which is one of my favorite things to do when I find a box.

What I don't like is when the box takes you too far off the trail and I feel like Uncle Travelling Matt in the woods needing a machete. (Anyone get the Fraggle Rock reference?) I've become a very big spaz about stepping on plants when we go off-trail and am always snapping at Deedle, "Watch where you're stepping! You just smashed that plant!" It took him a few minutes once to reassure me when I accidently snapped a tree's branch when holding on to it for support during a letterbox walk up a hill.

Living by Lake Erie, I love visiting the lake. There are already a handful of boxes hidden at lakeside parks. I'm slowly developing ideas to populate the rest of the lakeside parks that don't have boxes hidden there yet. Then you can all come visit us and do a Lake Erie Letterbox Tour :) I think it would be neat to hide a box by the Rock n' Roll Hall of Fame. There's a park next to it, but it's a city park (ie, no trees, manacured grass). We need to take a trip down there one day to see if there are any feasible hiding places....

Doodle.

From: ainawgsd (gmc81@randomkitty.net) | Date: 2004-09-24 18:09:21 UTC
Being from Central IL where the terrain is mostly flat except for the
areas around rivers and lakes, my favorite hiding places tend to be in
fallen logs, trees, and under wooden trail-side structures (such as
bridges or stairs) on less-used paths. I also like to use animal
trails to take people off main trails becuase it prevents/masks social
trails.

In recent outings to Starved Rock and Turkey Run, I've taken a strong
dislike to hiding boxes on busy trails. There are two boxes in Turkey
Run that are very well hidden and the locations would be excellent
except that they are both right on one of the busiest trails in the
park. I don't like feeling the need to drag a box 1/4 mile or more
away from it's hiding spot so that I can stamp in comfortably.
