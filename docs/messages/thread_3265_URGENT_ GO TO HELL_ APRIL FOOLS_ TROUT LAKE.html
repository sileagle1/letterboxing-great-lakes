>Brande (and everyone else, too),
>
>To a degree, letterboxing IS a secret society. Not
>that we want to keep people out, otherwise we'll never
>get new boxers hunting and new boxes planted and that
>would just cause the sport to die out. But the act of
>letterboxing is the "secret society" as in, when said
>letterboxer(s) are in the woods, what they're doing is
>in a way a covert mission. Others don't KNOW that
>there are boxes hidden in these woods, but we
>letterboxers DO and in order to protect our little
>rubber treasures we try to not draw attention to
>ourselves out on the trails. Which is why, when we
>find a box, we need to resist the urge to scream "I
>found it!!" when there are joggers around, and why
>it's so important to rehide that box so carefully so
>that, if, say, the kids blowing up firecrackers by the
>creek have noticed us in the woods and when we leave
>they decide to go investigate what we were doing their
>chances of finding the box and leaving obscenities in
>the logbook are lesser rather than greater.
>
>(Yes, we have found a logbook before where non-boxing
>teens left obscenities in the book; but at least they
>left the box intact. And yes, we have been
>letterboxing before where kids were blowing up
>firecrackers down by the creek. Luckily the box was
>hidden far enough from the creek where the kids didn't
>see us disappear into the woods. We also found box
>contents scattered around the woods once where a drunk
>evidently found the box first and left his empty beer
>can in the real hiding place.)
>
>The whole trailname aspect adds to the "secretive"
>quality of letterboxing. In the "real world" I'm
>known as Diane, but to letterboxers I am Doodle Bug.
>And let me tell you, it is SO COOL to be at a
>gathering and someone comes up to you and cries, "So
>YOU'RE Doodle Bug!" Makes you feel like a celebrity.
>
>Now, to paraphrase some sci fi character from some sci
>fi movie (maybe Yoda?) with Letterboxing comes Great
>Responsibility. Because as much as finding little
>rubber stamps (or sometimes big rubber stamps) is
>central to letterboxing, there's so much MORE to it
>that that article just doesn't touch on. Letterboxing
>isn't just about finding little boxes. It is about
>spending time with family and friends, enjoying the
>outdoors, exploring new places, treating these little
>works of art with respect, treating our surroundings
>with respect, and this article touched on none of
>that. There are so many resources out there to help a
>new letterboxer learn that there's more to
>letterboxing than just pulling on the hiking boots,
>printing off the clues, and pulling the box out from
>behind a pile of rocks. But this article made it seem
>like that's all there is to it, which I believe is
>what's enraged so many people on this list - me, at
>least. There's a letterboxing "spirit" that the
>article just doesn't talk about at all. It's the
>"spirit" that makes us want to convert all our family
>and friends into letterboxers, too, and show them HOW
>to letterbox, how to respect the box and their
>surroundings and appreciate that letterboxing is so
>much more than just a hidden container. In a way,
>letterboxing is a cult! We want newer letterboxers to
>take the time to learn at least a little of the
>history and respect for the cult - I mean game before
>pulling those hiking boots on. And some will, but
>some won't. And we're worried about the some who
>won't, because they're who endanger our boxes. And
>although one would hope that the wills would be
>greater than the won'ts, recent activity in the Great
>Lakes region (read some of the posts from the past
>three months) has shown that the won'ts seem to be
>outnumbering the wills. So clues get pulled, because
>there's no way of knowing who hunting for the box is a
>"will" and who is a "won't." And people don't want to
>risk the won'ts.
>
>Now did I confuse anyone else with that last
>paragraph?
>
>Doodle Bug.
