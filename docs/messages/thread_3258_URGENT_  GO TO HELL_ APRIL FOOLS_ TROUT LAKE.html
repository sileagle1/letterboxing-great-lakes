Re: URGENT! GO TO HELL, APRIL FOOLS, TROUT LAKE

From: duncan53211 (duncan53211@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-11-06 00:03:42 UTC
For those interested, here's the email I sent the reporter:

Greetings from The Dragon, owner of the Go to Hell and other
letterboxes in your county.

Myself and a number of other letterboxers are not very happy with the
article you published, particluarly since neither the article nor the
letterboxers that you interviewed stressed the main component to
letterboxing: take care of the boxes you find as if they are your own
so others may find them in excellent condition as well. Many of us
that have letterboxes in your county are fearing for our boxes right
now because experience has shown us that if the safekeeping of boxes
is not stressed in an article, boxes tend to disappear as "newbies" go
and find the boxes without proper knowledge on how to care for them.
Many, like myself, are currently busy pulling all our clues to our
boxes in your county for that very reason. Boxes like "Go to Hell" are
only special if they remain in good condition.

If you wish to continue to print articles on letterboxing or wish to
print a follow-up article, I would strongly suggest that you inteview
more seasoned letterboxers in MI like SpringChick, Azobox. and Team
Rabbit. I would also suggest that you point out specifically that
anyone interested in trying letterboxing MUST first refer to and take
to heart the suggestions for beginners found at the following websites:

SpringChick's website: http://www.stampinthebox.com/start.asp
LBNA faq: http://www.letterboxing.org/GettingStarted/
particularly:
http://www.letterboxing.org/GettingStarted/getstart_finding.htm
and
The Newboxers Group: http://groups.yahoo.com/group/newboxers/

Also, be advised that the individual clues to each box are owned by
the placer of the box. Before any specific boxes are named in any
article, permission should be received by the owner of the clues.

Thank you for your understanding.

Tim Kuehl
aka The Dragon

I think that taking your family out with you to do LBing is a great
idea. I don't think any of the ideas here are saying otherwise. We
just want to educate people that it's not just about YOU finding the
box it's about others finding it AFTER you. SpringChick takes her
grandchildren out with her, but she also keeps a watchful eye to
make sure they put the boxes back properly concealed. It is a great
opportunity to show children an example of the golden rule (do unto
others...). Boxers take alot of time and energy to put a box
together for others to enjoy, and by taking care of it it insures
that may more others will have the opportunity to enjoy it as much
as you did. If they are just going to get distroid once a month,
then the chances of the placer wanting to put it back out there are
slim.

From: Doodle & Deedle Bug (doodle_n_deedle@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-11-06 13:13:08 UTC-08:00
Brande (and everyone else, too),

To a degree, letterboxing IS a secret society. Not
that we want to keep people out, otherwise we'll never
get new boxers hunting and new boxes planted and that
would just cause the sport to die out. But the act of
letterboxing is the "secret society" as in, when said
letterboxer(s) are in the woods, what they're doing is
in a way a covert mission. Others don't KNOW that
there are boxes hidden in these woods, but we
letterboxers DO and in order to protect our little
rubber treasures we try to not draw attention to
ourselves out on the trails. Which is why, when we
find a box, we need to resist the urge to scream "I
found it!!" when there are joggers around, and why
it's so important to rehide that box so carefully so
that, if, say, the kids blowing up firecrackers by the
creek have noticed us in the woods and when we leave
they decide to go investigate what we were doing their
chances of finding the box and leaving obscenities in
the logbook are lesser rather than greater.

(Yes, we have found a logbook before where non-boxing
teens left obscenities in the book; but at least they
left the box intact. And yes, we have been
letterboxing before where kids were blowing up
firecrackers down by the creek. Luckily the box was
hidden far enough from the creek where the kids didn't
see us disappear into the woods. We also found box
contents scattered around the woods once where a drunk
evidently found the box first and left his empty beer
can in the real hiding place.)

The whole trailname aspect adds to the "secretive"
quality of letterboxing. In the "real world" I'm
known as Diane, but to letterboxers I am Doodle Bug.
And let me tell you, it is SO COOL to be at a
gathering and someone comes up to you and cries, "So
YOU'RE Doodle Bug!" Makes you feel like a celebrity.

Now, to paraphrase some sci fi character from some sci
fi movie (maybe Yoda?) with Letterboxing comes Great
Responsibility. Because as much as finding little
rubber stamps (or sometimes big rubber stamps) is
central to letterboxing, there's so much MORE to it
that that article just doesn't touch on. Letterboxing
isn't just about finding little boxes. It is about
spending time with family and friends, enjoying the
outdoors, exploring new places, treating these little
works of art with respect, treating our surroundings
with respect, and this article touched on none of
that. There are so many resources out there to help a
new letterboxer learn that there's more to
letterboxing than just pulling on the hiking boots,
printing off the clues, and pulling the box out from
behind a pile of rocks. But this article made it seem
like that's all there is to it, which I believe is
what's enraged so many people on this list - me, at
least. There's a letterboxing "spirit" that the
article just doesn't talk about at all. It's the
"spirit" that makes us want to convert all our family
and friends into letterboxers, too, and show them HOW
to letterbox, how to respect the box and their
surroundings and appreciate that letterboxing is so
much more than just a hidden container. In a way,
letterboxing is a cult! We want newer letterboxers to
take the time to learn at least a little of the
history and respect for the cult - I mean game before
pulling those hiking boots on. And some will, but
some won't. And we're worried about the some who
won't, because they're who endanger our boxes. And
although one would hope that the wills would be
greater than the won'ts, recent activity in the Great
Lakes region (read some of the posts from the past
three months) has shown that the won'ts seem to be
outnumbering the wills. So clues get pulled, because
there's no way of knowing who hunting for the box is a
"will" and who is a "won't." And people don't want to
risk the won'ts.

Now did I confuse anyone else with that last
paragraph?

Doodle Bug.

Letterboxing is for families, but it is suppose to be one of those things that you hear from word of mouth, not publicized, especially when they publicize it with no discrection for the people's boxes that are planted. When my family (which is my parents, myself and my 5 kids) started out in July, I just happend about it from the Ren Fair site. I read what it was about and then went to the sites it suggested, and read everything there. I feel people who are really interested in it, will research it, and respect what is written and have respect for others and their boxes. Our kids considered it finding a "buried treasure", and we supervise them when they rehide the box. If we feel it's not hidden good enough, we go and show them how to hide it so it's covered and looks like nothing is there. We were very lucky to run into another letterboxer, that helped us in our first hunt, and how to rehide, and they showed the kids how to do it as well. I feel if anyone plans on publicizin
g about anything like this that is a word of mouth, that they do alot of research, and not give out al the details, but let the person do the research on their own. If the family is really interested, they would do the research themselves to find out more about the hobby, by doing a search for a letterbox site, and find all the info about it. I feel if everything is handed to people on a platter, then they are going to go wild, and have no respect for the hobby. For those who are serious about the hobby, will do the research, find what they need to do, and read the sites as to how the hobby is done, and be more apt to respect the boxes and rehide the boxes the right way. If they read the sites, I believe it says about creating a trail name. It makes the hobby more interesting, and cryptic. I feel more connected, and the people stand out more by their trail name than a real name.
