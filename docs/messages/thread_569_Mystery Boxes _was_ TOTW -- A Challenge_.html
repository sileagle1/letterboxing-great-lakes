--- In LbGLK@yahoogroups.com, "lesann847" wrote:
> Mystery boxes really fascinate me, so I'd be very interested in a
> discussion on this thread. So far, there is no such thing as a
> generic mystery box. Each one I have found has been unique,
> particularly imbued with the personality of its creator. This is
> because there is so much work that goes into developing a
> good mystery, the creator just shines through in every element of
> the box.
>

I think for me this is part of the draw with mystery boxes -- the
fact that each one is so unique from the next and there is always an
element of surprise, not knowing what you will run up against, right
up until the end -- and too, the flavor that is given by the
personality of the creator.

I prefer mystery boxes where I can at least get the location
narrowed down before leaving the house. It doesn't need to be the
specific spot, but the correct city and park are nice to know. Some
of my favorites require a bit of running around on site to get the
right trail or area of a park or city. This is okay, and it can
really be a lot of fun running around town collecting clues to a
box.

But once I've followed the clues and get to the spot where the box
is hidden, I want the clue to be pretty clear about where to find
the letterbox. My pet peeve is getting to the end of the clues and
not having a clue where to look for the box. This puts the seeker
in a position to have to look high and low for the hiding spot and
can result in the upset of an area, turning over rocks and loose
logs, etc.

I think theme is perhaps more important with a mystery box than a
non-mystery box because it provides a cohesiveness. It gives the
seeker a framework within which to look and interpret clues. Having
a theme also helps as the creator of a mystery box to keep a focus
when creating the clues.

My favorite clues are those where I learn something by doing the
research required to solve the clues, riddle or puzzle -- perhaps
about the location, or the theme (i.e. maybe a person, a bit of
history, a culture or a custom), or even about the person who
created the box. Of course some appeal more than others because
they concentrate on things that are of interest to me, such as boxes
about a particular musical group or band, but this is more related
to personal preference than the quality of the mystery.

> The "quality" of the stamp is less important to me than the
> process by which I found it. Nonetheless, I delight in skilled
> carving, or clever jokes embedded in the stamp.
>
Yes I would agree with this wholeheartedly. With a mystery box it
is more about the process. Of course all of the mystery boxes I
have found have included very nice stamps, and perhaps I would feel
different about this if that had not been the case.

I also echo some of the comments made by Azobox regarding the
frustration of *mystery spoilers* both from the perspective of a
seeker and a placer.

As a seeker, I have several mystery boxes from all over the country
in various stages of unravel -- some I have run stuck on, some seem
the type that will fall into place with time, and still others I
simply haven't had time to complete. Sometimes you garner clues and
hints from reading totally unrelated letterboxing posts or clues,
and this always gives that "Aha" feeling. But there is a difference
between putting two and two together to make such a connection, and
having a mystery outrightly spoiled by a careless letterboxing
story. And sometimes it may seem innocent to the person posting --
a list of boxes found on a particular day may include a mystery box,
thus giving away a perhaps unknown location simply by placing the
other boxes that were found that day.

As a placer, it can be extremely frustrating to put the time and
work into creating a mystery, putting together a story or a puzzle
referencing an obscure landmark, etc., and then have another
letterboxer's clues inadvertently give away your mystery by
referencing the same landmark AND giving location and/or further
details. There is then the choice -- do I rework the clues or just
hope people don't make the connection? I appreciate when people
take the time to at least read through mystery box clues before
posting their own clues so this is less likely to happen.

One of our favorite (and most frustrating) hunts was the Newton
Apple box in NYC. Prior to leaving MI, we had narrowed down the
general location of the box as well as formulated some theories of
how to interpret the cryptic clues once there. Old Shoe and I spent
the better part of a day trying different possibilities and finally
at the end of an exhausting day spent trapsing around a several
square block area in NYC, we called it quits. We found out later
that we had been right on with one of our interpretations and either
missed the box or it simply wasn't there. Even though it was
disappointing not finding the box after all that -- it still stands
out in my mind as one of the most fun hunts we've done.

SpringChick

From: lesann847 (l-fischer@northwestern.edu) | Date: 2004-08-20 12:22:25 UTC
Thank you Azobox and SpringChick for posting on this thread; it
is both interesting and helpful to me to know what mystery box
fans value about the hunt. I was struck by Spring Chick's:

> As a seeker, I have several mystery boxes from all over the
country
> in various stages of unravel -- some I have run stuck on, some
seem
> the type that will fall into place with time, and still others I
> simply haven't had time to complete. Sometimes you garner
clues and
> hints from reading totally unrelated letterboxing posts or clues,
> and this always gives that "Aha" feeling.

I hadn't even considered that people outside the area might
hoard up mystery clues for solving at leisure on the off chance
they might visit the area some day. I know how I'll be spending
the days I spend indoors this winter (:->), but more importantly
this bit of information will influence mystery boxes I place.

While I want letterboxers to pursue my mystery boxes (and other
boxes), I place because it feels good. I enjoy the process of
conceptualizing a box; I am pleased when I can find the right
artwork that inspires the stamp, I love to make custom journals,
most of all I like to explore a theme very fully and see just how far
I can take it. Once I make something, I don't like to have it lying
around the house; letterboxing has provided me with a venue.

I love the research involved in developing letterboxes; I wish I
could share everything I've learned with letterboxers who visit,
but find myself making having to make choices--I know the
letterboxer will want to move on to the next box not sit in the
woods reading a dissertation while swatting huge mosquitoes.
With a mystery box, the seeker does some of the research I've
already done; I can weave elaborate stories that may or may not
include clues. I have a bit more scope to share information on a
theme; afterall, only someone very interested will bother with a
mystery box. I have more of their attention.

Most importantly, planting a box--particularly a mystery box that
demands so much of its creator--is a way of reaching out to
those who enjoy the things I enjoy.

Both Azobox and SpringChick addressed the issue of
thoughtless posters giving away locations of mystery boxes
through list discussion, clues, or box placement. I try not to post
that I've found a mystery box until months after I've found it, and
even then I try to avoid mentioning its name on open lists. I do
this not because because I'm averse to discreet discussion, but
I don't trust myself not to let something inadvertently slip.

As to clues or placement giving away a mystery box location, if a
placer has not solved the mystery she or he may not even know
it's there. If this happened to one of my mystery boxes I might
write to the placer of the second (nonmystery) box and see if
there was something we could work out, and if not I think I'd
rather change my clues than hope letterboxers don't make the
connection. The one thing you can say about letterboxers is that
they're a particularly intelligent bunch.

From: Doodle & Deedle Bug (doodle_n_deedle@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-08-20 12:48:09 UTC-07:00
Just wanted to talk a little more about the mystery
box thread. One of the first ideas I came up with for
planting a box of my own was for a mystery box. In
this instance, the main reason for making it a mystery
box was to keep the location low-key, in case
non-boxers googled the location and learned about the
box and then harm befell the box (or location) --
which is entirely possible, since that's how I learned
about letterboxing in the first place, while searching
for information for a location I was directed towards
clues for a box planted there. Then as i thought more
about the location (and after having read through
numerous clues for other boxes) decided that in
addition to having to do a little research to find the
box, it might be fun to make the directions to follow
to the box once you got to the location a little more
cryptic, too. The box is still in process (as all
mine & Deedle's are, I know).

I really admire the mystery clues...even if a lot of
them have me banging my head against the desk because
for the life of me I can't figure them out. It makes
the "ah-ha" moment when they're finally cracked that
much sweeter...well, as sweet as a moment can be when
the men in the white coats are taking you away :)

Doodle.
