From: Becky (imdurangogirl@aol.com) | Date: 2006-08-30 14:11:06 UTC
I'm coming to the list to ask everyone's opinion on how to handle a
situation. I received an email through letterboxing.org from someone
wanting to know if the box was still there. They wanted to know,
because if it wasn't there they didn't want to take the time to look
for it with their child, so the child wouldn't be disappointed. When I
read the email I had many thoughts go through my mind. One thought, was
look for the box and take the opportunity to teach your child that all
though in life we don't always get what we want, the journey can be
just as rewarding. The second thought was this really isn't the sprit
of letterboxing. I did respond to the person, letting them know that
to the best of my knowledge the box was still present, although I've
not been to the site in a few months. But now, I wonder if I should
have taken the opportunity to educate a little bit to the "spirit" of
letterboxing. Any thoughts?

Nor do I, as previously stated. I think the rub here was that there
are those who want a guarantee and who will only look for boxes that
are *for sure* there.

Just like everyone has their preference as to long hike or short
hike, paved trail, mountain top or drive-by, so there are
differences of opinon in how much a person feels should be left to
the unknown. Personally I don't have a problem driving a distance
to look for a box and coming up empty-handed. Ninety-nine times out
of a hundred I will have enjoyed a rewarding hike, perhaps traveled
to an area that I had never been before, just got out and enjoyed
the day. I certainly value my time, but it would be hard for me to
consider it a waste of time just because I didn't score a stamp.
Heck before I ever heard of letterboxing, I used to do those hikes
without expecting anything other than a peaceful walk in the woods
and maybe a view of the lake.

So, let's say you as a seeker want a reasonable assurance that a box
is in place before you go looking for it. While you certainly have
the right to ask me if the box is in place, I am not obligated to
tell you if I personally feel that it is more in the spirit of
letterboxing if there is an element of unknown when setting out on a
letterbox hunt. If you then decide to skip my box because you were
not given a guarantee, I really don't care -- it is your loss, not
mine, because even if the box had not been there, you were sure to
have enjoyed a wonderful walk along a beach or in the woods, etc.
This is why I plant boxes where I do -- along scenic trails, in
historically significant places, locations with a view -- so if the
box is missing or just not found, it has still been a memorable
experience.

There are no guarantees in letterboxing. Somebody can write and ask
me the status of one of my boxes and I can tell them that I am
fairly certain that it is there because there was a found status
logged a couple of weeks previously. That does not mean the box is
there -- perhaps those letterboxers who logged the find left the box
exposed or were seen by non letterboxers, perhaps nature intervened
since then, etc.

While finding a box at the end of the trail is certainly the primary
goal for many people -- to differing degrees, those who take time to
smell the roses, enjoy what nature and history have to offer, and
treasure the time spent with family (or in solitude), definitely
will encounter less disappointment with this hobby, even when they
come away from a hunt without a stamp. Just as with so many other
things in life, a letterbox journey is what *you* make it to be.

SpringChick

--- In LbGLK@yahoogroups.com, "Carol Osada" wrote:

> But I think some of us are just preaching to the choir
> so to speak, because we already understand what this
> sport is all about.

Interesting comment and very true. It made me think though...
those of us who have been involved in letterboxing for a while have
heard it a hundred times and wonder, does it really need to be said
again? (yeh, yeh, we get it...) But when there are many new members
in the congregation, maybe a repeat sermon does serve a purpose.

Perhaps that is where we are falling short in educating new
letterboxers and passing along the intangible concepts -- the spirit
of the game, the ethics, the stealthy aspect, etc. We have latched
onto those things and they seem so natural and ingrained in the way
we perceive letterboxing and the way we play the game. We take
those things for granted and assume they go without saying --
because to us they do, not to mention they tend to be things that by
nature are not out-in-the-open.

But none of us started there, rather we all came into letterboxing
with a surface understanding of the motions you go through to play
the game -- follow clues, stamp the book, increase your "F" count,
repeat as many times as possible... And on a rudimentary level,
that is letterboxing and for some people that is all there ever is.
In some cases that is because that's all they want from it, but in
other cases, I think it is because they don't know any different,
because there is no handbook outlining the peripherals, because that
is all they have been exposed to. For those who do think of
letterboxing on a deeper level, that was an understanding that was
picked up along the way, from those we came in contact with, from
experience, etc. Many who have been around for a few years,
particularly before the regional talk lists, gleaned these concepts
from interacting with/watching people like MapSurfer, Legerdemaine,
Der Mad Stamper, Jay Drew, etc. But a lot of those folks are no
longer actively participating, nor are they visibly passing along
the fundamentals of letterboxing that many of us take for granted.

There is an interesting conversation over at the newboxers list
about being sneaky and in particular, how to teach kids to letterbox
stealthily without making it seem like a crime. It is encouraging
to see those concepts being passed on to newer participants.

SpringChick
