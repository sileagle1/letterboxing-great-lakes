TOTW -- Log Books

From: Deb (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-04-12 10:20:42 UTC
There was a short-lived thread on the big list this past week about
various log book styles and techniques. As well, it is a discussion
I had this past week with a fellow boxer as I am just finishing up
my second book and ready to break into a new one -- and still
looking for a comfortable balance of how to do it.

So this week's TOTW is personal log books -- what type of book do
you use or do you use cards? If you use cards, what do you do with
them? What do you log in your book -- finds, plants, exchanges, all
of the above? Do you make notes? If so, what do you record? Do
you keep the clues as part of your log book?

From: Delirium (donaldson_home@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-04-12 07:11:08 UTC-07:00
I remember this thread from the big list, and some of the responses I read on it prompted me to go out and change my logging style.
I have a big logbook- it's a nice book with thick pages and a banana-leaf cover. Not something you'd take out with you. When I go out, I take a regular book with medium weight paper. That way, I can stamp multiple times to get the "perfect" stamp.
Once home, I cut the stamp from the "field book", mount it on paper, attach it to the big book with eyelets or some such, then record a rambling sort of narrative: name of box, location, date, who went with me, trials and tribulations attached to finding the box (a common theme in my 'boxing).

My book has a little bit of everything, finds, plants (including two logbooks that were either filled up or from boxes I had to yank), exchanges, doodles, etc.

It's like my birding book- I have a field guide for going out, which I use to record finds, dates, places, but I also have a large bound book from Audobon which doesn't go out, but serves as a more permanent record of the hobby.

From: (mjpepe1@comcast.net) | Date: 2004-04-12 14:42:24 UTC
Sue and I use a beautifully embossed leather logbook made by Oberon. They come in 3 sizes. I use the large size @ 6 x 9 but should have bought the 4.75 x 7 small size as we only place 1 stamp per page, unless it's a series and can fit more than 1 per page.

We stamp on the right side only with box name, location [park/trail name], city, state, date and placer. Then I generally write a little commentary about the location, an adventure that happened finding the box, etc. We have been taking pictures and place a picture applicable to the box on the facing, left page.

For those interested, the link for Oberon is below:

http://www.oberondesign.com/store/default.php

While these journal covers are on the expensive side, they are truly works of art, available in many designs and colors but they will last a lifetime, are handmade and include a blank journal. Refills blank journals are available from the same company but these appear to be standard sizes, available from most Staples or other office supply stores.


Have fun and just get out there & box!!!

Mark Pepe
http://pepeanddavidow.blogspot.com/

From: (duncan53211@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-04-13 01:07:19 UTC
When I started letterboxing, I didn't really have much of a clue
what to do in the log book. I got a neat artist's sketch book from
Schwartz's that has a transparent green cover. I stamped my personal
stamp on the first page, and it shows through for a neat effect.
Also on the front page I have all of information needed to get the
book to me if it ever gets mis-placed. I also keep track of my
current FPHAEX counts on the first page.

I generally use the right side to stamp my "finds". I have recoded
the find #, the date, and usually the name of the box, although I've
gotten quite lax in keeping up with it other than the #. On the
left page I have stamped my "plants". I stamp HHs in with my finds,
unless they are my HHs, which go with my Ps. I have my exchanges
starting in the back of my logbook working towards the front. Yes,
my log book is a twisted mess, and I don't suggest anyone to follow
my system.

After seeing other LBers log books, I intend to do things completely
different with my next log book. I will make it more like a diary.
As I plant, find, echange, etc. I'll start at the beginning and go
consecutively. I will note the # of the P,F, or whatever, the date,
name, and record a note about the experience. This is what I would
encourage new letterboxers to do.

As a side note, I think we could do new letterboxers a service by
having more helpful information about how to begin letterboxing than
what is found at the LBNA site. The FAQ that everyone refers to is
out of date and it only touches the surface. I didn't find it
overly helpful. Maybe we should make one of our own for all the new
LBers in our area.

Dragon

Re: TOTW -- Log Books

From: azobox (jparkerg@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-04-14 10:25:45 UTC
When we began letterboxing it was almost a spur-of-the-moment
decision. So when we headed out of the house, we grabbed a couple of
journals that we just had on hand. I'm still using that first one.
It's 5x7, wirebound, medium weight cream colored, very slightly
speckled paper. The paper has a smooth surface. The book covers are
wood, which turned out to be a great advantage as it provides a solid
surface as I'm stamping.
I only stamp on the right hand side. I began in the front with my
finds (obviously). When I decided to plant a box, I stamped it in the
back. What then evolved was that pattern of "finds" from the front of
the book and placements from the back. I stamp multiple images on a
page. So, the stamps in the front and the stamps in the back are
moving towards eachother in the middle of the book. I do not include
many notes, although I have admired logbooks I've seen where details
of the find are included. I'm usually ready to head off to the next
box rather than sit and reflect on the current experience. I hope to
change that habit. When the book is full, I'll probably continue the
current stamping plan. I don't keep clues with the logbook but I do
have a file with many clues for boxes we've searched. I also keep
park-specific maps with the clues.
Hitchers I've launched get stamped in the back with placements.
Exchanges go with "finds" in chronological order.
I do like having all of the images in one book.


azobox



Re: TOTW -- Log Books

From: Deb (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-04-14 16:34:24 UTC
I am still searching for the way that works best for me. When we
first started letterboxing, I was just so excited to get out and find
a box, that I didn't really give much thought to the book (or even my
stamp at the time). Our first log book was a 5" x 8" spiral sketch
book with a plain cardboard cover -- the only variety available at
the office supply store we stopped at on our way to Chicago. I
stamped chronilogically, plants and finds all together (no exchanges
in the first book). When I reached the back of the book, I turned it
over and stamped on the opposite sides as the paper was quite thick
and did not bleed through.

I didn't like that the plants and finds were all mixed in amongst
eachother, and also didn't like that if I messed up a stamp, I was
stuck with it -- especially for plants as these were the stamps I had
carved. In January 2003, we started a new log book -- a very nice 4
x 6 leather cover book with textured tan colored pages. With the new
book, I decided to use the log book only for finds and exchanges, and
to do plants on 3x5 cards, which I keep in a small photo album. The
pages are a bit thinner than my first book and some of the inks bleed
through, so I will not be able to use the back sides of the pages in
this book. This all seemed okay until a few times when I was sharing
my book with a friend and had only finds to show, as my plants were
home in the photo album.

This second book is nearly full and I have just purchased a new book -
- a cute little bound book that looks very old English library. It
is about 4 x 6 and has decorative edging on the pages -- I will
upload a photo later this evening. With this book, I will try yet
another approach, along the lines of the method azobox uses -- stamp
from the front for finds and exchanges and begin stamping plants from
the back. I will also continue to stamp plants onto the cards and
put them into the album as a more permanent record.

As to what I put in the book besides the stamps -- I tend to make
lots of notes -- who I was with, date, box name, location, sometimes
the name of the person who planted it and the date it was planted.
Comments about the weather, the hike, the scenery, the clues -- if it
was a fairly easy find or we had to work for it. I also make notes
about boxes I looked for but did not find so that I can e-mail the
placer and hopefully give some helpful information, or just so that
when I come back and look again, I will know what my thought process
was.

SpringChick

From: Angela (missangi1210@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-04-15 23:59:32 UTC
RIght now I have only gone out to find one box. I love photography,
so I decided I would take a picture of where each one is hidden, but
from far enough back that you can barely see the box. I don't plan on
showing the pictures to those who want to search for the boxes, as it
would ruin the surprise, but I think it will be fun to look back at
where they were hidden. It is kind of like a Where's Waldo type of
thing. I also typed up the clues and printed them really small, cut
it out, and taped in on the facing page. The stamp went on the right
side with the printed photo. I'm the type of person who will look
back and say, "I wish I did it this way" and want to go back to find
all the stamps again just to make a new book, but I am trying to hold
back from that feeling...lol. I liked reading everyone else's ideas,
it gives me some good ones!
