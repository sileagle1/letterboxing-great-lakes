Aimlesst,

I have some files on my yahoogroup, about the various carving
materials that you might be interested in. Here is the link to the
homepage of the yahoogroup:
http://groups.yahoo.com/group/Letterboxing_Bindlestiff/
Go to "files" (on the left side of your screen)
then select "supplies"
You will find two files of interest:
1. Carving Material experiments - with personal opinion reviews
2. Carving Material Types - and cost comparions of alternative
products to PZ-kut

Also, my daughter, Jade (formerly known as Enders Angel) really likes
the soft, smooth, easy way that Speedy-Cut carves. However, it doesn't
do well with fine detail and it doesn't hold up under heavy use. That
doesn't mean it's not a viable option for learning to carve on,
though. We've found that mounting it extends the life by a long-shot.
(I have a picture trail site with a mounting tutorial using cheap
materials that's easy to follow; if you're interested)

Sorry to hear you're backing down on you initial excitement and plans
for carving. When I stumbled onto letterboxing, I was so excited I
couldn't wait to begin! I found an eraser in my studio and hacked out
my first stamp with a steak knife. (Boy did it look like it, too! LOL)
I placed that box (Kent Trails letterbox) the very next morning. When
the box went missing after a year, I re-carved the stamp to be very
similar to the original in order to give hope and inspiration to new
carvers. I'll throw a copy of the image up in the photos section (look
in the misc album) so you can compare it to things I've carved more
recently. I find it amazing that ~I~ have come so far.

Geez oh pizza, everyone starts somewhere. I don't see the sense in
being ashamed or embarrassed about the beginning of any journey,
artistic or otherwise.

Sail on!

> Gabrielle Coil wrote:
> The type of carving material that is best is largely a
matter of personal preferance. I am a strong believer that there
aren't many "bad" carving materials out there...just that some are
better for certain applications. Harder material will support more
detail, but is more difficult to carve. Softer material is easier to
carve but may not be a good choice for very fine detail (like the Rose
Gardener or March Hare stamps from the gathering). Personally, I don't
have the patience for uber-fine details, so I don't really mind a
slightly softer material. My advice is to go ahead and try a couple of
different materials if you can to see which you like best.
>
> Now the more important question for you is, did you get the white
speedy-cut or the pink speedy-cut? Most people don't like the white
stuff as it tends to be crumbly (a property I have used in a few
carvings to my advantage). I used the white stuff for many of my
earlier carvings. My Fish Tank series doesn't see much use right now,
so it has worked fine for that. However, my first sig. stamp made out
of the white stuff had many "added character" marks by the time I
retired it from things banging against it in the back pack. The pink
stuff is a much harder material and seems more durable than the white
stuff.
>
> Good luck on your first stamp! And if you are a bit dissapointed
with your skills the first few times around...just remember that it
takes practice to work up to some of the intricate masterpieces you
have seen.
>
> Kimono Girl

Re: [LbGLK] Re: carving materials info (and pep talk)

From: Carol Osada (cjosada@hotmail.com) | Date: 2006-06-13 01:40:54 UTC-05:00
Aimlesst,

We all need a pep talk from time to time. But I had to chuckle when I read PJ's comment about the eraser carved with a steak knife. When the LB bug first bites some people, it must really bite hard. The bug bit me too. I didn't wait to carve a stamp; I bought a commercially made stamp and just hit the trails.

This recent LbGLK Gathering was my first, and I too was impressed with the gathering stamps. They were definately the crme de la crme of stamps, maybe because only experienced carvers are confident enough to volunteer to submit a box. But sometimes a simple, well done stamp is all that is needed for a box on a trail. Once, I found a box with a stamp carved by a 5 year old girl. I could hardly make out the image (Piglet), but I just love that little stamp.

It's not a competition. The more you carve, the easier it becomes and you develop your own technique and style. This group of creative carvers has been very encouraging and helpful when it comes to sharing advice and experiences with products on the market.

Just have fun.

Beetle
