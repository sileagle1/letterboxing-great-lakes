From: merlinandardea (rhesse34@hotmail.com) | Date: 2004-06-17 13:48:33 UTC
I noticed a couple comments about Poison Ivy and thought I'd pass
this along.
Spotted Jewelweed (Wild-Touch-Me-Not or "Impatiens capensis", if
you're into botanizing) contains a chemical (2-methoxy-1, 4-
nathaquinone!!!) that in addition to it's fungicidal properties (Got
your attention Fungus Woman?) is excellent for treating Poison Ivy
rashes. I saw it used on a badly swollen face in the evening and the
same, nearly normal face the next morning.
It grows in wet areas usually where there is some moving water, say
around a shady spring or stream, but it can also be found around the
edge of marshes. Stinging Nettle is a common companion, but it also
works well for stopping the stinging too.
To use it, you squash the watery stem and apply the juice to the
affected area. It's a very common plant, so there's no need to worry
about destroying an endangered species.
Jewelweed has orange flowers (there's a closely related species
with yellow flowers), and both are shown in "Wildflowers of
Wisconsin" by Stan Tekiela. However, I would be surprised if any
local wildflower book didn't include one or both.
Happy, no itch 'Boxing.
Merlin

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2008-05-30 05:54:12 UTC-04:00
As a general rule, people with fairer complexions are more susceptible to poison ivy and those with ruddier complexions have a better resistance. But with each subsequent exposure to poison ivy, you become less resistant to the stuff, and it is not uncommon for people who have for years been exposed without incident to all of a sudden develop reactions. I can certainly attest to the validity of that statement. There was a time that reaching into a patch of poison ivy was of little concern to me and would leave me with a little red bump or two. After several incidents over that past 3-4 years, I now break out in blistering welts covering much of my body just from being in the general vicinity of the stuff. Each outbreak seems to get worse and linger longer. Last year I had a run-in with poison ivy over Memorial Day weekend and was still treating it well into July.

I've also read recently that the toxins in poison ivy and other urishiol producing plants (i.e. poison oak) are getting stronger, supposedly due to an increase in greenhouse gasses. Apparently the more carbon dioxide they have, the stronger, more potent and more prolific they grow. This will also cause harder outbreaks and reactions in people who have not previously been bothered.

Then there is this interesting article (http://www.wwmag.net/pivy.htm) about immunizing yourself by ingesting poison ivy leaves. In theory it sounds logical, but I cannot get past the nightmares of blisters lining my stomach and esophagus, not to mention my mouth and lips. So for now I will continue to immediately shower and wash everything thoroughly after each trip in the woods. The article does give some good info about using Jewelweed to counteract the effects of poison ivy if you are not already familiar with that. Also, having tried many, many over-the-counter remedies, washes and scrubs to treat, I swear by Technu scrub -- but it is a bit difficult to find and everywhere you go to look for it, they will try to sell you other stuff that "works just as good." Don't buy it... keep looking for Technu. Some Walgreens carry it, but not all. The best bet is to get it online so you're not running all over creation looking for it when you need it. They carry it at drugstore.com.

SpringChick
