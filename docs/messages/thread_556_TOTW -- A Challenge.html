From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-08-17 10:36:20 UTC
Hi everyone! A big welcome to all of the new members who have
joined the group in the past few weeks!

For this week's Topic of the Week, I am posing a challenge instead
of a question. I think most people get started boxing by looking
for letterboxes. But eventually most letterboxers also begin
planting boxes of their own. I think it is at that point, once you
have planted your first few boxes, that you really begin to have an
appreciation for the game and everything that goes into the creation
of the letterboxes you have been hunting for.

So, my challenge is for everyone to create plant a letterbox. I
think for some, taking that step to place a box and write clues is a
bit scary -- will people like my box, will I do it right? But
believe me, there is no right or wrong way to do it, there is no
secret to a perfect letterbox. Sure, as you get more involved in
the game, perhaps you will focus on themes or the historic
significance of a location and those are great boxes, but not a
requirement for a letterbox.

So, I am challenging you to just do it!

Actually, I was going to challenge everyone to create a mystery box,
but decided some of the newer members may not be comfortable with
that, especially if they have not yet found a mystery box. And I
also realize that even some of the more seasoned letterboxers are
just not the mystery box type. So, I am not specifying any
particular kind of box -- just plant a box! It would be nice to see
some mystery boxes come from this though -- so if you've been
thinking about one -- challenge yourself a bit and give it a try.

I am hoping this challenge will generate some discussion and
questions on the list about how to do this, or what is the best way
people have found to do this, etc.

When you have your box planted, post the clues to the LbNA web site
or on your own web site or perhaps you want to post only to this
list -- it is up to you. But let us know what you have done -- of
course, without giving away any mysteries.

Just do it!

SpringChick

From: lesann847 (l-fischer@northwestern.edu) | Date: 2004-08-17 12:32:03 UTC
Mystery boxes really fascinate me, so I'd be very interested in a
discussion on this thread. So far, there is no such thing as a
generic mystery box. Each one I have found has been unique,
particularly imbued with the personality of its creator. This is
because there is so much work that goes into developing a
good mystery, the creator just shines through in every element of
the box.

For my personal taste, a great mystery box includes a fun story
or framework, some research I can conduct before I get out in
the field, clever clues (I don't mind having to return a few times--
right TT? ;->), and of course a good stamp. It's an added bonus
for me if the creator includes puzzles, ciphers, codes, rebus,
anagrams or other forms of play.

The "quality" of the stamp is less important to me than the
process by which I found it. Nonetheless, I delight in skilled
carving, or clever jokes embedded in the stamp.

My sometime letterboxing partner (and dear husband), is more
goal-driven than process-oriented. Sometimes, that's just what I
need to finally track down the mystery. We were boxing in
another state, I had done the preliminary research to find the
right site. I was, however, given wrong information by the local
tourist board and we were continually misdirected by locals
throughout a long day of tracking down the mystery box. After
making a circuit around the entire county, we finally located the
box two blocks from where we began that morning. It was a little
too much "process" at the time, but we still laugh about it and
how he just wouldn't give up.

I'd love to hear other stories and comments on mystery boxes.

Quality over quantity is an admirable mantra for letterboxers. I would
rather plant (or seek) one quality box than a half dozen ho-hum, follow the
directions type boxes that really offer very little in terms of creativity,
location, challenge, history, etc. Of course, both have a place --
particularly when populating a new area with letterboxes and when trying to
attract new 'boxers to an area. Face it, *most* people don't start off by
solving mystery clues and hunting for boxes with cryptic landmark
references... although I have been impressed by a few newcomers who have
not opted for the easy way to F10.

Of course, you are free to plant as many as you like... For those who have
been at it for a while, this probably is just all in a week's activities,
which is why I added the bit about the mystery box -- perhaps that is new
even to some who have been planting boxes for a while... and a well-done
mystery does take a considerable amount of time. The intention was to give
a little kick in the butt for those who have been hesitating or even for
those who have kind of gone stale.

I wouldn't necessarily call it "the easy way to F10." For me, I'd call it
more "letterboxing isn't the only thing I do in life." I don't want to sit at a
computer or sit in a library and do a bunch of research about clues. And
neither do the kiddies who sometimes accompany me outdoors. If I read a set of
"mystery" clues and recognize the landmark right off, fine. If not, I won't
spend a lot of time indoors doing the research. I have to research enough stuff
indoors, I don't want to add letterboxing clues to the mix.

I'm outside as much as possible. I have an outdoors government job, but
that's far from enough "outdoors" for me. I also do outdoor photography as a
hobby, and I take small groups of underprivileged kids on hiking and camping field
trips. So finding letterboxes along the way is just a little icing on the
cake, so to speak. What hooked me on letterboxing was it's ability to fit into
my lifestyle so easily.

For example, I was just down at the Little Red Schoolhouse in Willow Springs,
IL. Not being in Cook County much, I never even knew the place existed.
But being an alkaline area rather than an acidic area, the kids & I got to see
plant life that was a little different. They also got to see the huge
domination of grey squirrels compared to our area further north where the red squirrel
predominates and pushes out the gray. At first, they were constantly amazed
at the "baby squirrels" running all over the place. They were more amazed
when they found out that those weren't babies, they were just a smaller, softer
species. And it was a good way to introduce some of them to the concept of
species, subspecies, etc on a living level. We also got to see a teeny tiny
little snapping turtle hatch out of it's shell, and marvel about how, at only
being minutes old, it looked just as grumpy as an adult. They got to get a
little bit upclose and personal with the view of thousands of lilypads, which
they'd never seen before, and were amazed to see birds actually walk across the
lilypads in search of insects. And they got to see a Great Blue Heron rise up in
flight -- not a scene new to them, but something that's always a beautiful
sight. And since an awfully lot of people walk the trails out there, they got a
lot more practice in pretending to be CIA operatives.

The other day we were at the Graue Mill and the kids were helping me come up
with clues for planting boxes and helping me scout out good hiding places.
But at the same time, they learned all about the workings of a grist mill, saw
the (supposedly) oldest house in DuPage County, learned about the Underground
Railroad, learned that Fullerburg Forest Preserve has the worst smelling
latrines that they've come across to date, learned a little something about the
Native Americans that lived along Salt Creek waaay waaaay back, learned a lot
about the likes and dislikes of the huge cottonwood trees that are a major species
there, finally got upclose and personal with some female goldfinches (they'd
only noticed the flashy males to date), and found out that just like human
females, female goldfinches do indeed have a voice of their own, etc.

So, anyway, when it comes to letterboxing, I like the fact that there's
something for everybody. Options. Americans are big on options. :-) For the
most part, I like the option of having my letterboxing fit in with the other
stuff I'm doing out there.

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-08-19 12:21:02 UTC-04:00
Yes, as I said -- there is a place for both.

Frankly I am impressed with anyone who gets off their butt and gets outside
to letterbox -- no matter how easy or difficult the find or the placement.
Everyone looks for something different in this hobby -- some people are
attracted to the creative aspects, some to long hiking adventures, others to
puzzling clues and still others embrace the hobby because it is a
combination of all of these things -- take as much or little of each as you
like. Although traditionally letterboxing has been a cloak and dagger kind
of thing with mysterious locations and cryptic clues playing a big part in
the inception of the hobby, I do realize that the hobby has evolved to be
much broader than that in order to accommodate a much more diverse group of
participants. Mystery boxes are certainly not everyone's cup of tea
(although not all mystery boxes require extensive indoor research), and
neither are long, challenging hikes -- heck, we all have different
definitions of what a "long, challenging hike" even is.

However, I cannot help being especially impressed when a new boxer attempts
some of the tougher finds -- either in terms of challenging clues,
out-of-the-way places or a grueling hike. It makes me feel like, "Yes...
they get it!" as I do feel it is important for participants to understand
the roots and "underground" aspects of the hobby -- if for no other reason
than to have a respect for the efforts of people who choose to adhere to
that standard of play. That is not to make anyone else's effort or style of
play insignificant or even categorize it as "easy" as that is something each
person defines for himself.

SpringChick

Um, OK, for the record, my response below was meant to
be devil's advocate pointing out how silly we all on
the list were getting about delving into the "history"
of the game and how it was originally "meant to be
played" at its "inception." I personally like how we
play the game in the US, and do believe it's a bit of
a cloak and dagger operation and believe that we need
to keep it like that with the influx of media that's
been going on. Simply because too much attention
truly destroys a good thing and ruins its original
essence, ie the Beat Generation (and if anyone really
wants, I could draw remarkable parallels between the
Beats and Letterboxing in the US right now, but I'm
sure we've all got better things to do :)

Things change and evolve over time, and it's a good
thing. It's necessary for survival. Yes, the first
guy in Dartmoor left a calling card on the moor. Why,
who knows with those crazy Brits. But others found
it, decided it was a good idea, turned it into a game,
decided to start using stamps instead (maybe because
calling cards went out of style?), and then this funny
little game they invented came to the US, and we're
playing it in the US, where we made more changes so
the game could survive here. Dartmoor letterboxing
and US letterboxing are not the same thing, as people
have often pointed out on the big list (ie, the
majority of clues are readily available for us rather
than having to scour the moor hoping to stumble upon
100 boxes so that we can finally get a list of where
those 100 boxes were placed). This is not a bad
thing. McDonalds in Costa Rica is not the same thing
as McDonalds in the US -- Letterboxing ain't the only
thing that's been changed once imported.

So, since we're playing in the US, if one wants to go
by the "inception of the game" and "how it was truly
meant to be played," check out the history of how the
game started over here, since this is where we're
playing it and it's not played the same way as over
there. Bring back the calling cards? How can we
bring back something that was never a part of the game
we're playing on this side of the ocean?

Actually, I just had a thought. If anyone's really
concerned about the history of the game and how it's
gotten all fuddled, contact Louise Donovan (see post
42476 of the Big List's archives). If anyone knows
everything there is to know about letterboxing, it's
got to be the gal doing her dissertation on it.
