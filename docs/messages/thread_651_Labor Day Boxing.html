Labor Day Boxing

From: Timbertoes (toomey@genevaonline.com) | Date: 2004-09-07 16:41:17 UTC
My Labor Day boxing experience consisted of checking on my boxes on
Friday late afternoon. My sister, who is quite new to letterboxing,
went with and I had her work out the clues. It was fun doing this
with her because we never did a whole lot together (she's 9 years
older than me with four brothers in between us) and she's not a big
fan of the outdoors. By the time we got to the last box it was dark
and I had her searching around this tree while I held the
flashlight. She says to me "Well, I've really got something to write
about in my journal tonight - I never expected to be doing this!" We
had a nice time chatting on the way back and listening to night
sounds. One of my stamps had broken in half so I took it along with,
repaired it that night (including mounting it on wood - that rubber
cement does work well when you let it dry first!) and replaced it
early the next morning.

Didn't get out the rest of the weekend, but now that the kids are
back in school I have two days off work during the week that I can
box on my own (or clean house, run errands, do laundry, etc. if I
HAVE too!). There are lots of Milwaukee-area boxes that I haven't
done yet that will make good day trips. It's a good time of year,
too, since there aren't many bugs or people out (at least on the
weekdays).

Do many of you letterbox in the winter months?

Timbertoes


Winter LBing (was Labor Day Boxing)

From: lesann847 (l-fischer@northwestern.edu) | Date: 2004-09-07 19:44:47 UTC
I love boxing in the autumn, and I love the absolute solitude of boxing in the
winter. I had a lovely time searching for Weapons of Math Instruction in an
empty Pike Lake SP last winter. Some delightful hiker with a sense of whimsy
had decorated the conifers with Christmas ornaments. I saw lots of wildlife.

Just a couple of problems with winter boxing--last February, it was about 10
degrees, and with a good snow cover when I tried to get The Dragon's Edgar
Allen Hippoe. I found it fine, cleverly following a deer trail as if I was a
photographer trying to snap a picture, but I couldn't pry the frozen box out of
its hiding place. I also found the place for The Dragon's 45-90 box, but I didn't
want to leave a trail in the deep snow--there were no other tracks for
camouflage. I didn't even attempt Rib Mountain in the winter--I'm not that
experienced a skiier ;->

I'll have to see if I can get up there before the snow flies and the temperatures
drop.

Acorn

Re: Winter LBing (was Labor Day Boxing)

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-09-07 19:54:39 UTC
I agree -- letterboxing on a sunny winter day can be serene. Two
years ago I wasn't working during the winter months and was fortunate
enough to be able to get out often. One of my very favorite
recollections is a sunny winter afternoon at Saugatuck Dunes State
Park. Back in the protection of the trees, behind the dunes, the
wind was minimal and the sunshine felt so good as the snow crunched
underfoot.

Different story once I broke out of the trees and over the sand
dunes -- the full wrath of old man winter blowing in off the lake.
My fingers nearly froze trying to stamp into the Birthday Box, but it
is still a great memory.

SpringChick

Yes, we certainly do. As Acorn mentioned, the obvious side-trails in
the snow can be a problem, but I recall a suggestion by DogScout (?)
wherein she carries a bottle of yellow food coloring and disguises
her off-trail detours as though they perhaps had another purpose. Of
course she has her dogs along for the search.
(Hey, I'm trying to be discreet!)
And appropos SpringChick's comments about the quiet and solitude of
the winter trails, I couldn't agree more. Besides, you'll have the
trails to yourself.
On the downside, landmarks take on another dimension when snow
covered. We searched at length, unsuccessfully, for a box because we
never found a certain colored boulder along the trail -- we couldn't
see the trail! But generally, I'm a big fan of winter letterboxing.

From: Kathi LK Krankoski (krank@ameritech.net) | Date: 2004-09-09 09:40:23 UTC-05:00
As some of you may have seen, I also use straight pins to "surgically
repair" stamps. By running them through the edge, it can strengthen a weak
spot. (Just be careful to go straight and not pierce the printing
surface...voice of experience!) I've spliced scraps together (ala Little
Box on the Prairie) or modified existing stamps (FOOW3) with pins (along
with the contact cement).
