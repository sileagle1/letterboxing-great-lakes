From: TJ_Mich (tj_mich@ameritech.net) | Date: 2007-08-07 12:52:37 UTC-07:00
If you don't like that carving material, or it crumbles easily, then don't use it for your signature stamp!! Why put all that time in to carve it, and create something that you want to last for a good long time, if it is just going to fall apart? You will just be frustrated. Pick up some better material that you like. Carving medium is much cheaper than your time and artistic investment.

As for transport, we cut a large "plus sign" out of felt, where the center intersection is a hair bigger than the stamp surface, and each of the 4 'legs' are big enough to fold up over the sides and across the top. Makes a good little protective pouch, but is easy to open up, even one-handed. (We do the same on our box stamps too.) Of course that still won't protect a stamp from all damage, so you might want to keep it in one of the smaller compartments of your backpack.

Happy trails!
--TJ_Mich

From: Barb Funk (mmfunk99@greenapple.com) | Date: 2007-08-07 16:08:42 UTC-04:00
Sorry to hear about the loss of your signature stamp. =(

The only stamps of mine that have broken were carved on the orange
stuff....so I don't use it anymore---especially knowing boxes in the GLK
region have to get through tough winters too. I use PZ cut only anymore
and the felt protection as well and I think it helps, too.

I don't know what your signature stamp looks like but if it is more of a
rectangle shape or has some piece of it that extends out from the
rest---that could be a more vulnerable part---and one you might not cut
as much of the "extra" away from. In other words leave some protection
around the more vulnerable part by cutting away, but not off. Having
that foam backing will help protect for sure.

I have noticed some letterboxers put their signature stamps into a small
jewelry-sized box--even lined with felt or other material. Many folks
have two or even 3 different sizes of signature stamps for then they
stamp into HH or smaller logbooks so the box works out really well for
them---and protects on top of it.

Amen to TJ Mich's comment about making the investment in the carving
medium!

From: lcoil79@ameritech.net smokey (ainawgsd@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-08-07 13:14:02 UTC-07:00
If this is your signature stamp, you should really invest in a better carving material that you would prefer working with! Our smaller stamps live in little tins (like the kind that mints come in) and that keeps them safe from being beat up by other things in our backpacks. But our team stamp is much larger and I simply wrapped it in a piece of scrap flannel and tie it with some string (actually, the "string" is also a thin strip of scrap flannel). It's held up pretty well on the trail and even survived a bout with the washing machine! Although after the round with the washer it certainly has more "character," not quite enough to need to be recarved, but enough that I don't think we'll be hand-inking it with markers anymore.

Backing can help prolong a stamp's life. Also, not carving quite so deeply. I have a tendency to carve really deeply, especially when I'm clearing out large white areas, and that will weaken the stamp more than shallow carving.

From: Coffee Beans (coffeebeans06@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-08-07 17:15:56 UTC-05:00
My advice for a good beginner carving option is Master Carve, which I've
found at JoAnn Fabrics and Hobby Lobby... I don't go to Michaels too
often so I can't remember if they carry it there. One size it comes in
are these big, thick white bricks (I think they're like 3x5" for
~$3ish). It's really soft and easy for carving with Speedball cutters
-- plus the thickness of it prevents cracking/breaking without needing
to mount the stamp on something else. I've actually done a majority of
my carvings with this and been happy with the results... both Atom and
my sig stamps are in this and they have held up (we store them in our
backpack inside a lock n lock along with our signature ink pads).
Recently, Atom and I have upgraded to PZ Cut and have used both the
orange and white grade B stuff. It's available online through
Stampeze. It's thinner and firmer, and I think you can carve more
detail with it. Good luck!

Another alternative to inking that uses the same concept if you're not that brave is plain old pencil. When I'm doing a really detailed stamp (usually using white mastercarve) sometimes I'll transfer my image and then very lightly rub pencil over the image. This makes the carving medium "dingy" and when I cut away the cut sections are brighter. It offers a little contrast.
