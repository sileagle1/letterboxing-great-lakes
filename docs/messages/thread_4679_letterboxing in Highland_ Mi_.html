Hi Heather...

I'm replying quickly and haven't looked at others' responses to
this... so excuse me if it's been covered:

If I were you I'd focus on box etiquette, secrecy, and general/basic
"rules" of letterboxing. I think these things are crucial when
educating newbies on letterboxing and in turn it (hopefully) helps
prevent vandalism and box loss.

Stick to the very basics in the class -- traditional boxes and clues,
the signature stamp, the log book, the personal log book, and carving
the stamp. That's enough to fill a class. Any mention of the
variations (micro boxes, postal letterboxing, placed & find counts,
etc.) only serves to overwhelm the new letterboxer. They can learn all
that later if they are still interested. I found this out the hard
way (giving too much information) with a group of homeschoolers whom I
held a class for recently. Trust me, their eyes start to glaze over
after awhile... you see, they just want to make a stamp and hunt down
a box! I've also explained the hobby to many-an-adult who can also
get this same overload look on their faces pretty quickly.

SO.... less is more when teaching a class, and again I'd preach about
HOW to correctly letterbox so as to keep it the fun but covert
activity we've all grown to love.

IMHO,
PennyPenny
