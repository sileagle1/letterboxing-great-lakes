Message to MN boxers (and others who place boxes in MN)

From: azobox (jparkerg@yahoo.com) | Date: 2008-06-30 18:08:42 UTC
The following msg was received at LbNA:

From: Ed Quinn, Division Resource Mgmt Program Coordinator:

Message:
It has come to our attention that letterboxes are being placed in
Minnesota state parks without prior approval of the park manager.
Would you please advise users of your site that a permit is required
to place a letterbox on land administered by the MNDNR Division of
Parks and Recreation. The permit process is important for both the
division and the person responsible for the letterbox. It insures
that the letterbox is placed in a safe location for users, and ensures
that natural/cultural resources are not impacted. In addition, we can
help the individual place the letterbox in a place where it won't be
destroyed during management actions such as prescribed burning and let
them know when to disable the site during periods when it is not
appropriate for people to visit the letterbox, such as when the park
is holding a deer hunt. Letterboxes without permits are classified as
abandoned property by MN State law and may be removed by division
staff. Thank-you for your assistance.

From: Ed Quinn, Division Resource Mgmt Program Coordinator, E-mail:
ed.quinn@dnr.state.mn.us

From: be_a_badger (schuppner@uwalumni.com) | Date: 2008-06-30 23:01:12 UTC
True Story - Once upon a time, some unsuspecting letterboxers planted
a box in a park in a neighboring state. Unbeknownst to the
letterboxers at the time of planting, said park has a geocaching
policy that specifically applies to letterboxing.

Time passed and things changed as they often do.

One day, the letterboxers received a Contact the Placer note from the
park manager, informing them that the box was pulled for lack of a
permit and suggesting that the box be picked up at the park office.

The intrepid letterboxers conducted some research, located and
reviewed the park policy and responded to the park manager. The
letterbox appeared to comply with the park's policy, except for the
permit. The letterboxers had heard no cries of distress or planting
policy concerns from natives to the area. The park's policy did not
require a fee and did not contain any renewal or other onerous
requirements. See the guidelines and permit forms at
http://www.dnr.state.mn.us/state_parks/geocaching/index.html.

A prompt reply was issued to the park manager. Were there any issues
with the location? If not, the letterboxers would be happy to
complete a permit application.

This prompted a veritable flood of information and helpfulness from
the park manager. The location seemed acceptable. Although the snag
was cut, the stump holding the original hiding place remains. Others
would be involved in the review process. Once approved, the park
manager would return the letterbox to its home - a very happy event
for the letterboxers. The park's interest in the letterbox was also
revealed. You see, while the box nestled in its snag, park staff came
along and cut the snag. Being neat and tidy workers, they were busy
picking up branches and otherwise cleaning up after themselves when
they discovered and removed the letterbox.

The letterboxers proceeded to archive their clue and mark it as
temporarily unavailable. As the park manager suggested, they plan to
adjust the clue to change the snag to a stump.

As the letterboxers continue their adventures, they look forward to
receiving an approval and confirmation that the box is living happily
ever after in its home.

Please feel free to contact us off-list if you are interested in
hearing any future installments of the story.

The Christmas Elves
