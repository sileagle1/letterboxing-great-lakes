Egads! What are we to do?

From: tiptoetonto (tiptoetonto@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-05-20 04:14:55 UTC
To those of you that lurk (like we do)or participate on the Big list,
you know that Tonto and I are in a little bit of a bind. We aren't
sure what to do...We planted a box on April 23 called "Cicada Mania"
in honor of the 17 year cicada invasion soon to strike our area. We
have received a steady flow of "contact the placer" remarks on the
box..all very complimentery with no problems mentioned about the
clues or the area. We were very excited about all the "hits" it was
getting until a few days ago when we received an email from a first
time letterboxer frustrated that she couldn't find the box due to the
clues being too confusing and vague, and she gave up after searching
for 45 minutes. And if that wasn't bad enough, upon her retreat back
to the parking area she found that her brand new car had been broken
into and her purse had been stolen!

She emailed us to let out her frustration and to suggest that we
change the clues giving more exact directions and that we should warn
everyone to consider that this is a dangerous area before attempting
this box. Tonto has lived in this area all of his 58 years, and we
feel that Orland Park is by no means any less safe than any other
suburban forest preserve. We wouldn't hide a box in an area knowing
it was considered more dangerous than any other! This preserve is
across the street from a middle to upper income residencial area and
very well used for biking, hiking, walking etc.

We responded back that we were very sorry that this unfortunate
situation had happened to her. We mentioned that as letterboxers we
all take a risk going into ANY public place and cars are as likely to
get broken into in a shopping mall parking lot, a grocery store.. you
name it.. as much as a forest preserve. We are vulnerable in ALL
public places. We said we would consider placing a reminder in all
of our boxes that prudence and vigilience needs to be exercised when
letterboxing, but we weren't comfortable on placing a warning for
this box alone.

Then tonight, I read the daily digest from the Big list..to our
horror she brought this up on the big list under the
subject.."BEWARE: Northern Illinois" Yikes! It was bad enough to
have your clues disected and critiqued (We did get a chuckle about
the grammar and punctuation comments!) But, the line was drawn when
she actually posted that her husband thought we had set her up to be
a victim! The post suggested that we were laying in wait to break
into her car while she was out looking for our box! I feel sick to
my stomach as I write this...If I wasn't so upset, I'd be
hysterically laughing at the thought of "Teacher" Tiptoe & "Elder"
Tonto living the life of Bonnie & Clyde...But we aren't laughing! We
are really upset by this.

At first, we took it all with a grain of salt knowing that this was
her very first attempt at a box..
Others can defend us by pointing out the insanity of thinking that we
would put out clues, and three weeks later still be hanging out at
the parking lot waiting to commit a crime! (BTW, we do have
alibi's..I've been up to my eyeballs with my Fine Arts Night coming
next week!...You know what I mean Leapin' Lizard and Mrs. Cottontail!)
It's almost comical...But seriously, think of the potential
ramifications of these accusations not only for us, but for all of us
that love this hobby! Not so funny.

There was some discussion questioning the safety of all the Cook
County Forest Preserves..this covers a lot of boxes and boxers!

The situation gets more complicated when on the 10 o'clock news
tonight the lead story was of a murder which happened today in a
Glenview forest preserve(a northern suburb of Chicago and part of the
vast Cook County FP system. The man had apparently been hiking to
keep fit.

All of this brings up many questions...Any thoughts to help us muddle
through this? Those that have actually found this box could you
weigh in?..Those that live in the Chicagoland area and have boxes in
CCFP...Anybody?
Tiptoe...and Tonto who is shaking his head...

Absolutely not. I'd also like to say when I started letterboxing and
was reading through pages and pages of information, there was a very
good piece written about letterbox safety. One of the things it
touched upon was leaving valuables out in the open in your vehicle at
trailheads. I'm sure as a placer you feel bad for her (I know I
would) but in no way should you feel responsible. As far as the
safety of the game preserves I can't address that since I'm not from
your area. But I will say I have a beautiful state park near me.
When I'm there I feel completely safe. However, crime does still
happen there. Over the years there have been a few bodies turn up,
and some other incidents. Do I feel this makes the park unsafe, No I
don't. Let's face it, anyone doing something illegal no matter what
state you are in, is going to look for a secluded, remote area to do
it. Being aware of that doesn't make me afraid; it just makes me a
smarter boxer. 1. I remove anything that may be tempting to a thief
out of view in my car. Right down to CD's and change. 2. I lock my
doors. 3. I carry pepper spay 4. I do not box alone 5. I make
sure I'm finished well before dark. I felt that she had newbie
frustration to begin with combined with a terrible experience, and
took her frustration out towards the only target she could, the
placer. Had she found the box and continued boxing for another six
to eight months and THEN had her car broken into she would have never
written that post that way. She would have looked at it as what it
was, a random act of crime. ~The Cats Meow~

From: xxxxxxxx (BrighidFarm@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-05-20 08:14:07 UTC-05:00
The sales prices of homes in the area often have absolutely nothing to do
with crime in the forest preserves in the area. Unfortunately. People who
commit crimes have cars -- many of them might not have driver's licenses and
many of them don't have insurance, but that doesn't stop an awfully lot of
them from driving cars. As far as "type" of crime goes, if they were going
to break into a car for material possessions, it makes more sense to do it
in fancy neighborhoods since it's unlikely that the fancy people are going
to come into the poverty stricken neighborhoods to hike or bike in the
forest preserves. Particularly if you know that the police force and ranger
force are spread thin as it is in patrolling the forest preserves. They
might be a lot less apt to attack you or your car in your driveway but in a
forest preserve with few other folks around and the cops far outside quick
response time? Sure. Why break into a car of someone you know probably has
as much or less money than you do? If I was poor and inclined to break into
cars to supplement my income, I'd be heading for where I thought there might
be something a little better to steal.

And keep in mind, an awfully lot of homeless people take up housekeeping in
the forest preserves, once the weather is nice enough that they can survive.
Far safer for them to take up housekeeping in woods that are in nicer areas,
since they also risk getting attacked in the woods. They have no money, no
food, they're hungry, they might want to buy drugs, there's a nice car
sitting there that just might have some change or credit cards in it, they
just might break into it if the timing is right.

And look at the attack that just happened up at Camp Pine Forest Preserve
this past week on a walking trail -- three men attacked, one died. One of
the men was 67, one was 77, I'm not sure of the age of the other one, and it
happened about 2pm in the afternoon. Three elderly, retired gentlemen out
for an afternoon walk. This was not a bad neighborhood. So far the police
haven't given a reason but I can't think of too many reasons that a 20-25
year old male would actually *be* justified in killing one elderly man and
injuring two others.

Cook County forest preserves have plenty of crime. Crime in all of them has
been on the increase and, like the police told that letterboxer, it's been
kinda on *more* of an increase in the southern sections of Cook County.
That's why I *haven't* yet gone down for the boxes in the Orland Park,
Palos, Palos Heights area yet that are in the preserves. We found "Shake!
Shake! Shake!" a couple of years ago, and that's not in a secluded area
anyway, so yes, that one I'd still probably go back for today if I hadn't
found it already as long as I went when there were also plenty of cars there
from people fishing and such. But with the others, I haven't been able to
combine my free time with a time of day that I'll feel OK parking at those
preserves.

But any forest preserve in a major metropolitan area is going to have plenty
of problems with their forest preserves if they have any to speak of.

Unless I'm going searching for a letterbox, the only places I go anymore in
the Cook County preserves are the places right out in the open unless it's
plenty crowded. And even at that, I check out the nature of the crowd.
I've hiked the Cook County forest preserves for over 50 years and for quite
a few years now, the only area I've been in where I've felt "safe" was the
Salt Creek Bicycle Trail and Wolf Prairie. If I'm just hiking and not
letterboxing, my local hiking is now done strictly in DuPage, Kane, and
DeKalb preserves.

Keep in mind that this lady and her husband don't know any of the local
letterboxers at all at this point. She doesn't know who might be "well
known" and who isn't. This was her first letterboxing experience. And they
might not have been thinking of the fact that there would be no need for any
criminal mind to post fake clues just to draw people. Enough people to rob
are already drawn to the preserves. This might not only have been her first
letterboxing experience, but she also might not have a ton of experience
with the preserves. She went with a group of other adults in a brand new
car. The first thing I would've done was to take the car of whichever adult
had the worst looking car in the group. And leave all credit cards at home
except for one which would stay right on my person.

Do I think that means folks should stop placing boxes in Cook County
preserves? Nope. It's just unfortunately another area where, while the
media has been building up and popularizing this pasttime, they've fallen
down on the job as far as discussing both discretion and safety. Even on
the rare occasions that discretion is discussed at all, I've yet to see any
TV blurbs or magazine/newspaper articles that stress safety. Forest
preserves present a fantastic opportunity for crime. Too many people don't
realize that. Or they don't think *they're* going to be the victim. But
*some*body has to be the victim.

Even in seemingly safe areas. Like I posted on the big list, my scariest
situation to date was out in Kane County at Johnson's Mound.............and
up to that point, I'd hiked Kane County trails for half a century and felt
as safe as a person could feel in the woods. Now, the guy *could* have
broken into my car. I was the only car parked up there that morning. But
I'm pretty convinced that he was actually after *me*, not simple theft,
which gave me the chance to get out of there because I had seen *him* but he
hadn't yet seen *me*. But I also think that *could've* been letterbox
oriented, altho I'm absolutely positive the box wasn't planted with that in
mind.

When I got to the top of the Mound and parked, there were 4 different trails
I had to pick from. Two of them didn't fit the clues at all, so I could
discard those trails right off the bat. The other two, I wasn't really
sure. So I decided to just pick one and start down it and see if the rest
of the clues started to fit at all. After about 10 minutes, I knew I was on
the wrong trail, so I doubled back to the parking lot to take the other
trail.

The woods came right up to the edge of the parking lot. Before leaving the
woods and walking out into the parking lot I stopped because I saw a beat-up
old pickup truck parked next to my van and no other vehicles there but our
two. So I just hung back to see what the situation might be. Very slowly
circling my van was a slimy, greasy guy -- looked like he hadn't a bath in
at least a month or two -- maybe about 25 years old. I stayed well back in
the woods, and the clothes I always wear for boxing help me blend in well
with the woods, just watching him.

The guy circled my van about 3 times peering in the windows. Then he
glanced over at the trail I *should* have taken to find the letterbox.
Walked toward that trail. Got to the edge of the parking lot and looked
back at my van and just stood there a bit looking at my van. Then glanced
down the trail and stood looking down the trail a bit. Looked back at my
van. Then turned and started down that trail at a fast walk.

By that time, I had gotten my keys out of my pocket with my door/ignition
key ready to go. Ran over to my car, hopped in, and took off on outta
there.

Now why did the guy pick the trail I *should* have taken to the letterbox to
go down himself? There were four trails he could've picked from.
Coincidence? Quite possibly. But maybe not. He might have known that the
letterbox was down there and decided to take advantage of the fact that I
might be down there and that, except for his truck and my van, the place was
completely deserted. And that's one area where a person could scream and
scream and scream and never be heard if there wasn't someone already in the
area. He couldn't have known that I'd be alone, but in his mind he could've
been thinkin' hey, let's check it out and see. Why not just wait for me to
come back to my van? Possibly because he felt fairly secure that I *was*
down that particular trail, and by following me, there would be even less
risk of meeting other people that might be driving up to the parking lot
that morning.

I'll never know for sure, but my gut feeling at the time told me that the
guy was dangerous and that I'd been very very lucky to have seen him before
he saw me. But the reeeeally stupid thing was that my gut had told me when
I first started the long, winding drive up the Mount to the top, that I
should turn around and not try for that box. And I just ignored it. I
couldn't "see" any danger at that point. So *if* God was protecting me
early that morning, He/She had actually protected me twice. And at least I
listened the second time around. :-)

I never went back to search for the box. Tho I probably could have and
probably would have been perfectly safe the second time around. I'm sure
many many people found that box with absolutely no suspicious happenings at
all. But crime, even if it's not frequent, is going to have a victim, and
*some*body has to be that victim. Possibly I could've turned out to be a
victim that day if the timing hadn't been perfect -- perfect for *me*.

Atom 118 and Coffee Beans,

((Advisory v. Warning))

I can't help but go back to a comment I made about giving a suggestion
that several boxes of mine are placed in an area where I suggest or
advise taking some water because of the steep terrain. Do I do that
with all of my boxes? No. But since I have knowledge that I need water
almost every time I hike that trail, I think it's important to share
that insight with others. Every box I plant, I try to do so as if the
seeker is OUT OF STATE and not familiar with the area. I think we owe
that much to each other.

I also used to think it went without saying that we should always all
"be prepared" by taking water, an ACE bandage, matches etc., but as I
watched SpringChick point out specifics of exactly how to be prepared in
one of her responses, I was suprised at how many folks came back
thanking her for the advice and insight of having a charged cell phone
when hiking, for example. It never occured to me that someone would NOT
take their cell pone but very often what is "obvious to one" is not to
another. I've watched someone else make a comment on this board that
was obvious to them---and I sat here silently taking strong note of new
information too.

So, I think since these incidents have now occured, it might be a good
idea to add a note to be alert, lock your valuables out of sight and not
hike for this box or these boxes alone. It still ends up being the
individual's decision. I may never go look for these boxes you are
talking about because I am not from Chicago and am definitely not
familiar with the area, but if there was a similar "advisory" for a box
in Columbus, I know it wouldn't stop me from going alone---only because
I'm close to home, I know the area and where the hospitals are in an
emergency. I also know where my cell phone works and where it doesn't
here. I'm sure that shows in my confidence level while I am hiking too.

When my dog got bit by the copperhead, I was 100% out of my confort zone
because we were in a Nature Preserve in NC and I had NO CLUE where an
emergency animal clinic was, for example. It was pure luck or Divine
Guidance that someone I didn't even know called my cell phone
number....and ended up "helping me land the plane" over the phone by
researching for me on the internet where I should take "Gunner"!! Now
what is "obvious" to me to do in an emergency like this (use a lifeline
and call someone to do research on the computer while you are on the
phone) is something I am going to share with others.

My vote is to simply add an advisory.
