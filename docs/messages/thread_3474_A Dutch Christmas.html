A Dutch Christmas

From: cdem_ltrbox (wp4@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2006-12-20 05:11:07 UTC
Every year for Christmas, we explore how a different country celebrates
the holiday. This year we picked Holland--and it sounds like they must
be letterboxers at heart. Some of their gifts are wrapped as surprises
and are disguised as much as possible (small gifts hidden in a
vegetable or a pudding perhaps)--not that far from stamps hidden in
camo-taped boxes. Other gifts are hidden, and the recipient has to
decipher poems and riddles to find the gift. I think we've done that
more than a few times!! Now I have our Dutch Christmas as an excuse to
letterbox.

BirdWoman/Waltzing Pigs

Yes, but there is a very excellent Dutch cookbook called Eet Smakelijk (meaning to eat well and with taste), put out by the Holland Junior Welfare League, that has just all the best Dutch food recipes outside of the Netherlands. Lest we forget... Banket is not the only culinary delite contributed by the Dutch... there is Dutch Apple Pie, otherwise known as Appletart (much better than traditional American Apple Pie), all kinds of smoked meats and fish, Gouda Cheese, Erwtensoep (Pea Soup), Stroopwafel (waffle cookies with a caramel syrup filling), Krakelingen (butter cookies shaped like an 8 with sugar on top), Jan Hagel (thin, flaky almond cookies), Oliebollen (literally, "oil balls" -- powdered sugar covered Dutch donuts, traditionally served on New Year's Eve), real licorice, and the most awesome delicious hard candy there is -- Zeeuwse Babbelaars (literally interpretted as "Dutch candy").

And who else do you know that put chocolate sprinkles on their bread for breakfast?

SpringChick

Apparently the Spanish love Nutella too. I was served breakfast that
included toasted bread with a thin layer of nutella. The spread melted
and it was delicious. I can't say I'd want it regularly, but a treat once
in a while isn't bad. I think I'd sprinkle some dried cranberries on it
to add some more flavor.

I cannot bring myself to put mayonnaise on fries. For me, mayo goes in
egg salad and other COLD dishes, never hot. The thought of that makes my
stomach flipflop. Ironically, some Europeans thought putting ketchup on
ANYTHING was odd (daresay, barbaric) some 20 years ago. I suspect the
invasion of McDonald's and others has changed the perception a little for
the younger generation. In addition to the mayo sauce for pommes frites,
I think they have varieties, like mayo with curries, mayo with herbs. I
haven't tried them, but how many Americans eat buffalo wings with ranch
dip?

My brother studied in Melbourne, Australia and he said they referred to
ketchup as just plain "sauce."

Our Spanish exchange student could not understand how we could eat
corn-on-the-cob. From his perspective, corn was used only to feed
livestock. We did get him to try it. Never did know if he enjoyed it or
was trying to be a good sport.

Just my ramblings before the big feast,
