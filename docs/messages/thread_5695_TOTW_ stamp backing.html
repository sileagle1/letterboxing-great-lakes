This weekend, we thwarted off the cold temps, packed up Kemosabe and found a few boxes.
In so doing we were commenting on the different ways of mounting stamps. We even came
to one that seemed to be mounted to a chunk of rough sawn wood, basically almost bark! It
worked fine. We also came across several types of foam backing that worked very nicely. We
are curious what others think of backings on stamp and what in the world do you use to
adhere the stamp to the backing material.

RE: [LbGLK] TOTW: stamp backing

From: Debbie Kotlarek (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2009-03-02 10:49:58 UTC-06:00
We've found a lot of stamps and based on our personal experience, our
preference is..... NO backing! We can get the best image when stamping if
we can press directly on the carving material. We often end up with blank
spots on larger stamps that are backed with wood (and then need to try
pressing the page to the stamp instead of vice versa). Even acrylic and
foam backings have oftentimes impacted the quality of the image we can get.
We used various backings on some of the first stamps we carved, but based on
what we experienced in finding stamps, we quit using any backing a long time
ago.

Wisconsin Hiker

From: Katrina Lustofin (drkaty@gmail.com) | Date: 2009-03-02 12:44:14 UTC-05:00
I've backed my personal stamp with wood and it's held up well but it lives
in a tin in my fanny back, protected from temperature extremes and wetness
and wild animals. I'm with WiHi in this regards - for stamps in boxes I
prefer no backing. Even the foam backing can negatively influence the image
quality. I often find myself having to push on the paper to get a good
image if the stamp is backed. Besides, if I really think the stamp is too
flimsy, I can always use my stamp tin or the box itself to help with the
pressing.

Katy

Re: TOTW: stamp backing

From: dartmoor_dreamer (school@blees.com) | Date: 2009-03-03 13:49:13 UTC
In Minne-snow-ta planted stamps with a plywood backing will eventually
be covered with mold or will warp. If stamps are not backed at all,
they crack and break after a freeze-thaw-freeze-boil cycle or two.

I prefer stamps that are backed with 1/4 inch foam. The foam keeps the
stamp from breaking. Mold doesn't seem to find anything to eat. The
added thickness of the foam gives your hands something to grab and the
stamp is still flexible enough to stamp a nice image. And best of all;
the foam-backed stamps survive the local temperature extremes.

For adhering, I use Krazy Glue with a brush applicator.

-Dartmoor Dreamer

Re: TOTW: stamp backing

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2009-03-03 18:06:41 UTC
I have not had a problem with unbacked stamps cracking either in the
extremes of cold and hot in Michigan. When I do back with wood, I
prefer to use hardwood blocks (i.e. oak) which I have had issues with
only when they were completely submerged in water for long periods of
time. Plywood does tend to suck in water and therefore the potential
for mold.

As Wisconsin Hiker, I too prefer unbacked stamps as they take less
room in the box and I find it easier to get a good stamp impression.

SpringChick

From: Carol Osada (cjosada@hotmail.com) | Date: 2009-03-03 14:03:34 UTC-06:00

If any water gets inside a letterbox, that will damage the logbook, but an unmounted rubber stamp can be dried off and used again. So I don't tend to mount my stamps. Some types of wood and foam could absorb water and cause the stamp to get moldy. yucko!



Boxes will crack if they are frozen to the ground and pulled out of their locations. Or, if a frozen box is accidentally dropped it will crack. A good reason to bring camo duct tape along for box repairs.



What would make a stamp crack? I'm not sure the answer is the weather. There are some very old boxes out there and the stamps are fine. But I did have one stamp crack...it was an indoor box. So, sadly, a cracked stamp might simply be an accident that occures when someone bends the rubber, plays with it, and then....crack!



One of the good reasons to be part of this yahoo group is that we all learn from the experience of others.



Here's hoping that all the boxes survived this winter with the unusual amount of ice and snow.



Beetle
