Me too. I'm a Speedball Girl. I mostly use my crimped-down #1 blade, occasionally regular #1 or #2. I'll use a scalpel to trim off edges.

I prefer the white PZKut (that orange is way too harsh!!! Plus I like the consistancy of the white better.) I exclusively do burnished pencil or inkjet transfers. But what I've also found to help visualize my carvings is to take a light marker (yellow, beige, etc) and color the slab after transferring but before I start carving. Then when I cut, I see the white where the surface has been removed.

From: lcoil79@ameritech.net smokey (ainawgsd@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-10-28 06:48:35 UTC-07:00
Me too, me too! I haven't tried the exacto knife yet (I did buy a nice set, but promptly misplaced it and haven't seen it in over a year). But I am very happy with the results I get from a speedball cutter. If I could only have one blade, it would be the #1. If I could only have two blades, it would probably be the #1 and the U-shaped gouge (I think it's a #5). Mostly I use the #1 blade and a pinched #1 for fine details. For clearing larger areas within the stamp design I might use the #2, or if it is a particularly large stamp even the #3. And I LOVE the U-shaped gouge and the #3 for clearing large areas around the design...it goes SO much quicker than the #1 or #2.

I'm very happy with the amount of detail I can get with the pinched #1. I'm sure I can't get as much detail as some of our other excellent carvers, but then again I'm just not that patient and I rarely have that long an attention span. I need to sit down and carve a stamp all at once...it becomes an obsession that I can't let go of until it is finished. If a stamp takes me much longer than an hour or so to carve I start to get frustrated and even loose interest.

Kimono Girl
