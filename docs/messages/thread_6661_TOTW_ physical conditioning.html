TOTW: physical conditioning

From: tiptoetonto (tiptoetonto@yahoo.com) | Date: 2010-06-21 16:09:53 UTC
We spent a fabulous weekend trying to get through the Wizard of Oz series along the Hennepin Canal with Wisconsin Hiker and Martini Man. This required bikes for the most part, unless you have time to hike 75 miles. The truth be told, I (Tiptoe) hadn't been on a bike in a very long time. This proved to be a real challenge to my physical limitations. I feel I can hike fairly long distances in a day without hitting a wall. But the biking put me into the wimp category after just a few miles.

The discussion came up about biking vs. hiking and there physical demands. I feel stronger at hiking (and chose to hike on this trip the last day) as opposed to biking. Others felt that they were the same as far as muscle and stamina requirements. Just to fulfill my curiosity, what do you think about the physical shape a letterboxer needs to be in before hitting the trails, and is hiking and biking the same for some of you.

For the record, we passed a horse and rider on the trail (the human of the duo picking black raspberries), and that seemed the perfect way to get through this OZ series...Trailrider, this series was made for you and any other horse-boxers!
Tiptoe....and Tonto

From: Debbie Kotlarek (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2010-06-21 11:26:23 UTC-05:00
On a flat bike trail like the Hennepin, it is much easier for me to bike.
Each push of my foot takes me a greater distance than if I was simply
walking, I get a breeze from moving faster and there is no "impact", since
my feet are on the pedals. If I bike a long distance (long for me would be
over 25-30 miles) without getting off much, my butt might be sore the next
day from the bike seat. However on the "yellow brick road" I was getting
off my bike every 1-3 miles, so no problem. Granted, it was much easier to
bike on the hard surface side of the canal, but even on the bumpy grassy
side, we definitely made better time biking than hiking.



Now if we would be talking about a HILLY or MOUNTAIN area, I would
definitely prefer to be hiking, not biking!



Wisconsin Hiker

From: Kathryn Aggen (tiptoetonto@yahoo.com) | Date: 2010-06-21 14:00:09 UTC-07:00
I'm not a horse person, so I'm not sure about all of the requirements a horse needs on a long trail.  One problem that I could foresee is that there are quite a few crossings requiring one to get off a bike and go through tunnels that are only about 7 feet in diameter and 20 to 30 feet long. (I posted a picture of one in the picture section)  Im not sure that would spook a horse or not.  They would certainly do well on the grassy path clues, as those were more horse friendly and indeed where we came across the woman on her horse.  I wish I had asked her more questions regarding horses on the canal, but we were so anxious for her to be on her merry way so we could look for a nearby box, that I didn't want to engage her any more than a friendly "hello". Also water on the trail is hard to come by and the overgrowth along the canal and steep bank might be hard for a horse to get near that source...however, we did see a big golden retriever jump in and
out at one of the day use areas.  Seeing that they have specific equestrian trails, the ranger at the visitor center might be able to give you answers to all of your questions.I did laugh at how in the past I thought boxing by horse seemed so difficult, but I can't tell you how many times I thought..."Now, this is more suited for a horse, than me on a bike"!  I was even wishing for a golf cart, but the road and bridge crossings wouldn't work too well!  You would definitely need someone else with you, as many of the boxes are located off the trail and require more scrambling to the boxes than we all had expected.  Jim was teasing me that I must have thought it was going to be a straight shot with the boxes placed right on the trail ready to stamp in and then go on.  It was all I could do to just get to the box, let alone then go scrambling in the thickets to find the darn box, stamp in, and return the box.  Took a LOT more time and energy than I
had planned.  All I can say is, thank heavens we had Martini Man and Tonto fetching most of the boxes for us.  Nonetheless, Wisconsin Hiker and I both managed enough scratches and she poison sumac to prove we did go in and find some!Hope you get to go and at least snag a few boxes!Kathryn (TIptoe)
