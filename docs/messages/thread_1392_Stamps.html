--- pkleingers wrote:

> My carving skills are rudimentary at best-but the
> only way they improve is
> to keep doing it-really! And I personally prefer a
> simple hand carved to
> most commercial.

Absolutely! Stamp carving at first seems completely
impossible. I NEVER thought I would be able to do it,
ever. But me being the kind of person that wants to do
things the "preferred" way, I knew that I would have
to carve my own stamp before I could allow myself to
even begin letterboxing. So I bought the little
Speedball kit at Michael's, held my breath....drew a
little armadillo on the medium and carved it out. I
was completely *amazed* on how well it turned out,
considering that during the carving process it pretty
much looks like crap. The key is to not get too
complicated on your first few stamps. Choose small
objects without a lot of detail. Practice....and it
gets easier. I find that drawing on the medium with a
Sharpie helped me define lines, light/dark better.
Every stamp I carve now, I try something different.
For the replacement stamp for the Seven Pillars, I
tried teeny tiny letters. Shockingly, they didn't come
out half bad!

You can do it!

--Armadillo Jo

From: azobox (jparkerg@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-06-08 01:25:04 UTC
As I mentioned on this list a week or so ago, I had the pleasure of
finding that cool little armadillo stamp in one of my boxes and was
thrilled. So, don't be fearful, give it a try.
My first box had the absolutly most primitive stamp possible 'cause
that's all I knew how to do. I've advanced a little since then but
when I had to replace that box's stamp, I deliberatley recarved a
primitive stamp. There is a charm to those stamps.
We've all had the first-stamp experience..... and lived to carve a
second and a third or more.

As SpringChick mentioned in a workshop or two, knowing when to quit is
the most difficult lesson.

azobox

From: Armadillo Jo (jes7o@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-06-08 05:50:43 UTC-07:00
The maroon handle comes with the carving tools set,
which has all the different numbered blades included.
There's a Speedball Speedy-stamp beginners set that
comes with a 4x6 sheet of the pink stuff, a wooden
carving handle and two random blades. You'll need the
#1 blade for stamp carving. Either choice will get you
on the path to carving. If you have trouble finding
what you need at Michael's, try
http://www.stampeaz.com They have a variety of carving
supplies and are the purveyors of the holy grail of
carving materials, PZ Kut. Good luck!

--Armadillo Jo

I second the "Yay!" Although we didn't really care that much about the
stamps initially (we really like the CLUES part), we have developed a
definite preference for hand-carved stamps. SO much so that if faced with a
decision on what boxes to search for in a limited time, we will always
choose those with hand=carved stamps. The first 3 boxes we hid were
custom-ordered stamps (similar to what I've heard is common in Dartmoor and
I think what Matt the Rat does), but then we got brave enough to start
carving. Practice (and a great lighted magnifying lens!) have helped us
improve our skills. It was not as hard as I thought it would be!

That is great -- glad to hear you tried it and are addicted! Looking forward to seeing your gathering box and hopefully making it out to that side of the state later this summer to see some of your other work!

Letters are hard to get a uniform look. They are my least favorite part of a stamp. A lot of people just don't put the box name on the stamp because they don't want to carve letters. I used to always put the box name, even though the letters were crummy, but lately I have been excluding the box name on some where the stamp needs to be smaller to fit into a smaller box.

All I can say about the letters -- it gets better with time and experience. A few things I have found helpful --

- Use Sans Serif fonts -- the ones without the little tails, legs and feet on the letters (Times Roman is a Serif font, whereas Arial is Sans Serif -- i.e. "without" serif).
- Carve out the small inside spaces first, like the insides of the letters o, a, e, d, b, p, etc. It is difficult to get these once you've taken away the support from around the letter itself.
- Make small sectioned cuts -- don't try to do around the whole outside of a letter in one stroke, rather first make a cut along the bottom of a letter, then up the right side, then across the top, then down the other side.
- When doing circular cuts, for instance around an "O", keep the tool steady and instead of moving the tool along the letter outline, turn the piece of carving material -- this seems to give a much smoother cut.
- Make the letters larger.
- I always carve the letters on my stamp first because this is the most difficult for me. This way if I mess up and have to start the stamp over, I haven't spent tons of time on the image already.

Good luck!

SpringChick
