TOTW - (Early) What Excites You...

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-05-15 15:25:20 UTC
I am posting the next TOTW a bit early for a few reasons -- last
week's topic seems to have gotten stale, I will be really busy with
other things next week, and just because I am longing for some
positive, productive letterboxing discussion to remind us all of the
reason we love to do this ;-)

This week's topic: What really excites you about a letterbox?

We all have some boxes that stick out in our mind as our favorites
or those "must finds" and we all get a rush from different things.
I am not necessarily looking for names of specific letterboxes --
this is not a popularity contest, but if you want to mention one in
example, it certainly is allowable. So... are you tickled by a
really artful stamp? A devious clue? Being the first-finder? The
thrill of tracking down boxes by certain people? An awesome hike or
location? A good mystery? What makes you look back and say "now that
was an awesome letterbox?"

SpringChick

From: Delirium (donaldson_home@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-05-15 12:18:05 UTC-07:00

My favorite box- or rather- BOXES are "The Mighty Mac" and "Top O' The Pickle" both in St. Ignace.

Why? Because the location. One of the reasons I love letterboxing so much is because it really highlights those hidden gems a city has to offer. I'm mentioned how pretty the parks in Rochester Hills and Kalamazoo are, but for sheer breathtaking-ness, I have to pick those two.

Mighty Mac I visited with my husband, daughter, neice, sister and brother-in-law. The view from the overlook showed the bridge in all its steely wonder (sorry, but it really is a sight that carries you away). In fact, a picture taken of the three of us there is the only photo my husband keeps on his desk at work. But even beyond the man-made views, when we visted in June, we were overwhelmed by the wildflowers. Trillium! Marsh Poppy! Lady Slippers! I could swoon with delight.

Top O' The Pickle was another great one, because it is literally hidden in a road side rest stop that spits you out right on the beaches of Lake Michigan. After the box was found (not so exciting for a one-year-old), there is the added bonus of sand and surf.

So there it is. While I always love finding a goregous stamp, it's the parks that really do it for me. It lets me look at cities and see their beauty instead of the sprawl.

Delirium

From: Kathi LK Krankoski (krank@ameritech.net) | Date: 2004-05-17 00:22:57 UTC-05:00
Just plain being able to FIND one is exciting! I remember the first box I
found (Deb Rock's Bass Ponds)...the excitement of after reading up on the
topic for a while, preparing my journal, printing out clues, getting
directions from the hotel shuttle driver to find the area, and finally
actually getting out on the trail. First landmark...Yes! I'm on my way!
Next one...next one...yowsa!!! I found it!!!! No longer a crazy abstract
concept...I'm now a LETTERBOXER!

I'm disappointed that the two boxes nearest my house (other than my
self-placed!) are MIA. My free time is limited(as anyone with a
too-young-to-drive teenaged daughter in the house will attest) that I can't
pick up and drive far to find more distant boxes. I'm doing my best to hide
my own...which brings me to the other thrill...going back to a placed box
and checking out how many people have come to find it. As said on the other
list, "If you place it, the WILL come!!" (Not as much fun as finding them,
but ya gotta do what ya gotta do...whatever works to get your kicks...)

--klk/fw

Re: TOTW - (Early) What Excites You...

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-05-18 15:29:27 UTC
I have given some thought to this one, both before and after posing
the question. There are so many things that make a letterbox stand
out in my mind... A beautiful hike -- wildflowers, beach, river,
waterfalls, old-growth forest, tropically lush greenery; interesting
and/or historical places, buildings, stories; challenging clues --
puzzles, codes, cryptic, unique, creative (recent example: Lemony
Snicket); a hand-carved stamp -- skillfully detailed or childishly
primitive; a handbound logbook; a unique container... But these are
all topical things.

What really excites me is the spirit of the game -- when I come
across another individual who by conversation, clues or letterbox
exemplifies the legacy of the game. The thing that has always
captured my attention is the way the game was played when I first
learned of it -- mystery, elusion, clues in odd places, things left
unspoken, secret souls, odd fellows and old masters, paying one's
dues -- having to earn the trust of fellow-boxers, mystery boxes
(tough ones that only a few had ever solved), unpublished clues,
secrets and surprises...

Unless you have experienced this level of play, it is difficult to
explain the rush one gets when all of the pieces of an elusive, much
sought-after box fall into place. I can compare the feeling only to
the "flow" an artist achieves when thoroughly engrossed in
creativity. Unfortunately this is not something that can be taught,
it is learned only by experience, and even then, not quickly. In the
chase for numbers, black and white answers and the quick and easy
way, many involved with the hobby today will never understand or
experience this. True, they will still have found an enjoyable hobby
of searching for little plastic containers in the woods, but that is
only a fraction of the game of letterboxing.

SpringChick
