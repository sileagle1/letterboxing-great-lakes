to camoflage or not...

From: Doodle & Deedle Bug (doodle_n_deedle@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-08-05 07:13:37 UTC-07:00
Well, Deedle and I are very close to finally planting our first box. We've got several pieces-parts of boxes lying around...a location for one, but no stamp carved yet. A stamp carved, but now we need to scout where to plant it in the park we want to plant it. Ideas and half-written clues for another box...you get the picture.

Anyway, we had decided that we wanted to camoflage our boxes, so we bought a roll of camy duct tape from Wal-Mart the other day, and I set about taping up one of our containers to see what it looked like. The rational for camoflaging this box in particular is that the woods where we want to place it are behind some houses, where we've seen kids roaming the woods before. The thought being that if there's no gleaming plastic it will be relatively safe from curious non-participants, in the event that it's dislogded from its hiding place.

Once I was done, I looked at our camy-covered container, scratched my head, and thought, "Found by the wrong person, they're going to think this is a Trenchcoat Mafia thing or something..." I mean, _we_ all know it's innocent, but to someone who's not in-the-know, with terror alerts what they are, are they going to think the worse?

So, fellow letterboxers, I ask for your opinions. Is it better to camoflage or not?

Doodle aka Diane.

Re: to camoflage or not...

From: lesann847 (l-fischer@northwestern.edu) | Date: 2004-08-05 15:02:56 UTC
Generally, I think it's better to camouflage, particularly when the
letterbox is not near an athletic stadium, major skyscraper,
suspension bridge, or other potential target. In natural areas, it's
unlikely there would be a threat, so it's less likely that someone
might jump to that conclusion.

I've seen some pretty clever camouflage in urban areas lately
where, of course, camouflage tape would defeat its own
purpose. Urban boxes tend to be microboxes, and would be
less often suspected of harboring something dangerous (this
may not be the reality, but it may be the perception if someone
comes across it).

In the woods, I always camouflage tape my boxes and when I
check on them, there's often a moment of panic when I can't spot
them right away. :-)

Acorn

From: azobox (jparkerg@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-08-05 20:52:08 UTC
I pretty much *do* camo my boxes.
I didn't used to but after seeing how well the taped or painted boxes
blended with surroundings I've gone that route.

I had to laugh when I read Acorn's and Czeltic Girl's comments about
not seeing their own boxes when checking on camouflaged placements. I
had the same experience and had that same heart-stopping
thought, "oh, it's gone", only to see the box right where I'd left
it, very well hidden.

Minis and micros practically disappear.

I was surprised to note when I purchased my camouflage tape at Gander
Mtn that there atr least three different variations (hardwood
forests, softwoods, etc -- I can't remember the specifics).
Anyway, it's fun to try to match the environment where the box is
placed.
Go for it, Doodle.

azobox

I have only camouflaged a few of our boxes so far and only the ones

I might think twice about camoflaging them for just that reason. Some folks
are so paranoid these days, and ya add some melodrama to that, and you might
have the CIA videotaping everyone who stops and visits the box. :-) And that
would be after they soaked it in water or tried to blow it up or something to
make sure it was "dead." Imagine their surprise when they open it and find a
homemade rubber stamp. But then.................

Then the government will see the homemade stamp as some sort of terrorist
symbol and stake out the box.

In the ones I've found, most of them were very well covered by rocks, stones,
or leaves and twigs, in their hiding places. A couple were surprisingly right
out in the open but not in a way that anyone would ever see them, and they
were where they'd receive no sun so there'd be no glint of plastic.

And a couple of others, I hid a little bit better than I found them because
it seemed to me that the plastic *was* just too noticeable. But I have a
question about that. How does one know exactly what the person who owns the box
meant for the box? I hated to just leave them how I found them, but on the
other hand, I didn't know that that wasn't what the original placer intended. I
just assumed it wasn't. Is hiding it better than I found it always an OK thing
to do? Should I notify the placer afterwards? Right now, all the boxes I'm
searching for are within a couple of hours drive of my house, so it wouldn't
be a big deal to go back and redo it if the owner of the box wanted it left the
way I'd found it. Or isn't it really all that big of a thing anyway? I
mean, hiding them a bit better didn't goof up any clues or anything.

Re: to camoflage or not...

From: Timbertoes (toomey@genevaonline.com) | Date: 2004-08-06 15:45:02 UTC
This reminds me of a funny story... my 9-year-old son and I were
searching for a box recently and, after following the clues without
much trouble, we came to the spot where the box should be. We both
start looking around the area and he says "Hey Mom, what's this
thing?" Preoccupied, I glance over and say it looks like a piece of
garbage. He puts it down and we search for at least another half
hour (I hate giving up!). Finally, after hearing my limit of
complaints from my son and 7-year-old daughter who was also with us,
I sat down on a log in frustration, looked down and saw this odd
shaped camouflaged container. My son says "That's the thing you said
was garbage. I tried to open it, but I couldn't." Of course it was
the letterbox, but it was such a clever container that I couldn't be
too angry at myself for being so narrow-minded! All of the boxes we
had found previously were the Rubbermaid box-type - nothing like
this. Moral of the story: I'm a lot more open-minded (and I listen
more closely to my son!).

Timbertoes
