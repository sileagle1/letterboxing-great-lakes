From: lesann847 (l-fischer@northwestern.edu) | Date: 2004-07-21 11:52:28 UTC
In some ways, the Time article was just what I expected, but in
other ways it was better than I expected.

It was better in that it emphasized the trinity of "art, athletics and
mystery." In that aspect, I was pleased that they "got it."

It was also better in that pictures from our gathering were used.
I think this was because the photographer lucked into a
gathering (rather than the photographer who followed the
Maplines), and so had lots of variety for the photo editor to
choose from.

What was just as I expected bears on the realities of magazine
publishing. I thought it possible that this article would never be
published at all. Such an article would be pretty low on Time's
list of priorities when you consider what is going on nationally
and internationally (was it bumped from the previous week?).

Based on the nature of Time Magazine, I thought one page,
including pictures, would be the upper limit of the space alloted
for a piece on an unusual sport/hobby, and there is only so
much one can say in one page.

An editor in such a publication sometimes assigns a journalist
to the story based on her pitch. It's possible that this was Deirdre
Van Dyk's story from the beginning, hence the East Coast focus.
If the article is not pitched but comes from the editor, she or he
neverthless assigns the story to one main journalist, often
someone with whom they have cultivated a strong working
relationship.

Whether or not it's a particular journalist's story, the editor may
contact a couple of stringers for local color. One of those is
Elizabeth Coady who was at our gathering. Such freelance
writers work hard and often see very little of their work in print.
Freelancers have strong fortitudes in order to combat being
undervalued, overlooked and outright rejected so often.

Of course a journalist is going to lead an interview subject to
believe that everything she or he says will have a major influence
on this important story appearing in a major publication. Would
people spend the tedious time to be interviewed if the journalist
said, "Well, I don't really know if this will be published or not.
Chances are about 5% that we'll quote you."? Not when the
alternative is to actually letterbox rather than talk about it. ;o)
