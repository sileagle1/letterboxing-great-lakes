Future of Letterboxing and Secure Boxes

From: Carol Osada (cjosada@hotmail.com) | Date: 2007-07-26 22:31:09 UTC-06:00
Fungus Woman and I hit the trails today. Heat, high humidity, mud puddles, and some on-again-off-again rain drops can't keep us home on a day off from work. We went in search of several boxes today; some were there, some were missing ( or we were just completely off the mark.) But we hiked our little legs off!

The point I want to make is this. There is no way on this green earth that any individual youngsters or families with children would have gone in search of these boxes. After the heat started to affect our speech and mental capabilities, it was obvious that letterboxes are safe when considerable thought is given to the placement, clues are well written (and not too obvious), there is some challenge, or the boxes are placed in remote areas that require stamina, perseverance, back tracking, and lots of deductive reasoning (and prayers) to find them. If a clue causes you to read, research, solve it, drive, hike, re-read, use a compass, and 'think outside the box', I'm convinced that any mischief makers or unsupervised youngsters will look for something easier.

All this hiking around today....why do we abuse our bodies this way?!!!

Beetle

I know some of you have participated in giving interviews, so don't
take this personally... I really think the first step is to quit
encouraging all the publicity. I know some people think it is better
to have an experienced person participate in an article, but an
alternate approach that I've used is to explain why I WON'T
participate in an article someone is writing. I have taken the time
to tell the reporter why many think publicity is a bad idea, what has
resulted from some articles and that if they DO insist on writing an
article that they should stress the secrecy and discretion aspect.

I agree with what Beetle wrote recently - letterboxing is something
that started in England as a challenge for adults, so I am
disappointed to see this turning into a "kid's activity", with
articles (and now even corporate ads!) appearing almost quarterly in
Family Fun magazine. We have taken our nieces and nephews out
several times, but it is not as a kid's activity, but an adult
activity that they get to do with us. They have learned compass
skills, problem-solving, etc. We have created boxes that families
with kids can easily do, but I've had at least 2 boxes visited
by "Play Groups" consisting of 3-4 adults and 15-20 preschoolers.
Yikes!

One easy way to take your clues off-line, is to list them in the
Files section on this message board. Another more involved appraoch
would be to start your own website or blog using one of the free
sites available (or start your own Yahoo group to host your clues),
and link to it from this site. You can also then give the link to
whoever you are comfortable sharing it with.

I'm sorry to see this happening, since I really like the convenience
of finding almost all clues on the letterboxing.org site, no matter
where we travel (although in the past year or so we've also had to
check AQ, for the people that won't use the original letterboxing
site). I plan to keep my clues on the LBNA site until a problem
develops in our area. If it does, I'll move them to a private
website that I already have developed. However in addition to
excluding the troublemakers, that tactic will most likely also
exclude people that may be traveling through the area.

Wisconsin Hiker

From: (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-08-05 14:43:26 UTC
I have to agree with Beetle wholeheartedly. While I certainly can understand time constraints and other demands and restrictions that influence a person's ability to put distance on their boxes and even put in the time to make clues puzzling, etc., I think Pennypenny makes a good point when stating that when planting a box that is more easily accessible, one has to accept that there will be a much higher rate of loss.

I often hear people pondering as to why vandalism and missing boxes seem to have become such problems. First off, these problem have always been there -- it is just a part of the game of letterboxing. We are so much more aware of it now with all of the talk lists and message boards, etc. I do think we are seeing many more occurrences, but there are thousands more letterboxes. I would venture to guess that in ratio to the number of boxes that are out there, the number is probably less if you don't include widespread vandalism such as the recent events in CT.

We have to consider that many things about letterboxing have changed and evolved to make the game accessible to a much broader range of participants, namely the overall difficulty level of boxes being placed. While it is great that so many people are discovering the hobby and finding ways to make it work for them -- ie. given time constraints and limitations due to physical ability and young children, etc., there then needs to be the understanding that along with making such allowances, there is an increase in missing boxes.

And then of course, there is the publicitiy...

Re: [LbGLK] Re: Future of Letterboxing and Secure Boxes

From: Carol Osada (cjosada@hotmail.com) | Date: 2007-08-05 23:49:36 UTC-06:00
It was HOT today! The kind of hot that causes sweat to run down your face until you can't see where you're walking. But I was off work today and no weather condition is going to keep me in the house when there are letterboxes out there. Those letterboxes are EVERYWHERE! So much for homeland security....LOL!

I'm working on several new letterbox placements...soon to be released. It's not easy to accept the blame for a missing box, but sometimes the location of the box causes the increasing higher risk of it disappearing. As a box creator, I dislike hearing about logbooks that are scribbled up because that just indicates that a child found the box and they were not being supervised. That is certainly not true in all letterboxing families.

The first time one of my letterboxes was reported missing, after less than a month, I was devastated! I mourned! But 'all things must pass' and letterboxes don't last forever.

Best we can do is to educate each other and be mindful that someone put a lot of time and thought into the creation of the letterboxes we find.

1. Carry replacement and repair supplies. Random acts of kindness on the trail is always appreciated. Contact the placer asap.
2. When you find a letterbox, let the adrenaline rush settle down before you pick up the box. You may forget exactly how the box was placed.
3. Check to make sure the baggies are all sealed tightly. Carefully press the air out of the bag before sealing the last bit of the zipper closure. This allows the baggies to fit in the box and there is less likelihood of the letterbox lid popping off due to excessive bulk in the box.
4. Some boxes have incredible tight sealing lids. I try to find the tightest lids I can. However, if we all took a few extra seconds to make sure all four corners are completely pressed down and the edges of the box are sealed (no baggies hanging out), this would help preserve the integrity of the boxes through those rainy and snowy seasons.
5. I try to remember and emphasis when I reveal to others about this crazy pastime that I am involved in is this....avoid eating food and handling the boxes. Those little woodland critters can sense the slightest trace of food on a letterbox. Even scented hand towelettes may not smell like food, but may cause them to become curious as to what the scent is.
6. And the last thing I try to remember on the trails is to BE SNEAKY!!! That's the fun part! Look around. Wait for the best opportunity to retrieve the box. Sometimes you have to wait till the coast is clear. Sometimes you have to leave and come back later. This is soooooo hard to do, but we do it to protect the boxes as if they were our own creation.

Like any hobby, letterboxing will go through ebbs and tides. Even when magazines and newspapers print articles about letterbox (what's that), I tend to think that most people skim the article and turn the page. Why do I think this? Because a lot of people roll their eyes and aren't about to go hiking to find a rubber stamp in a Tupperware box. When you think about it, it's pretty weird! They may try to find one of two boxes, but it's the truly insane ones like us that keep searching.

Happy Hiking and Safe Trails

Beetle
(Sometimes called Carol)
