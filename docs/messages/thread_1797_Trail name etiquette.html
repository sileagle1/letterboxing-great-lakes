Trail name etiquette

From: rob_of_loxley (stickandstring@gmail.com) | Date: 2005-09-08 00:48:01 UTC
As a new (and highly addicted) letterboxer I have a question on
trail name etiquette. I posed the question to Grandvilla and it was
suggested to repeat my question here. Here goes...
I discovered letterboxing through Springchick's Flickr
photostream and introduced it to my family. We have hunted a few
boxes together but I have hunted many by myself. For the boxes that I
have hunted with the family, I created a stamp and we use our family
name when logging in. For the boxes I have found on my own, I created
a personal stamp and use the trail name of Robin of Loxley. Is this
proper form?
Grandvilla also had a great suggestion of letting the kids create
their own stamps and having them do as much of the carving as they are
able to. Then they will also be able to stamp in themselves. I also
thought it would be fun if they had their own log books to use. Any
thoughts?
On another note, I have placed my first box this evening in the
Allegan State Game Area and I am very excited about it. I have yet to
write up the clues and I am a bit nervous in that I want to make them
"good" ones. Wish me luck.
Thanks everyone.
Regards,
RofL





Re: [LbGLK] Trail name etiquette

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2005-09-07 22:03:04 UTC-04:00
Hi (again) RofF! I think that letting the kids make up their own stamps is a great idea -- you will be amazed at how proud of that little stamp they will be as they stamp into the box log books. And of course, having their own logbooks will be a real treasure for them also. Just keep in mind that it will mean a little extra time at the box to allow for the additional stampings, but well worth it in my mind to get the kids involved and feeling like they own a part of it.

Oh, and perfectly acceptable to have a "family" trail name and individual names for each member.

SpringChick

RE: [LbGLK] Trail name etiquette

From: Debbie Kotlarek (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2005-09-07 22:05:23 UTC-05:00
We automatically assumed that our nieces and nephews would want their own
stamps and logbooks when they started at ages 7, 10 & 10. The older two
picked out their own store bought stamps and made up their own trail names.
After losing a variety of store bought and handmade stamps, I carved a
signature stamp for the youngest with his approval of design & name. Having
everything separate has worked well, since they sometimes like to choose a
different color for stamping something in their own logbook and they have a
more individual involvement with stamping in, etc. Our niece (now 12) has
even started writing her own little comments in the logbooks when we stamp
in. We don't always have the same kids with us each time, so each of their
logbooks is slightly different. However SpringChick is definitely on target
when she says it will take more time! Sometimes we have had 5 kids with us
and it does take quite a while to get everyone logged in. It is really
confusing when we find a hitchhiker and even though we try to be organized,
on more than one occasion we've discovered (after we're gone) that someone
has missed stamping into something!

They are each planning to carve something for the Haunted Harvest Gathering
and are excited about hiding their first boxes.

Wisconsin Hiker

Re: Trail name etiquette

From: cdem_ltrbox (wp4@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2005-09-08 05:08:29 UTC
We have 2 kids (9 and 11) and started letterboxing a year ago. We
decided that each of us would have our own stamp and logbook
(instead of a family stamp and logbook) for several reasons. First
of all, we don't always letterbox all together--my husband insists
on working instead of going on all of our little summer
expeditions! Plus, I wanted the kids to have their own logbooks for
when they leave the nest in the (more rapidly than I can believe)
not-so-distant future. In fact, on one letterboxing trip this
summer (Schoolhouse Rocks!), the kids mentioned that they would be
taking their grandkids letterboxing someday and that it would be
cool to show them the logbooks they had as kids. Yeah!!

Stamping can be a bit chaotic with 4 of us stamping in, but we're
trying to get a system down for inking and stamping. My daughter
even writes in the placer's logbook on occasion.

We did decide to create a family stamp ("Waltzing Pigs") for when we
place boxes, since that is a family affair for finding the hiding
spot and creating the clues, etc. We've only placed one box so far,
but another one is soon to come.

BirdWoman

From: Doglvrs RASW (doglvrs_4@msn.com) | Date: 2005-09-08 14:12:11 UTC
As the highly addicted to LBing mother of 2...here is my experience.
When we first started out, there was one stamp - the family! We stamp our
clue sheet - so we just kept them all in a binder. The binder was heavily
looked over on a regular basis.
Then each of the kids wanted a stamp...they started out at personal
travelers, but as soon as they had them in their hot little hands, they
wanted to stamp every box we found.
"the skull" (8 y/o boy) carved his own stamp and is so proud of that little
thing - he can't wait to stamp into every single box we get! He is also the
2nd most addicted to LBing, and the 2 of us have gone together on many
hunts.
"Daddy's Girl" (11 y/o girl) wanted words for her stamp, and so I carved it
for her. She is less interested in making sure her stamp is in every box we
find...and mostly would rather stay home and paint her nails if given the
opportunity.
They both have their own log books now (thank you Santa) and while "the
skull" is eager to fill his himself..."Daddy's Girl" just wants us to take
her logbook along for the stamp.

In short - my sons hand carved stamp is his pride and joy! He even took it
to school yesterday to show what he did on his summer vacation. He can't
wait to stamp in and get logged.
PLUS - when we've been at gathering's, he has his own stamp to exchange with
people, and this keeps him busy much of the day.

YES - it's busy when stamping in to a found box....but in the end, it's so
worth it - we don't even think about it anymore.

Doglvrs
