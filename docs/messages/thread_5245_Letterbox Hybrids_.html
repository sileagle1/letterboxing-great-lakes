Letterbox Hybrids?

From: Carol (cjosada@hotmail.com) | Date: 2008-08-12 02:10:35 UTC
A friend who just bought a GPS unit is inquiring about geocaching and
asked me about "letterbox hybrids." She wrote:

"Letterbox Hybrid: A letterbox hybrid cache is a combination of a
geocache and a letterbox in the same container. A letterbox has a
rubber stamp and a logbook instead of tradable items. Letterboxers
carry their own stamp with them, to stamp the letterbox's log book and
inversely stamp their personal log book with the letterbox stamp. The
hybrid cache contains the important materials for this and may or may
not include trade items. Whether the letterbox hybrid contains trade
items is up to the owner."

Here are my questions - Are geocachers creating geocach/letterboxes?
Has anyone come across this? Recently, I found some geocach 'stuff' in
one of my letterboxes: a wooden coin, a travel bug metal tag and a key
chain. Luckily, my stamp was still there.

Beetle

Re: [LbGLK] Letterbox Hybrids?

From: Katrina Lustofin (drkaty@gmail.com) | Date: 2008-08-11 22:57:21 UTC-04:00
Yes, geocachers are creating letterbox hybrid caches. Some of them are
created by people who participate in both activities, some seem to be
created simply to be a cache that is a little different. Most letterbox
hybrid caches are listed on the geocaching website - they have a little
envelope icon to differentiate them from normal caches.

A few years ago I accidentally planted a letterbox virtually on top of a
geocache - in a tree just a few feet away from the tree the cache was in. I
found out because one of the cachers took the time to look the box up on
LBNA and email me, warning me that most of the cachers in our area were
unaware of letterboxing and might, in ignorance, trade for the stamp. When
I went to pull the box and move it, I found that 5 or 6 cachers had logged
in, and one had left a trade item for me - though thankfully didn't take my
stamp!

Katy

makes sure that everyone who reads them knows better than to take the stamp!

From: Kermit (kermitboxer@gmail.com) | Date: 2008-08-13 07:27:36 UTC-05:00
Geez, don't yell at Carol, she's trying to understand stuff.

Go ahead and yell at me -- I have very little use for geocachers in general,
but I am grudgingly willing to share my trail with them as long as they:

1) Read the damn box and don't take my stamp.

and

2) Read the damn box and don't take my stamp.

I used to geocache (for about a week), it took me all of 10 minutes to find
a trinket to place in a cache, but it takes me a day or more to develop,
transfer, and carve a stamp. Hell yeah my "sensibilities may be violated by
something less than an exquisitely hand-carved stamp!" I see letterboxing
as a community, a journey, AND an art. I only see geocaching as the first
two.

I would argue that the majority of letterboxers tend to treat their finds
with some reverence, as they recognize the effort that goes into the
development and placement of a box -- we find a stamp theme, develop/carve
stamp, find meaningful location, develop clues (often puzzle clues with
layers of complexity), hide box, and cross our fingers that it doesn't get
trashed within a year.

Go ahead and attack if that makes you feel better, but standing in a spot
and getting a GPS coordinate for a hidden trinket box? There's a lot less
invested there.

-Kermit

Re: Letterbox Hybrids?

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2008-08-13 17:10:04 UTC
I was hoping that we could just let this issue lie, since it is an
emotional topic for some and there are often specific situations and
justified reasons for the way that people feel.

But even if a letterbox has been muggled by a careless cacher, or the
stamp erroneously exchanged by an ignorant cacher or the letterbox
removed and replaced with a geocache by a thoughtless cacher (yes, I
have had all of these things happen to my letterboxes), that still
does not make all geocachers bad people nor does it make geocaching a
bad hobby.

During the 6 years that I have been letterboxing I have met some
wonderful letterboxers, but I have met some real jerks too, not to
mention careless letterboxers who don't bother to reclose or rehide
the boxes, resulting in missing or destroyed letterboxes. In like
regard I have come across some real jerks in the geocaching world, but
I have also come across a lot of really nice people who are out on the
trails, enjoying the outdoors doing their hobby.

Actually some of the most active geocachers here in the West Michigan
area also letterbox, and I have had great communication with many
others when there are issues with boxes and caches not playing nicely
together.

One thing I have found when you discover there is a cache located near
one of your letterboxes... Position your letterbox so that the
geocache is the first thing that a seeker will find when he arrives in
the area. I had a box that was continually being found by cachers and
often left lying open in their haste to move on to find the geocache.
I repositioned it about 3 feet away so that a person would have to
stumble over the geocache to get to the letterbox and no more problem
-- they find what they are looking for and never even realize the
letterbox is nearby. I have done this with at least 4 boxes and there
have been no further problems.

It's a big world, I'm sure we can all play nicely together.

SpringChick
