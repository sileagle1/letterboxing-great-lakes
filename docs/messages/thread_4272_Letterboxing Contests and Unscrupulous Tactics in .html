From: familyof6portland (familyof6portland@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-07-26 01:22:14 UTC
Hey all! (Feel free to offer advice on this if you wish)Anyhow, I have
placed several boxes in the last six months and have been noticing
that nearly everyone finding them are part of a group of people from a
small town south of where I live (a group of nearly 50 people in
teams). Apparently, they are doing some sort of a letterboxing
contests. Fine. However, I have been feeling uneasy for about two
months now. My clues have ranged from really easy to difficult, but
the same people keep finding my boxes. At first I thought it was a
coincidence, but then had a nagging feeling that my clues and
locations were being shared amongst these peoplew when I would have
groups finding my letterboxes in two days/ same day, week, and
everyone got the clues perfectly. I have also received many emails for
from some of them. Today, I found out that this group is sharing
clues, locations, and exactly where the boxes are, and they have even
posted a phone number hotline to help find boxes. Nearly all my boxes
have been compromised, as well as almost all of the boxes in the
mid-Michigan/Lansing/ Grand Rapids, MI locations. To make matters
worse, it has been posted on the internet and in the town paper with
contacts and phone numbers.

It appears we have some unscrupulous letterboxers out there with
disregard to the rules.I have thought about confronting them about
this breach and lack of honesty involved. My wife thinks we should
pull all of the boxes and clues and let them know why. In addition,
should I directly contact these people. I'm really frustrated as I
have already had several stamps and logbooks ruined from these groups.
Any ideas how to approach this? I don't want to stop carving and
planting but this just rips!

From: azobox (jparkerg@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-07-26 11:16:16 UTC
The current situation in CT, and this (continuing?) episode in
mid-Michigan call attention to the changing face of letterboxing.

It isn't the same pastime that captivated many of us over the last
several years.

I'm among the holdouts who clung to the 'good old days'. But, in spite
of that longing for what 'was', I am resigning myself to the realities
of the new letterboxing.
What does this mean? More and more boxers are taking their clues
off-line. Word-of-mouth distribution becomes a safer way to
disseminate special box information. Subscription to limited
distribution cluebooks is another emerging option.

I have archived boxes -- although some of the actual boxes are still
in place. I have pulled clues. I have retired boxes. And I find myself
less inclined to spend time carving and creating stamps and looking
for special places when a quick read of this board and others tells me
that some newer seekers are playing a different game than the one I
understood.

Contests, team-boxing, and sponsorships are closer to the norm than we
ever might have expected. But, that's what we're seeing.

Is it bad? It depends on your point of view, of course. But, make no
mistake, it is not the same.

If I were in mid-Michigan, I'd probably pull the boxes. The seekers
are playing just half of the game, if that. And they're not using the
same rules that were generally followed in the past.
Frankly, I can't imagine that it's particularly rewarding to be led by
the hand to a box. But as I said, I'm old school.

Like our friends in CT who are enduring a rash of vandalism, we can
hope that the thrill of gang-boxing will fade when there are no longer
any new boxes to find. Perhaps then we can see some semblance of an
earlier brand of letterboxing. Don't hold your breath.

Yes, the times are a-changing.


azobox

From: duncan53211 (duncan53211@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-07-26 15:14:58 UTC
1) Posting trail names is fair game. I want to know their trail names
so I know whether they have been at my boxes in MI and whether I
should be worried.

2) This group is sharing clues and info. If you pull your clues or
make them available only through special means, as soon as one of them
gets hold of your hidden clues, the clues will be shared to everyone
and not be hidden anymore.

If you want to hide your clues, the easiest and most effective way

alternate approach that I've used is to explain why I WON'T

resulted from some articles and that if they DO insist on writing an
article that they should stress the secrecy and discretion aspect.

I agree with what Beetle wrote recently - letterboxing is something
that started in England as a challenge for adults, so I am
disappointed to see this turning into a "kid's activity", with
articles (and now even corporate ads!) appearing almost quarterly in
Family Fun magazine. We have taken our nieces and nephews out
several times, but it is not as a kid's activity, but an adult
activity that they get to do with us. They have learned compass
skills, problem-solving, etc. We have created boxes that families
with kids can easily do, but I've had at least 2 boxes visited
by "Play Groups" consisting of 3-4 adults and 15-20 preschoolers.
Yikes!

One easy way to take your clues off-line, is to list them in the
Files section on this message board. Another more involved appraoch
would be to start your own website or blog using one of the free
sites available (or start your own Yahoo group to host your clues),
and link to it from this site. You can also then give the link to
whoever you are comfortable sharing it with.

I'm sorry to see this happening, since I really like the convenience
of finding almost all clues on the letterboxing.org site, no matter
where we travel (although in the past year or so we've also had to
check AQ, for the people that won't use the original letterboxing
site). I plan to keep my clues on the LBNA site until a problem
develops in our area. If it does, I'll move them to a private
website that I already have developed. However in addition to
excluding the troublemakers, that tactic will most likely also
exclude people that may be traveling through the area.

While I don't agree with taking a group of preschoolers on a
letterboxing outing, I don't agree with letterboxing being an adults-
only hobby. My three kids know to be just as respectful as we are when
we're out. They're watchful to make sure that everything is put back
in the box securely and that the box is hidden, sometimes better than
we found it. Chances are that children who are disrespectful about
other people's boxes have parents who are also, and those parents
shouldn't be boxing either.

Letterboxing isn't "exclusive", nor should it be. But it is becoming
more well-known, and with that comes the increased chance of negative
things happening, same as anything else that has increased publicity.
Chances are, the hype will die down after a while and it will go back
to being those who are truly doing it for the right reasons. Until
then, I think taking extra precautions is a good idea, especially when
there are people out there who obviously don't care if they're ruining
a such a good thing for the rest of us.
