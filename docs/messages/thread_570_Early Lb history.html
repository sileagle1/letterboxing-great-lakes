OK, I can hold my tongue no longer. Having just taught a class on letterboxing for a
local Parks and Recreation Department, and having done a great deal of research on
the history of the craft, I would like to share what I believe to be true.

Over a century and a half ago, James Perrott worked as a guide at Dartmoor. One day
in 1854, he left his calling card and a self-addressed postal card in a sealed glass jar
buried in the bank at Cranmere Pool. This pool, incidentally, is totally dry. The next
person (and many after) who made the twelve mile trek to Cranmere left their calling
cards and postcards. Once found, messages were written on the cards and then sent
on. This began a tradition of 'letterboxing' on the moors. The original glass jar has
long since been replace with a tin box containing a rubber stamp and a guestbook
which was added in 1905.

As you see, the stamp was a very early addition. Furthermore, while most stamps
hidden in Dartmoor National Park are commercially made, they are usually made from
original designs conceived by the hider. One of the first stamps ever hidden in the US
is this type of stamp. It was hidden in Oregon by The Green Dragon in October of
1998 and is known as the Brown's Camp Letterbox. There are a number of handmade
stamps in Dartmoor as well and are called 'handcuts.' These are supposedly highly
prized, not scorned in the least.

Personally, I hold the early letterboxers in our own country in the highest regard.
They developed a game that is very sophisticated, one for intellingent adults. I am sad
to see that so many newcomers to our hobby have not realized this and consider it an
appropriate activity for four year olds. So much for my mini lecture... I'm back to my
carving bench, hoping that one day I will be half the carver that SpringChick is.
