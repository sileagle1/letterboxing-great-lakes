The definition of an 'event box' is pretty wide open. Basically it
is a box that was created specifically for the event -- it may be
used only for the event and that's it, or it may go on to be a
regular letterbox after that. It may have clues, or some other
ingenious way of making the rounds at the event.

There are 'event boxes' that are made specifically for one event,
then there are travelling event boxes that make the rounds from one
event to another.

I have a travelling event box that I started at the GL Gathering
last May and it has been to about 15 gatherings around the country
since then. I have it back in my possession right now and it will
definitely be making an appearance at our gathering before it flies
off again. I can't go into detail about how that one is passed
around since it will spoil it for at our gathering.

At the GL Gathering last year there was a series of about 12 boxes
made by letterboxers from all over the US that were planted in the
woods at the park of the gathering. Clues were written and
distributed just as any other letterbox. There were also a few
travelling boxes that were hidden near and around the shelter and
there were no clues, rather the location was passed around via word-
of-mouth. I initially shared the location of each of these boxes
with one person at the gathering and they then passed it to another
person, etc. People had to ask around and listen and watch to find
out how to get them.

There really is no set rule as to what an event box must be -- just
a log and a stamp and the rest is limited only by your imagination.

Someone else had asked about the "share your stuff" table. It is
simply a table where people can bring in a stamp carving for others
to see or a cool handmade log book or even an ingenious LB
container, etc. The stamps and/or boxes on this table are off-
limits to stamping -- they are simply there as show and tell items.

Another big hit at gatherings are personal travellers. Many people
have personal traveller stamps that you can get as an exchange if
you know the right thing to say to them or how to ask them for it --
the way you know these things is to keep your eyes and ears open, to
read the archives at the letterbox list and to visit their personal
web site and look for hints, etc.

The main idea -- FUN!!!

SpringChick
