From: beckers410 (Mathw613@aol.com) | Date: 2007-05-27 04:56:45 UTC
Hey Guys,

I talked to my Dad tonight on the phone for about half an hour. For
those of you around a few months ago may remember some discussion of
his being a Vietnam Vet and how when he came home from the war he was
spat upon. He wrote me a few years ago (after I sent him an Email
with about 50 questions all about his service) that before getting
back to the states he thought he was a hero and he soon learned that
he wasn't a hero at all.

He was in the navy and never saw any combat, he worked on airplanes
and was in the service 4 years and 3 months during the war.

In an off post Email I got from Trailtracker she wrote, "please thank
your dad for me for serving!!!! ESPECIALLY since he is a Vietnam Vet!"

I cut and pasted that and Emailed it to my father. He told me over
the phone he wasn't sure what to make of the sentance and then
he "got it."

He told me that I was only the second person (in over 30 years!!!) to
actually say "thank you" to him for serving. He told me that the
first person was in a recovery group he was in, I was the second and
Trailtracker you are the 3rd person ever to thank him.

He told me that my mom never thanked him because she was so against
the war (they met while he was in the service).

Thank you Trailtracker for making my dad feel special.

I decided to post this to the group to see if there was anything else
we could do to "Thank" those that served in Vietnam -- not just my
dad, but all Vietnam Vets.

Any ideas??

Thanks,
Hx6

From: Barb Funk (mmfunk99@greenapple.com) | Date: 2007-05-27 08:00:21 UTC-04:00
Hartx6 and Mrs. Dodlvrs,

I'm on my way out the door to finish up planting letterboxes for the
Columbus Cluez Great Lakes Event and had promised myself I was not going
to check email or any letterboxing website before I left---but I'm glad
I did--- seeing these two posts.

Hartx6, my heart breaks too when I hear that so very few have thanked
your dad for serving. When I was the GM of The Container Store in
Columbus, I had my entire staff alert me any time someone serving was in
our store so I could thank them---again, especially if it was someone
who had a hat or something on that said Vietnam. This is something I
have always done but when I saw that Vietnam Vets finally became brave
enough to wear something that identified themselves AS a Vietnam Vet, I
wanted to be extra sure to show my support and thank them for doing so.
This is because them doing that, provided me with that opportunity I
might not otherwise have. It broke my heart during that war when our
soldiers got spat on and were treated with disrespect but when it
happened to my own son within hours of coming home from his first
deployment to Iraq, it kicked up more emotions than I thought possible
inside one body. I thought we had learned our lesson back then and it
never occured to anyone would ever have to go through what these Vets
went through.

I will say it again. Thank you Hartx6's dad for serving our country.
Thank you for doing so without concern to self and in spite of the
"apparent" lack of support. I still believe there are FAR more who
supported our Vietnam Vets than those who spoke out without the least
bit of consideration who did not. The firestorm that occured on The
View this week (not going to even NAME her NAME on this post) made me
physically sick, but I do try to remember that most who make comments
like that do not have anyone who has served.

Mrs. Doglvrs, I think your idea is wonderful!!! If it would help, I can
make sure we have a drop off box at the event! (R-gang did the same
thing for me and my PT Postcards for the 82nd Airborne at Dr. Watson and
Van Man's recent schoolhouse event for Easter goodies to send to
Afghanistan.) It was heartwarming to see how generous everyone was and
most importantly---the soldiers in my son's platoon couldn't have been
more appreciative!

Hartx6, I'm so glad to hear that my thank you made a difference and I'm
glad your dad understood why I said it the way I did. I'm very grateful
to know that. Tell him to get ready though, because he may just become
overwhelmed with the thanks I have faith he will receive with Mrs.
Doglvrs idea!!!

Later this weekend, I will email you off post. I have a couple of other
ideas!

Oh!!! BTW...your dad is DEFINITELY a hero!!! Just because your dad did
not see combat does NOT mean he is not a hero---the point is he was
willing. Ask any soldier who is or was a combat soldier and they will
be the first to tell you that! Please do not "misinterpret" the value
of your dad's contribution. It takes a TON of support for these
soldiers to be able to be on the front line. If anyone has ever read
comments from those who pack other soldiers' parachutes---you know what
I mean. A good friend of mine, after 23 years of service in the Army
and being part of the first deployment to Iraq, went back recently as a
civilian contract laborer---to diffuse bombs?! Not even 2 weeks later,
one of his buddies got killed right beside my friend as they got
ambushed on their way back from diffusing 32 bombs south of Baghdad in
one night. Now my friend is going to be a mechanic over there making
sure the military vehicles don't break down. The effort of everyone
counts--including those doing the laundry for our troops. It counts
when someone takes time to write a letter or send a postcard too!

Warm hugs and handshakes to all vets out there this weekend and always!
Trailtracker

From: perdu_watcher (perdu_watcher@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-05-27 16:23:38 UTC
Growing up during the Vietnam "Police Action," I lived near an air
force base; my neighbors were mostly those in the air force who
lived off base. There were rallies and protests in the park around
the corner...it was such a mess!

I wore a POW bracelet in about the 5th/6th grades and remember
getting snide comments from older teenagers/adults about wearing a
bracelet and how it showed that I supported everything that is wrong
with our society but I also remember at that tender age having the
presence of mind to say that I thought it showed support for the
person whose name was on it and that I was sorry that anyone
anywhere at anytime had to be a prisoner of war or that war even
existed.

Lt. Williams (my bracelet's name) was one of the lucky (I'm not sure
that's accurate) ones who returned home and actually wrote me a
letter (it was a form-type letter that went to all of those who wore
his bracelet) and I had no idea what to say to him in response. I
sent him the bracelet and said that I was glad he made it back home
to his family. I don't think I said thank you.

I lost my first love/crush (I'll never really know) a few years
later when the war was allegedly over, he was sent as "support"
right after High School graduation (he was a few years older than
me), I never had a chance to thank him but I'll never forget his
smile. good night sweet prince

Please tell your father, I thank him!
~Perdu

war was over. They sent him to Japan to do "clean up work" and he was there
for about 9 months when his father had a stroke and the Red Cross brought him
home. Because the war ended before he got out of boot camp he doesn't feel
like a veteran, I begged to differ with him!!
