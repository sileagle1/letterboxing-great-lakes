Animals sense of smell / the science info

From: bindlestiff_boxer (bindlestiff_boxer@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-04-08 10:31:42 UTC

One of the things I included in my Letterboxing presentation at the
local library was a bit about animals sense of smell. I wanted to
impress on the kids that they should not be eating snacks while out
hunting boxes. Food smells may transfer from hands to the box or its
contents and attract hungry or curious animals. The animals may then
destroy the box just because they are looking for the food they smell.
I also stressed to them that the best thing for cleaning stamps is
plain water, again because any lingering scent may attract animals who
wonder that that smell might be.

Here is the science behind what I was telling the kids:
Humans have an olfactory membrane that covers about 4 square cm. which
is just barely the size of two postage stamps.
The size of a cats olfactory membrane is about 14 square cm.
A dogs olfactory membrane is huge by comparison and covers up to 150
square cm. which is an area equivalent to a large pocket handkerchief.

To help you understand what the size of the olfactory membrane means:
a normal dog can smell a variety of substances at concentrations
1,000 to one million times lower than humans.

Stunning, isn't it? And you know that wild animals have to hunt for
all of their food (which takes up a great part of their existance) so
will check out anything that offers even the remotest chance of being
a meal ... especially if it seems like an easy meal (meaning it won't
try to eat them!)

Plus there are some animals that are just plain curious and will check
out just about anything just to see what it is. (Toss an empty paper
bag on the floor in front of your cat and just watch how they can't
resist sticking their nose in there just to check it out.)
