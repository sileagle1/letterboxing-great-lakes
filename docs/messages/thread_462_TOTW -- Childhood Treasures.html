From: Doodle & Deedle Bug (doodle_n_deedle@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-08-01 15:22:01 UTC-07:00
I had a cigar box, too. A fancy old wooden one from my grandfather. I remember glueing paper to the insides of the box so I could personalize the inside. I don't really remember what I kept in it, though...the only thing I recall is my Hot Wheels car. My brother had all the matchbox & hot wheels & other mini cars, but that ONE was mine. It was white, and had a flag design on the hood. I do recall that as I got older, I had kept my Girl Scout patches that couldn't fit on my sash in that box, and for all I know, they're all still in that box somewhere at my parents'...

One childhood treasure that wasn't kept in the box was the V. My brother & I were HUGE Voltron fans. We loved that cartoon, every day when it came on we watched, we'd race around on our bikes pretending to be the lions, and since our parents never bought us the Voltron toys we'd make our own with his Legos. Anyway, we lived really close to our elementary school. One day, we found this V. It was one of those letter tiles, like the numbers that are put on houses to show the address. Black background, raised white V. So to us, it was the V for Voltron, and we kept it hidden in a hollow at the base of a tree at the school, and we'd make special visits to check on the V during the summer. Hiding things in trees...my precursor to letterboxing?

Doodle aka Diane.

Re: TOTW -- Childhood Treasures

From: lesann847 (l-fischer@northwestern.edu) | Date: 2004-08-02 20:25:39 UTC
As a child, I had a large jewelry box, all covered with golden
paper, lined with velveteen, and endowed with song by a built-in
music box. This treasure trove was crammed with jewels: jacks
and marbles; costume jewelry and trinkets from the gumball
machine; skate keys and tiny rubber balls; individual playing
cards that my mom used to trade and collect as a kid, cards I
inherited as her eldest daughter.

I kept the box locked at all times. After all, I had two curious little
sisters. As a child who had her nose in a book more often than
she could be prevailed upon to play with the "little kids," I was
something of an enigma to them.

One of my books was "Tony's Treasure Hunt." As you can
imagine, the story followed Tony as he followed a set of
mysterious clues to find--surprise--a birthday present and party
at the end of the hunt. Always one to be inspired by literature, I
set a hunt for my sisters and cleared the jewelry box of the
baubles I was outgrowing. I wrote clues, I planted trinkets as
incentives along the way, and at the end they gained the big
treasure chest.

I wish I still had the clues, and I wish I could see the treasure
chest again, but I've never regretted giving all that loot away. It
gave my sisters pleasure, I loved writing clues and being
sneaky, and the memory of that hunt gives me even greater
pleasure than my sisters took in it.

Today, when I place a letterbox, I try to scan the stamp and
photograph the logbook and packaging so I can have a good
record that I can think back on with pleasure when the
letterboxes ultimately go the way of all ephemera.
