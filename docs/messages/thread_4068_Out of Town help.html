From: Wisconsin Hiker (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2007-05-20 02:27:15 UTC
Funny you should ask that right now - we're in the midst of planning
several weekend trips, several in other states. Two years ago when
we were planning a letterboxing trip, a boxer from Pennsylvania
(Lightin' Bug) asked us if we used the Microsoft Streets & Trips
program. He said that if we had it, he could email us a map of PA
with many of the boxes "pinned". I did a little research on the
software package and then very soon thereafter bought a copy at Sam's
Club for about $25. It's a great package, allowing us to zoom in to
print detail maps and pan out to print overview maps. It includes
the entire world, but I think only the USA is extremely detailed.
(However I have "pinned" all the boxes in Italy...) When planning a
trip, we read the clues and first get to the right city, then can do
a "search" on an exact address, street names or even park names. The
package will zoom right to the street or park you are looking for.
We generally mark the start of a letterbox hunt with a blue
electronic pin (and label the pin with the box name), change it to a
blue flag if we find the box, change it to a red pin if we look for
it and can't find it. We use purple flags for boxes we plant. The
software also shows locations of restaurants and hotels and allows
you to put hyperlinks in your pin labels. We have used it
EXTENSIVELY for all our letterboxing ever since we bought it. (Oh
yeah, it also has the ability to put in GPS coordinates to locate a
point.)

When we plan a trip, we usually creat a simple spreadsheet that lists
the box name, planter, number of boxes, and city for those we plan to
look for. We add a column for an estimated hike time or distance (We
REALLY appreciate it when plnters include this info!). We also add a
column for what # find or plant the box is and a column for
comments. That way we can note if maintenance is needed or anything
else we may wish to tell the planter when we send them an email about
the box status. We organize this spreadsheet based on a route we
plan to travel and then print it out. Our spreadsheet always include
a LOT more boxes than we ever can realistically find, but we figure
it is best to be prepared with a variety in case the weather is bad
or to adapt to our plans on that particular trip.

We print out clues for all the boxes on our spreadsheet and then also
print several maps from the Streets & Trips program. Some maps are
an overview to show boxes in relation to each, some to show how to
get to the general box location from a major road, and some very
close in to show the details of the surrounding streets names. We
staple the maps to the clues.

Based on expereince, we have also found it very helpful to print
fairly detailed maps on how to get from one box to another!

We then put the clues & maps in a 3-ring binder in the same order as
our spreadsheet (which was organized by route).

It's a lot of up front preparation, but that allows us to spend all
our time on the road actually out looking for boxes!

Wisconsin Hiker

At 09:58 PM 5/19/2007, you wrote:
>When you guys are going to a town you aren't familiar with to go
>letterboxing how do you prepare?

Well, I'm no expert... but for the upcoming Columbus thing... I've
searched for clues in all the surrounding areas and for clues within
real close proximity to all the roads we're travelling on there and
back. I've printed copies of all the clues that interested me,
labeled and grouped them by location. I've printed maps for all
those locations, both close up maps and zoomed out maps. These maps
are all stapled to the individual clue sheets. For any parks with
trail maps, I've printed those as well, and stapled them to the
clues. For some, but not all of the boxes, I did Google's satellite
mapping to see an overview of the area and get some kind of visual of
the place. Also, I ordered an Ohio state map, as well as a Columbus
area street map from AAA. I think I may do some "X marks the spot"
markings on these larger maps to get a more overview of where all the
boxes I want to find are located.

There are some more mystery type boxes or boxes in which you really
have to know the area to figure out where you're going. You may have
to forget about those, if you don't have a local person with
you. But, for a couple of those, I've tried googling keywords and
have figured out some of the locations. So, I guess that depends on
what kind of info you have to start with.

When I went to the Denver, Colorado area over Christmas, I did
similar things... not quite as much since I figured I'd have a
"local" with me at the time, so I didn't need as much mapping. At
that time, I signed up for the rocky mountain LB yahoo group and
asked for advice/suggestions on "must find" boxes. There are so
many boxes there and I had very little time to find them. The people
on that group were very friendly and helpful. I had several of their
suggestions all lined up. Unfortunately, when I was there, they had
an uncharacteristic 24 feet or so of snow (ok, ok, so maybe it was 24
inches... still a freaking lot of snow!). So, we were only able to
find 1 box. I have all the clues and stuff ready for next Christmas!, though!

So, I guess my three main pieces of advice are... 1. Google is your
friend! 2. Join the regional area Yahoo group and introduce
yourself, ask for suggestions. And finally #3...Google is your best friend!

Bali

From: xxxxxxxx (BrighidFarm@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-05-20 09:01:09 UTC-05:00
I'm a closet cartographer, so even tho I'm fat and a chocoholic, I probably
have more maps than ounces of chocolate around the house.

Microsoft Streets and Trips is software that I like. I don't use it to it's
fullest tho, but it looks like it's chock full of stuff. For planning for
going from town to town for boxes or even from one end of a city to another
for boxes, I think MS Streets & Trips does a great job.

I do map out from town to town, box to box, and keep the paperwork in order
of travel on a clipboard in the car, with a second copy in my backpack. But
unfortunately, we're kinda flighty folks and often change things on a whim.
If we see a billboard with some historical site or such, we might decide to
take a sudden detour to go see it. That might bring us in actually closer
to a box that *wasn't* next on our list. So we'll quick have to rearrange
things. So we map out everything in a general way but we don't get too
crazy about it because we know ourselves. I don't think we've yet done
everything in the order we've set out to do it. :-)

I also belong to AAA, so I stop in there and get any maps or books for the
cities I'm going to. And now AAA also has online trip planning. I haven't
checked it out yet, tho, to see if it can be used the same way the MS
Streets & Trips can be used as far as mapping from place to place to place
but from the mailing they sent me on it, it looks like it can be.

If I don't know the area I'm going to, stopping at AAA also gives me info on
places to see in that area just in case I want to do something besides
letterboxing. For example, we have a trip planned to Springfield for 3 days
at the end of July, but the main focus of the trip will actually be the
Lincoln Presidential Library and Museum with a little letterboxing on the
side. If I didn't have AAA tho, probably everything could be found right on
the internet. And I *do* do a lot on the interent. AAA basically gives me
starting points of interest. But then I always google everything to find
out hours, prices, etc.

I also have an atlas that has detailed maps of the six surrounding counties
for when I'm not going *too* far out of town.

When it's in my area or out of my area, I always google the particular
forest preserves we may be heading to. A lot of them have taken to putting
some pretty darn good trail maps right online. And actually, I've found
that a lot of the online maps are more detailed and up-to-date than the maps
one gets at the information kiosks and such at the preserve itself. Any
recent changes in hours, any prices, etc. are generally all on the place's
website. Things can change, and they'll change at the website right away,
but the change often won't show up in new brochures and maps until they've
run out of the old brochures and maps and thus have to print new ones.

But the main love of my life is google earth. It gives atlas type map view
and it gives satellite views, and it gives "hybrid" view which put the two
together. It can be particularly great to print out for forest preserves
because in many cases, one can even see the smaller trails. There was one
letterbox in Kane County that I could even see the "landmarks" in the forest
preserve on the map. It can be zoomed in and zoomed out easily and has a
really nice range of zoom. Generally. Sometimes conditions are such that
it can't get a really really close satellite photo, but other times I can
practically read the license plates on the cars in a parking lot. (OK,
that's a bit of an exaggeration, but it can give really good zoom if the
conditions are right.) So I downloaded the google earth info and have the
icon right on my desktop and I never go searching without first clicking on
that and checking things out.

usatf.com is also a good one to track out distance if it's a situation where
you can do that. It's better actually for when I come back from a trip. I
can map out the distance I actually walked and it will also show me the
elevations. But a person needs to have a knowledge of where they're going
in the first place to be able to use it. It's generally best after the
fact. But, like google earth, it also gives atlas map views, satellite
views, and hybrid views.

I also often stop in and check out Border's Books if I'm heading out of
town. The one by me has a nice map section and will often have one map or
another that I don't already have for the area I'm heading to. I don't know
what Barnes & Noble might offer.
