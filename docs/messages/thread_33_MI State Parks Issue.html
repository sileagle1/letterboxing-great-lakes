If I may offer my two cents to my MI friends -

I would say this would be a perfect opportunity to work WITH the MI
State Parks. Since they have not started their new policy yet,
there is time to work with them on a better policy. I would suggest
that a couple Geocachers and Letterboxers get together and draft a
proposal for them. Explain clearly your opposition to their new
policy and also explain to them that it will not work the way they
intend. Instead of them making money on it, they WILL LOSE money,
because all geocaches and letterboxes WILL be either removed or
hidden without their knowledge. (No one will pay $35 to hide a box,
even for a year.) AND fewer people will come to their parks as a
result. Encourage them to look at how CT and other states have
actually MADE money by controlling Geocaching and Letterboxing in
their parks and trails by ENCOURAGING it! Work with the State Parks
to control the proliferation of boxes and caches on sensitive
areas. Have cachers and boxers register thier boxes with the parks
for no fee - just for promotion and upkeep. If the Parks system has
a way to freely register the caches and boxes on thier grounds, they
can have final say as to whether the place the box is intended to be
placed would be safe for the environment they are in charge of
protecting. Let THEM suggest good locations for boxes and caches.
Allow them to advertise that geocaching and letterboxing can be
activities done in the parks! This should bring in more revenue
than them trying to charge people to hide boxes and caches! By far!

To be honest, I think SpringChicks boxes in Muskegon State Park are
a huge draw! I've been raving to the WI letterboxers and regular
folks about it being THE trip to take!

For the record, these boxes and parks are one of the prime reasons I
took my MI letterboxing trip last summer and why I'm looking forward
to going back! If they want any idea of how much money they took in
from just ONE letterboxer traveling through MI State Parks, they
should see my Visa bill! I paid $20 for an annual sticker (and
probably for another one this year), I stayed about 2 1/2 weeks at
various campgrounds at the state parks around the state at about $12
a night (about $200+). I also bought their firewood, shopped at
campground stores, etc. that should've also produced revenue for
them too. Now, there are a dozen or so new WI letterboxers that
seemed very interested in doing part or all of my MI trip. Multiply
my expenses by 12 and that is some healthy income! Add more boxes
to the parks, make us stay more, send us to the "less used" parks,
and up goes the revenue!

I pray us in WI won't have to put up with this! Maybe I should
start checking the WI state parks policies....

Dragon

From: Silent Doug (silentdoug@letterboxing.info) | Date: 2004-03-26 22:42:55 UTC-05:00
Here's some background I just learned about the state parks in PA and how
they handle letterboxing and geocaching. This might help if anyone wants to
approach any other state park officials.

I recently heard from a letterboxer in Western PA who was contacted by
parks officials about their letterboxes in a state park there. The
letterboxer had to fill out a form to get approval for the letterboxes, so
I started doing some research.

The PA DCNR web page doesn't have anything on letterboxing, but they do
have a geocaching policy (and I assume it also applies to letterboxes). I'm
going to followup with the park down the road from my home to check on
their letterbox policy, but in the mean time, I got this response from the
DCNR regarding their geocaching policy:

>The following webpage contains our state park geocaching guidelines:
>http://www.dcnr.state.pa.us/geocaching.aspx
>
>If you plan on placing a geocache in a specific state park, I would
>recommend contacting that park office and speaking to the park manager for
>further information, as he/she will have a central part in making the
>determination on whether or not to approve of the geocache. In addition,
>you'll be required to submit a Geocache Identification Form, which you can
>also download from the above website.

It's interesting that they provide "official geocache" stickers to be
placed on any "registered" geocaches in PA state parks. These are the same
stickers that you can purchase from the geocaching.com web site.

While it's an extra step that must be taken when hiding a letterbox, I'm
not opposed to their policy. At least they're not behaving like the NPS and
banning letterboxes altogether!

From: Deb (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-03-27 11:27:50 UTC
The Michigan geocaching steering committee is currently working with
the DNR to try and effect some change to the policy. I have been in
contact with them and gave some perspective as to letterboxing. The
original policy was $35 per cache/letterbox for a 3 month period of
time. At their last meeting, they were able to get the time frame
increased to a year, although the cache/letterbox must still be
moved every 3 months and the placement fee is still $35. They will
be meeting again, but I do not foresee that the fee will be
dropped. The $35 fee is their base when determining special use
permit fees, which is what they consider this to be.

There are a couple of things I see... First let me say that I have
nothing against geocachers and all whom I have personally come in
contact with on the trail and via e-mail have been great people. I
know we have a few on this list also who are involved in both
letterboxing and geocaching and I am happy to see that at least here
in Michigan, the two hobbies peacefully co-exist. However, that
being said... I have been hanging out over at the MiGO forum a bit
lately because of this State Parks issue and although it certainly
is not fair to slap down a blanket statement that applies to all
geocachers or all letterboxers, I see a very fundamental difference
between the two. It is coming through loud and clear that the
majority of them (at least those who are posting to the forum) use
the parks only for their hobby -- did not use them prior to the
hobby and will not use them in the future if the fee holds. To me,
having grown up in West MI and truly believing we are fortunate to
have some of the best State Parks in the country, that is
appalling! It tells me that for most of them it is all about the
numbers -- collecting caches, not about the hike. And sad to say,
but I think where a reverence for nature is lacking, there does tend
to be increased disregard for the land and environmental concerns.
I am sure the same can be said of some letterboxers, but of those I
have met, most did not begin enjoying the out-of-doors and the parks
when they started letterboxing, rather they began letterboxing as an
activity to enhance the time they were already spending at the
parks. And although we delight in finding the box at the end of the
trail and even plan our hikes around the boxes, we were and would
continue to use the trails and parks even if the boxes were not
there because it is not just about the numbers.

Unfortunately geocaching and letterboxing are treated equally when
it comes to policy at Michigan State Parks because at face value
they do appear to be very similar activities and it would be next to
impossible to exhalt one over the other in terms of philosophy and
environmental stewardship.

As a parks department, if suddenly there was a large subset of
people coming to the parks for the sole purpose of playing their
game, I might try to charge them for that also. I follow the logic
and agree that the parks are probably bringing in more money on
entrance fees and camping, etc. as it stands now, without the fee,
than if the fee were to be in place -- simply because nobody is
going to pay the fee and the activity will move out of the parks.
But they really think people are going to pay to play and the amount
of dollars we are talking is small enough that they probably won't
notice a huge drop in revenue if geocachers and letterboxers do go
elsewhere.

I was happy to hear that there is room for individual decision-
making on the part of the individual park rangers -- that even
though this policy has been handed down, they do have the ability to
make decisions as to how it will be addressed in their park on a
park-by-park, even case-by-case basis. I am even more fortunate
that the park rangers where a number of my boxes are planted are in
favor of letterboxing and have chosen to take a wait-and-see
stance. I will be going out this weekend to check on all of those
boxes and relocate any where slight spur trails are evident, and I
will be keeping a close eye on those boxes to ensure that they do
not cause concern with the park officials there.

I think that trying to convince the powers that be in Lansing is a
bigger war than we have the ammunition for, rather it would be more
effective to work with the local park officials educating them on
letterboxing and setting an example of good stewardship in hopes
that either they will continue to allow them on an individual
level. Once there has been a successful track record in that
respect, perhaps there will be some leverage at a higher level.

Just the way I see it...

SpringChick

over land usage and control when I look at an old map from the 70's
that we used to use when we lived near Midland MI for dirt bike
riding. They have a trail that loops the upper part of the state
that can be used for ORV, they have trails for mountain bikers, they
have land for hunters.....and they have this great initiative still
in the year 2000 to link and maintain use and recreation for
everybody. The site I'm referenceing is gun ho to draw tourist
dollars, so maybe they don't realize the State park people are trying
to cut off those same dollars? I just think this may be an important
angle to look at? I'm a bit of a hitchhiker here, since I'm moving
to PA soon (where I've lived before and am well aware of the corrupt
beaurocracy) but I did want to work with a practice box and my
friend would really enjoy maintaining it and growing into the sport
as well.

Anyway, some of this actually crosses over into the TOTW and maybe
better addressed there. Would it be considered "safe" to assume that
people wouldn't try to locate letter boxes during hunting season when

they try to require a permit as well? Nah, couldn't be they let

Any insight and update would be appreciated by my muddled mind!
