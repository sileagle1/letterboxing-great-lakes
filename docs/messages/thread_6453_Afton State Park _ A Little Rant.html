I agree with you WiHi. That's why I tightly control who has access to my clues. It's my letterbox and I'll plant it wherever I damn well please : ) State Park or not, if people would be more careful where they post their clues, or how they write them, their boxes would last A LOT longer!
I guess a lot of it depends on how you feel about your own boxes. Do you plant so that many people can easily find it? Do you try to make things challenging? Nothing wrong with drive-byes in my opinion, but unless you do some creative clue writing drive-byes usually don't last for too long. Also, posting the name of the park in your clues or TITLE of your clues is a dead giveaway to park rangers, etc., who might be savvy in finding letterboxes.
I'm not pointing the finger at anyone or saying they've made a bad choice, it's their box, but stealth means more to me than just making sure no one is looking while I retrieve a box. Some of my favorite boxes are in places where THEY'RE NOT SUPPOSED TO BE! That is half the fun. The series of 6 I planted for the gathering last fall still aren't posted anywhere and probably will remain W.O.M. It doesn't matter to me if only 1 person a year finds them, I want them to LAST. How many of you have had the awesome experience of opening a box that's been planted for 2, 3, 4 or more years and been the 3rd, 2nd, maybe even FF! I absolutely love the idea of the box sitting unopened, untouched for a long period of time and then one special day there you are seeing something that hasn't seen the light of day for years. Think of what has gone on in your life during that span of time and how long a year really is, or more for that fact. That box has been a silent
sentinel waiting for you to open it up and look back in time so to speak. Who was there last? Do they even still box? Have you ever heard of them? Hopefully they left a little message for you to read.
Gooble Gobble

From: Debbie Kotlarek (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2010-03-22 16:00:25 UTC-05:00
Some park districts DO realize that and ENCOURAGE boxing. I had a box that was discovered in the state Horicon Marsh vicinity by maintenance/mowing people. They took the time to contact me via the email in my box and they really encouraged me to replant it and keep it in the park. They had enjoyed reading the logbook and knew it had brought people to the area. The same thing happened many years ago at one of our local county parks. The ranger took the time to look up letterboxing on-line, contact me, send me a photo of the box in his office, and then REPLANTED it for me. He thought it was a neat hobby and appreciated the type of people it brought to the park. Too bad MN doesn't have the same attitude...

AMEN to that WiHi!!!!! That's what I'm talking about. I have several boxes out there that have taken 10-12 + hours to complete, I even have one with about 30 hours into it total. I'm not taking the chance of some yahoo park ranger or careless newbie (Please don't take offense to that previous comment if you're new, veteran boxers can be careless too) ruining what I have spent so long trying to achieve. Letterboxing is my art, and I protect my "pieces" like a mother hen.
With a little creativity you can make your boxes last a long time in the wild. Some of my favorite finds are boxes where the clues were hard to decipher or the hike was grueling for one reason or another. For instance, Leapin' Lizards has a box in Southern MN that requires you to, well, I don't want to spoil it for others, let's just say that your legs will BURN when you get to the top of a certain bluff (I think I rested at least 5 times and did it in 90 degree weather in July).
Gooble Gobble

I agree with you WiHi. That's why I tightly control who has access to my
clues. It's my letterbox and I'll plant it wherever I damn well please : )
State Park or not, if people would be more careful where they post their
clues, or how they write them, their boxes would last A LOT longer!
I guess a lot of it depends on how you feel about your own boxes. Do you
plant so that many people can easily find it? Do you try to make things
challenging? Nothing wrong with drive-byes in my opinion, but unless you do
some creative clue writing drive-byes usually don't last for too long. Also,
posting the name of the park in your clues or TITLE of your clues is a dead
giveaway to park rangers, etc., who might be savvy in finding letterboxes.
I'm not pointing the finger at anyone or saying they've made a bad choice,
it's their box, but stealth means more to me than just making sure no one is
looking while I retrieve a box. Some of my favorite boxes are in places
where THEY'RE NOT SUPPOSED TO BE! That is half the fun. The series of 6 I
planted for the gathering last fall still aren't posted anywhere and
probably will remain W.O.M. It doesn't matter to me if only 1 person a year
finds them, I want them to LAST. How many of you have had the awesome
experience of opening a box that's been planted for 2, 3, 4 or more years
and been the 3rd, 2nd, maybe even FF! I absolutely love the idea of the box
sitting unopened, untouched for a long period of time and then one special
day there you are seeing something that hasn't seen the light of day for
years. Think of what has gone on in your life during that span of time and
how long a year really is, or more for that fact. That box has been a silent
sentinel waiting for you to open it up and look back in time so to speak.
Who was there last? Do they even still box? Have you ever heard of them?
Hopefully they left a little message for you to read.
Gooble Gobble

Some park districts DO realize that and ENCOURAGE boxing. I had a box that
was discovered in the state Horicon Marsh vicinity by maintenance/mowing
people. They took the time to contact me via the email in my box and they

enjoyed reading the logbook and knew it had brought people to the area. The
same thing happened many years ago at one of our local county parks. The
ranger took the time to look up letterboxing on-line, contact me, send me a
photo of the box in his office, and then REPLANTED it for me. He thought it
was a neat hobby and appreciated the type of people it brought to the park.
Too bad MN doesn't have the same attitude...
