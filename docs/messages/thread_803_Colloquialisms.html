Supper Clubs must be like pasties...uniquely regional.
I did hear a rumor once that in Wisconsin, drinking
fountains are called "bubblers". Hoosiers call the
vaccuum a "sweeper" (which drives me CRAZY!) In New
Orleans, a median is called a "neutral ground". I'm
sure every state has these things that make it
unique....and provides us with unending conversation!

--Armadillo Jo, who is also a Navy brat and thought
that people in Indiana didn't know what a vaccuum was
since they were "sweeping" their carpets.

From: Doodle & Deedle Bug (doodle_n_deedle@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-10-26 07:38:35 UTC-07:00
For being a part-Polish girl from Cleveland, we never had paczkis before Lent began. Maybe because my grandpa was the Pollack, and my Croatian grandma did the cooking? I honestly don't recall ever hearing about them until maybe 5 years ago when the grocery stores started advertising them (grocery store or supermarket? :). Polish flats....that's a new one to me, too. But I knew the term "yatchsemaw" (that is nowhere near the correct spelling - per a website I found, this is closer: JAK SIE MIEWA). Means how are you, and I swear I'm not imagining this, but growing up as a kid (this would be late 70's, early 80's), I remember there being gimmicky car decorations that were a plastic hand on a suction cup that would wave and had "Jaksiemiewa" written on it.

Here in Cleveland it's pop. I have a friend who for some reason says soda, and every time she does it I stare at her, because she's lived in Cleveland her whole life! Do tree lawns exist outside of Ohio? I know they don't in New Jersey (I was friends in college with a Jersey boy - we used to have fun teasing him about our Ohio colloquialisms).

Evidently another Cleveland thing is to end our sentences with prepositions. IE, "Where are you going to?" "Where is my coat at?" Other people think this is odd....? :)

Doodle aka Diane.

PS - I have heard of King Cakes - the grocery stores sell those around here now, too.

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-10-26 23:21:55 UTC-04:00
Around here those subdivisions with the big monster Stepford Wives (or would
that now be Desperate Housewives?) houses and no trees are called "Happy
Houses." Never heard them called that before moving to Muskegon.

And of course, in Michigan we have the "Michigan left turns" which not only
is a term unique to here, but a whole driving procedure that catches people
not from the area off-guard and frantically trying to change lanes on busy
highways to make a left turn, which actually requires them to make a right
turn, and then quickly cross into the opposite lane and make a left turn --
usually with at least two traffic lights to sit through in the process.
Can't believe some civil engineer actually got paid to come up with that
idea!

I was trying to remember the name of this stuff my ex-mother-in-law used to
cook when Doglvrs mentioned Banket and the whole Dutch food thing. It was
called "Stip" (that may not be the correct spelling). It is a Dutch food
which consists of white bread, deep-fried in bacon grease and then smothered
with syrup and eaten for breakfast along with the 3 lbs. of bacon that you
fried to get the grease to cook the stip. How's that for clogging the
arteries?

SpringChick

Since we're talking Ethnic Eats here, the drippy bacon grease bread reminded me of something my Croatian grandma used to make. Stoogelina (that's how I always heard it pronounced, but per my internet search just now I got spellings of both studzienina and studeninja). Anyway, it's jellied pigs feet, and from what I understand, a common Eastern Eurpoean dish in general. Wasn't that appetizing, to go to my grandparents and see the dish of jellied pigs feet sitting on the kitchen table? :) She used to also make vegetable soup with tripe in it. They used to tell me the tripe pieces were chicken. With my father, they just told him it was tripe, and he nodded and ate, never asking what exactly tripe was...

From: Gabrielle Coil (gmc81@randomkitty.net) | Date: 2004-10-27 11:20:51 UTC-07:00
We had all sorts of ethnic foods growing up in my house. My favorite is a Christmas tradition my mom started of baking stolen, a German bread filled with fruits and almonds and topped with a glaze (usually plain vanilla, but sometimes mom gets wild and uses orange extract instead). Kind of like fruit cake, but without the rum and much better. This has, for as long as I can remember, always been Christmas morning breakfast...served at room temperature with a little butter on each slice...yum!
We also had panset, a Philipino rice noodle dish with cabbage, chicken, pork, shrimp (which was usually omitted for no particular reason), carrots, and I swear about half a bottle of soy sauce. I have no idea where my family picked up that recipie since no one is Philipino (although we had plenty of neighbors who were). It's still one of my favorite meals...it reheats well but there are almost never left overs even though it's made in the largest skillet in the house.
And colcannon (this is a phonetic spelling...I have absolutely no idea how to spell it) which is an Irish dish consiting mostly of cabbage and potatoes and maybe parsnips or turnips cooked unitl they are soft and mashed together...kind of like funny tasting mashed potatoes. It's not really as bad as it sounds...but it definately takes a bit of getting used to since it looks exactly like mashed potatoes.
And those are just the ones I remember being cooked on a regular basis. Since moving out of my mother's house I also have added dumplings (pot stickers) and pork buns (both Chinese dishes) to my ethnic repetior.

Kimono Girl

From: Timbertoes (toomey@genevaonline.com) | Date: 2004-10-27 18:56:33 UTC

Wow, sounds like your family was quite willing to try new things! My
family ate very basic fare - my dad is a meat and potatoes kind of
guy and since there were fourteen of us, Mom didn't get too
experimental. Stuff like hamburgers, pot roast, turkey, mashed
potatoes and gravy - that sort of thing. Mom would make us whatever
we wanted on our birthdays and my brothers and sisters would always
get mad when I asked for chicken and gravy over rice. It was (and
still is) one of my favorite dishes. Just ask my husband! One of
the weirdest things we ate was peanut butter and pickle (sweet)
sandwiches that Mom made us for lunches. She had them when growing
up in Chicago and passed it on to us. I loved seeing the response
when my friends at school saw my lunch. One friend even went so far
as to try it (she didn't finish). Other than that, food was pretty
dull. I even eat my hamburgers and hot dogs plain because it took so
long to get the ketchup and mustard passed around the table that it
wasn't worth waiting for. Ahhhh, memories!

Timbertoes, who once hid all her green beans around under her plate
until her lousy brother told on her (and she had to eat them cold).
