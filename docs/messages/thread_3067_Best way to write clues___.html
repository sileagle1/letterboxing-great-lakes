From: doubledragonletterboxer (M8KC5008@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-09-17 14:34:51 UTC
What I do is come up with a basic story line (if that's your thing)
then I find a spot I like. I walk through the trails that lead me to
the spot that I wanted to hide the box and write down anything I see
that looks like it would fit in my storyline as a clue (i.e. 5 trunk
tree is a 5 headed hydra). I write all the major clues down in order
from begining to end in a book (compass readings, signpost numbers,
ect.) and then go over them at home. I make up a storyline with the
clues and when I'm done I go back out to the location and make sure
they are right. I hope that helps
Double Dragon

Re: [LbGLK] Best way to write clues...

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2006-09-17 11:56:15 UTC-04:00
I do it a couple of different ways, depending on the location. If it is a park with a well-developed network of hiking trails, I don't usually scout the area first (especially if I have been there before and am familiar with the general nature of the park), rather I create my box/series and then head over there, often walking several trails in the park before I choose the hiding spot. In this case, I usually find the perfect spot (one can hope) and then write the clues backward as I head back to the trailhead. I make general notes for clues -- paces, degrees, identifying landmarks, etc. and then work them into a story, puzzle, etc. once I am home with the information. One thing I look for when choosing *the perfect hiding spot* is some kind of an identifying landmark nearby. It is all good to find the perfect spot along the trail where there are no stand-out trees or benches or turns in the trail, etc. It is very difficult to get people to the right spot -- this is when you end up with frustrated seekers trying to figure out *which* two-trunked maple tree in the sea of two-trunked oak trees you are actually referencing. One thing to be careful of when writing your clues backward from the hiding spot back to the trailhead -- everything is backward and your left is your right. I also find it helpful to number the clue notes as I write them.

If I want to plant a letterbox at a specific place, often in the case of an historical landmark, interesting spot, etc. but there is not really a trail system there, I usually try to scout the location first and find a couple of hiding spots before I even begin creating the stamp/box. This enables me to see what I am working with -- is the only hiding spot a very tiny place that would require a microbox? If I can't find a good hiding spot at the location I want to put the box, I will sometimes require the user to go there and obtain information (i.e. numbers from a placard, etc.) and then send them to a nearby park where there are trails and places more appropriate for hiding a letterbox. I will then work in the information that they were required to gather at the original location as part of the clue.

The type of clues I end up writing is driven by the location, the box theme and my mood at the time -- most often a combination of all of the above.

SpringChick

Writing the general driving directions at home, I take the compass, box and tablet with me to fill in the coordinates.
