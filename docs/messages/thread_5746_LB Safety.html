LB Safety

From: Carol (cjosada@hotmail.com) | Date: 2009-03-21 04:51:27 UTC
Today, I received an email from a new boxer who found one of my boxes and attempted to find another letterboxer's box too, but she and her buddy were totally shaken up by their experience. They just weren't sure if this was part of the hobby, but felt they needed to ask someone, or warn others. Here is what they said.
--------------------------------------------------------------------
..we tried unsuccessfully to find (name of letterbox.) We felt we did everything right. It started in a park that was in a bit of a questionable neighborhood, but seemed safe. It took us on a 30 minute hike along a path that had a river on one side and a steep hill with a fence on the other. This made us a bit nervous as there were areas that looked like they were inhabited unfortunately by homeless groups and we realized there was no escape route if we needed one. The clues took us under...an underpass filled with the same type of things.

The area we ended up searching for the box was full of trash, clothes etc... Soon there were men on the bridge and the only stairway to get us out of there without taking the trail back. We immediately gave up and racewalked back the way we came. If we had any idea that this was a scary area, we would not have gone. Should there have been sort of suggestion in the clues that you should go with a large group or bring some mace?;) We are two very smart girls, but got really mad thinking that a fellow LBer would have us out in this area! What if my 65 yr old aunt and mom decided to go on this hike? Don't get me wrong, we are up for a challenge, but we felt this one was a safety issue.
--------------------------------------------------------------------
I did not disclose the name of the box or the placer because I'm not sure how to address this issue. I am glad that these two new boxers used their common sense and got out of the area.

I'm glad they asked me for advice. I'm glad they spoke up. So now I ask the members of this group, what should be done when a new letterbox is placed in an area that is preceived as possibly unsafe?
They may be new boxers, but they are concerned enough to look out for all of us and our safety.

Beetle

From: xxxxxxxx (BrighidFarm@comcast.net) | Date: 2009-03-21 07:05:38 UTC-05:00
It's not just that the homeless can see them as "targets of opportunity",
it's also that homeless people can become extremely possessive of the very
few possessions that they do have and might think that boxers were messing
with their stuff, and think the boxers were seeing THEM as targets of
opportunity.

Also, statistics say that about 1/3 of the homeless in the northeastern
Illinois area are homeless because they have psychiatric
issues..........schizophrenia, manic/depressive, etc. Something a stranger
may say or do might trigger an episode for them. That number may have
changed now due to the number of people losing their jobs and ending up
homeless, but there are still plenty of emotionally disturbed homeless
people out there.

There is one boxer who's actually one of the original boxers in the Chicago
area who places boxes in some pretty bad areas. More than one of their
boxes can potentially cause problems of safety. But, lots of boxes out in
the wild are potential problems. Look at the fact that for awhile, people
were being mugged and beaten up in the northern suburbs in some preserves
that normally serve some very wealthy neighborhoods, and there are often
lots of people walking, jogging, biking. People were being attacked right
in broad daylight. And a totally deserted forest preserve that looks like
just totally natural forest could be harboring a serial killer waiting for a
victim.

I don't place boxes in either Cook County or Kane County anymore because of
scarey experiences we've had ourselves. Probably total overkill f'sure
f'sure, but I don't feel safe going to check on the boxes for maintenance
after what happened when searching for other boxes in those counties.

I think the huuuuuuuuuuuuuge mistake those boxers made was in not trusting
their gut. That's the first thing ya gotta learn. Always trust your gut.
If it feels bad OR looks bad........GET OUT. Absolutely no box is worth
getting one's body damaged over. And carry a big fat whistle where ya can
use it right away. I keep one looped on my belt that I can grab right away.
But my eyes and my whistle never take the place of my gut instincts.

~~ Mosey ~~

Re: LB Safety

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2009-03-21 17:26:52 UTC
I would tend to agree with the posters who advocate NOT placing a warning on the box. It is every letterboxer's personal responsibility to determine for themselves, based on their personal abilities, comfort level and the prevailing conditions if a letterbox is safe to pursue and to turn around at any time during their adventure if they are not feeling safe.

To place this responsibility onto the placer and to place warnings on boxes will only serve to set a precedent and people will come to expect that if seeking a box is questionable in safety, the placer will warn them ahead of time. This encourages people to rely on that forewarning rather than trusting their own judgment.

ANY box can be unsafe given the right conditions -- weather, construction, activities in the area on a certain day, or even the seeker's own ability and limitations. It would be impossible for a placer to anticipate all conditions that may or may not be encountered on any particular day and in many cases could actually warn when none is needed.

In this specific situation, the seekers expressed their discomfort with this box early in the hunt and as Mosey points out, made the mistake of continuing past that point.

Nothing in life is 100% safe, but the vast majority of the time trusting your gut instinct is the best barometer and your best defense when it comes to your own safety.

SpringChick
