From: Gabrielle Coil (gmc81@randomkitty.net) | Date: 2005-04-07 18:30:09 UTC-07:00
>1) I was wondering what people put in their personal

>log books with the stamp, a date found? A little

>journal entry? Just curious.

I usually put the date and city that the box was found in and sometimes a little blurb. If I picked up a hitch hiker I also note that next to the box stamp (or, if I don't do that I will note which box I found a HH in next to the HH stamp). But it's really all up to you what you want to put in your log book.

>2) I got 2 stamps today that were in "businesses".

>Are there any rules for placing a box at a business?

Other than getting the business owner's permission, not really.

>3) Are there any places that absolutely taboo for

>placing boxes?

>I have some great ideas for a cemetary series, is that

>too weird?

The only places that come to mind as being off-limits are national parks (doesn't stop some people, but it is actually illegal) and private property or other such places that the public doesn't have access to. I don't think that a cemetary series is weird at all...in fact one of my favorite series was a Holloween series that was hidden in two different cemetaries. I might have some reservations about making it a permenant series, especially if you don't have permission from the cemetary management.

From: Kathi LK Krankoski (krank@ameritech.net) | Date: 2005-04-08 10:23:40 UTC-05:00
Some more answers (as usual, I type a message based on reading it in a
digest, and then forget to look if anyone else has already responded with
the same thing directly on the list!):



1. I think there is variation on this one. Some get very elaborate, as
they stamp onto an index card (or similar) and then later turn it into a
scrapbook page. Others just stamp and go, while yet others may add a
comment or two. Whatever youd like to do is perfect for you! I minimally
do the date and location, but sometimes add extra comments. I use a small
daytimer type 6-ring binder for my log, so I can move, remove, or add
pages at will.



2. Were you by any chance referring to my boxes placed in businesses?
Those I definitely got blessings for, particularly since one must ask for
them by code name. Ive done three that way, and all have been very
cooperative and enthusiastic about it, though I must admit I was a little
disappointed to hear that the owner of Grumpys threw away the cup container
I made for the box, since he felt it was unnecessary.



3. I think the answer here is common sense. There are many boxes hidden in
cemeteries, but only when done tastefully and respectfully. You dont want
to lift a fallen tombstone in a historical cemetery to hide a box, but if
there is a tree nearby a grave, that would be okay. Even if you dont have
any particular thoughts about the sanctity of a spot, remember there are
those out there that do. You dont want to give letterboxing a bad name!

Absolutely taboo? About the only things here I can think of are
government property or high security areas. In these post 9-11 days,
security is much tighter. Base of the Sears Tower? Not a good idea. You
dont want bomb squads to have to investigate your suspicious looking
Tupperware (that camo tape will do it every time!). In my earlier, more
nave days of boxing (wow, last year!) I saw several cool spots on the
campus of the VA med center where I work. Duh. We have federal officers
posted at the gates. Uh, dont mind me, officer, Im just looking for the
letterbox FungusWoman placed on the grounds here. Dont think that would
reflect well on my performance evaluation!



Hope this helps!



--klk/FW, who also has lots more important things to do (like tax returns!)
but would rather think and talk Lbing!
