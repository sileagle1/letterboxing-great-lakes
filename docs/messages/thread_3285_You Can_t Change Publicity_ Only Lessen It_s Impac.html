You Can't Change Publicity, Only Lessen It's Impact

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2006-11-07 01:40:08 UTC
I've read all of the posts about the article in Livingston county.
While I agree that the article was irresponsibly written, isn't that
the nature of the media? They are always going to look for a scoop -
- try to find the story under the story, look for the unsuspecting
newcomer or glory-seeker rather than formulate their story from
the "media information page" on the web site. I don't think we can
change that. As the hobby grows and strays further and further from
what is was even a couple years ago, publicity is only going to
continue to be the fuel on the fire.

What it comes down to is making your letterboxes less vulnerable to
threats such as irresponsible, uninformed newbies, publicity
recruits and vandals. How? Well at the risk of sounding like a
broken record... longer hikes, more difficult clues and less
accessible clues are all ways to put a buffer between your boxes and
the casual letterboxer. I know there are those who cannot do long
hikes and there are those who don't want to "figure out" clues, but
this is letterboxing. If you take a look back at the earliest boxes
in the U.S. and the clues of the early players like Mapsurfer and
Der Mad Stamper -- you will find no gimmes or drive-by letterboxes.
Rather you find cryptic clues, puzzles, brain teasers and hikes up
the side of a mountain -- and what's more -- many of those early
boxes are still in place, unharmed, unaffected by publicity and
careless new letterboxers. The gimme clues have evolved over time
as people want more boxes, quicker, easier... I am sure there will
always be an abundance of drive-by and follow-the-direction boxes so
the hobby will never exclude folks who look for that, but if you are
a serious letterboxer and really want to protect your boxes, there
are ways to better the chances of survival.

Most people who read an article and jump up off the sofa to go look
for a letterbox do not set out on a 3 mile hike or spend a week
figuring out a cryptic puzzle clue before heading out for that first
box, rather they grab the clues that give quick fixes -- drivebys, a
short walk in the park type boxes. Take this one step further -- if
95% of the clues out there were longer hikes and puzzles, etc. a lot
of people who think it sounds like "a really fun thing to do with
the family" but "only want to do quick and easy boxes so the kids
don't get tired or disappointed" would probably never even bother.
We as a letterboxing community encourage a casual, irresponsible
attitude by casually planting boxes that took little thought in the
first place.

Of course there are always going to be exceptions, but if somebody
does take a week to figure out a puzzle clue before they rush out on
a 2 mile hike for their first box, I am thinking this person kind of
gets it from the start.

Sometimes the answers are found in history...
