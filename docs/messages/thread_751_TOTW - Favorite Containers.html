Good topic!
First, for `standard' size letterbox containers, I'm currently using
Anchor-Hocking "Pop Top Storables". I use the 3-cup size (.71 liter)
and they come in a three-pack at K-Mart. Admittedly, I use them
because they're pretty reasonable priced at about $1.25 each.
Sometimes I paint them; sometimes I use cammo tape. Most often I just
leave them in their plain plastic state.
My passion, however, is for the smaller mini-boxes. For all of my
hitchers and minis I use what American Science and Surplus calls
a "Specimen Container".
It's a small plastic, transparent cylinder/bottle which holds 100 ml
and is 2 7/8" x 2 " dia".
It has a screw-on lid and I love them. I often cover them with
camouflage tape.
I have placed them and then immediately had trouble finding them
because they blend in so well with their surroundings. Source is
American Science & Surplus (www.sciplus.com) and the catalog number
is 28848. They're six for two bucks.
And, BTW, cammo tape is available at Gander Mtn (and others) in at
least three different variations including `hardwood green', etc.
I've also used plastic 35mm film container size bottles with screw-on
lids also from American Science & Surplus (item #26751). Those are
ten for two bucks.
I've used those tiny ones for bonus boxes mostly also with cammo
tape.

I have no affiliation/connection with American Science and Surplus
other than being very pleased with their stuff.

If you have questions about any of these suggestions, I'd be happy to
help.

azobox

Here's my shameful little secret:
For a very long time, I was utterly lazy and but boxes in those gladware disposable tupperware thingies. Yup. Sad but true.
I learned that 1. critters chew through these things very easily 2. cold shatters them pretty quickly and 3. there are a whole lot of better ways to box up your stuff out there.

I also, for a while experimented with tins, Altoid tins to be specific. However, not being scientifically inclined AT ALL I neglected to coat the TIN with any sort of RUST INHIBITOR. Do you know how fast those suckers rust in a Michigan fall? Gack!

I have also used Peanut Butter containers, washed and rinsed and bleached like crazy. Those work ok. I don't know why I don't just go out to the store and buy more of those rubbermaid containers and cammo tape which have proven to work so well....I guess, like I said, I'm just lazy.

Ok, I feel much better now that I got that off my chest.
Del

Being new to the hobby, I don't have much in the way of useful experience on this topic. My current favorite container is the Aero 4 cup microwave containers...3 for $2 at Dollar General or about $1 each in the dollar section of County Market. From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-10-10 21:25:53 UTC-04:00
I've used quite a variety of containers -- at first anything I could get
cheap, usually from the $$ store. Although I've had pretty good luck with
most of them, the problem is that you can never depend on finding the same
kind again.

Over time I've kind of settled on a couple of favorites. For regular sized
letterboxes I prefer the Rubbermaid Seal N Saver -- either the 1.2 quart
rectangle or square. They are the ones that have a blue band around the
outer edge of the lid. They are quite heavy duty and the lids seal really
tight. They are a little pricier than some, but are worth it in my book --
WalMart has them for about $1.69 regular price. A while back I lucked upon
a bunch of containers just like this at Big Lots (a cut-price close-out kind
of place). They had the Rubbermaid name on them, but the lids had green
around the edges instead of blue -- very cool. And they were priced at only
.79 each. I bought all I could get but have used them all. I haven't seen
them again at Big Lots or anywhere else for that matter -- seems they green
edge lid was a fluke or has been discontinued or something. One of the
other reasons I like them is because they are clear and you can see the
contents. I think that helps anyone who might stumble on the box to get a
peek at the contents and perhaps will keep them from tossing it out as
trash. This is also the reason I don't paint my letterboxes. It is true
that painting them helps them to blend into the surroundings better, but I
think that they are more apt to be regarded as trash also, especially once
the paint begins to wear off -- they can get looking pretty nasty. I
suppose it just means I have to find better hiding places so the clear white
plastic doesn't glare when the sun hits it.

There is another Rubbermaid kind I use as a back-up -- with a top that has
kind of a foldover edges and a big lip in one corner -- not sure of the
name. The tops used to be dark blue and I have seen some in white, but now
everything I am seeing is a light blue color, which would not be my
preference. These don't seem to seal as tightly though.

For small boxes and a couple of HH I have gotten hooked on the specimen
containers that Azobox was talking about from American Science Surplus. I
can't seem to get my stuff into a container any smaller than that (coming
from the girl who packs two hefty suitcases to head to Chicago for the
weekend).

SpringChick

I'm so glad to hear they work well. I snagged some a few weeks ago after
my students did a lab on hydra, figuring they must be reasonably
waterproof since they ship marine specimens in them. You've given me all
the more reason to look forward to getting live specimens for the kids!
I washed them out and left them on my patio for a few days and so far no
interest from the wildlife or the dog, so I guess they don't smell
interesting. Now I just have to carve some stamps.

For microboxes, I like to use Fisherbrand centrifuge tubes. I actually
borrowed the idea from catbead. We use them in my lab and store samples
in the -80 freezer with them, so I know they hold up in the cold. We
also run them through the autoclave, so I suspect they hold up in heat
very well, too. The only down side I know of them is that too much
pressure on the sides will shatter them.
http://www.catbeadmischief.com/microbox.html
has details on them, including ordering.

I haven't made too many normal size boxes and none of them have weathered
the winter yet, so I'm not sure what my favorite is. I think I will
continue to favor microboxes, just because they are easier to hide. One
of my boxes got vandalized and I replaced it with a microbox, in almost
the exact same place, and it has been untouched by vandals, but found by
4 letterboxers.

Katy
