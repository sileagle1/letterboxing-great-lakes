From: lesann847 (l-fischer@northwestern.edu) | Date: 2004-06-13 17:26:33 UTC
I just want to say what an incredibly talented group you all are. I
learned so much.

Delirium's log book is in a class by itself--she's taken such care
with the selection of the beautiful book itself, the calligraphy, the
narrative, the mounting of the stamps and the layout of the
pages. I have never seen anything like it.

I was impressed by the altered book examples, both "May I Have
a Word with You"--great idea!--and the well executed "Bring Me a
Unicorn"--beautifully detailed stamp and the book choice and log
section were superb.

The event stamps were incredibly witty and funny; I didn't know
there could be so many variations on a theme. Other non-event
boxes added to the fun.

The Vampire series by the Dragon and Team Taco rocks; just
what I love--a long thematically based series characterized by an
engaging story and clear clues, talented carving, delightful
logbooks and boxes. Everything spiced with cleverness and
whimsy.

For an hour we had an intent group with sharp implements
huddling over their little pieces of plastic while SpringChick led
them through a carving workshop.

We got a first-hand look at one of Azobox' library microboxes
before he places it. Matt the Rat brought a tupperware box with
magnets screwed to it. FungusWoman brought lots of
centrifuge tubes for everyone, so expect lots of microboxes to
spring up throughout the Midwest. There were so many
interesting things on the display tables, I won't reveal them all.

Golden Boy said the food was delicious and there was more
than enough to eat--and that's saying something for him. He
said he may just come to the next meet for the food.

We all have the Dragon to thank for his organizational efforts; he
even took a significant chunk out of his day to run up to
Milwaukee and back to retrieve the tee shirts the vendor
delivered late. Dragon scouted many park options, planned and
publicized events, carved stamps and planted boxes, and
functioned as general troubleshooter. I'm sure I speak for us all
when I say how very grateful we are, Dragon. It was a great time!

I don't have a strict method of selecting boxes. When they are out of
state, the title has to catch my attention first. I've only hunted for
one Michigan box so far (I promise to remedy that this summer or fall).

careful of lions, tigers and bears and SNAKES. I will make exceptions ...
I still have to hunt for the first Indiana letterbox, Corbeau.

overnight, or that I can't return before dark sets in.

to find "some day."

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2006-07-03 21:05:20 UTC-04:00
Good question! So many boxes... so little time ;-) In a situation like
this, I use the following criteria (may not be the most objective way to
choose, but not sure there is an objective way).

1) Boxes by placers I know and have personally corresponded with
2) Unique boxes, WOM boxes, boxes with a reputation (i.e. a Mapsurfer or
Legerdemaine box)
3) Boxes whose placers have a reputation for nice stamps and for
maintaining their boxes
4) Boxes/locations that are of interest to me (i.e. do you like urban
boxes, lighthouses, long trails, mountains, covered bridges, lakeshore,
historical places, mysteries?)... this is especially true if it was a place
I wanted to visit and photograph anyway.
5) Series or several boxes located within the same place or a small
geographic area

I tend to be more of a "quality" vs. "quantity" type boxer, so hike distance
or terrain difficulty is not a factor to me. I would rather spend all day
hiking to the top of a mountain to score a Mapsurfer box for which I
struggled on the clues for 6 months than collect a dozen stamps from so-so,
run-of-the-mill boxes that were located 18 paces off the parking lot of a
rest stop.

I don't generally use the LbNA status reports to sway my decision whether or
not to look for a box as I feel they are very misleading and personally know
of a lot of people who don't ever log their finds, so just because a box
does not show any recent finds, I would not let that deter me if it meets
some of my other criteria.

It is also a good idea to post to the regional list for the area you are
going or e-mail somebody in that area that you may know or have exchanged
e-mails with before to ask for input as far as "don't miss" boxes in the
area you will be traveling. I know of more than one person who has WOM box
clues that are sent to people who will be traveling to her area and ask for
input on the regional list.

Have fun!

SpringChick

RE: [LbGLK] New TOTW

From: Debbie Kotlarek (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2006-07-06 00:35:09 UTC-05:00
Well, since we like to hike, we first look for the longest, most challenging
hikes when we are traveling to an area. We especially like mountain hikes.
These are then our highest priority. We then also print clues and maps for
almost all the other boxes near the good hikes. This allows us to find
other boxes while in the same area, or gives us alternates if the weather
isn't too good for a long hike.

We also solicit advice from the regional message boards in the area we are
traveling to. People will often post a response, or email us directly, with
"don't miss" suggestions, or info on boxes they think are missing. We will
often contact people in the region privately to ask if there are certain
planters in the area that use store-bought stamps. If so, we usually skip
these boxes. (The regional lists are also a good source of info about local
restaurants.)

Generally we end up with a fat binder of clues & maps, a LOT more than we
could actually have a chance of doing, but it then allows us options. We
appreciate people that include a distance or time estimate in their clues,
since this allows us to make a better choice about a hike that will fit into
whatever available time we have.

Wisconsin Hiker

Coffee Beans and I have listened to much of the advice given here about
carving set-ups and have taken notes.

We currently have a nifty head-band type magnifier. The lights on it
are rather dinky, so we also have a small desk lamp that we shine
directly over the carving media as we work. Using both, we get a good

TV (usually a show that the watcher likes but the carver doesn't!)

Coffee Beans and I have listened to much of the advice given here about
carving set-ups and have taken notes.

We currently have a nifty head-band type magnifier. The lights on it
are rather dinky, so we also have a small desk lamp that we shine
directly over the carving media as we work. Using both, we get a good
detailed view of the image without nasty head-shaped shadows.

We tend to do it on one of our folding TV tables while the other watches
TV (usually a show that the watcher likes but the carver doesn't!)

For most of my carvings I work at the dinning room table. I have an over
head light and a stand alone light that is in the corner, so there are no head
shadows.

Though, I have carved here at the computer desk . . . the lighting isn't as
good but it works.

I've also carved while laying on my stomach on the bed . .. this hurts my
back though.

I can carve with the tv or radio on, however, I don't carve until the kids
go to bed --- can't get two seconds without one of them wanting to talk to me.
While I can talk and carve at the same time I always want to make eye contact
and give them 100% of my attention, so I just don't carve until they are in
bed.

I don't have a magnifying lens, as the years tick away some day I will need
one.
