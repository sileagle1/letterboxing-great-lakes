From: lesann847 (Acorn@fools-errand.org) | Date: 2005-11-17 16:42:24 UTC
Wool socks are good, yet I find synthetic hiking socks just as warm
and they keep their shape better; I usually add thin synthetic liner
socks--wicks away perspiration and keeps blisters from forming (I
wear these in summer too).

My mother bought Golden Boy a heavy duty windbreaker and wind pants
which I promptly appropriated. I even wear them downhill skiing.

Lots of layers underneath--depending on how cold it is, silk long
johns and top (not at all bulky), hiking pants, turtleneck, fleece
vest.

When I packed and camped overnight in northern Wisconsin last winter
where temps were below -20 degrees in the morning, I also wore a
wool sweater. A little bulky, but nice and toasty.

When letterboxing, I wear wool gloves with the fingertips off; they
have a pocked that flips over mitten-like for the hike between
boxes. $5 at REI. When it's really cold, I can add mittens over
these.

At 04:43 PM 11/16/2005, you wrote:
>I'd like to propose the next topic - How do you keep warm on long,
>winter hikes?
>
>Specifically, clothing that allows you do move and stay warm in the
>more northern states.
>I am trying to provide "no excuses not to box" to my silent boxing
>partner. This year I
>want to be prepared and not look like the Michelin Man.

We really enjoyed going snowshoeboxing in CT last winter, so the snow
and weather won't keep us from getting outside!

Think layers -- it's better to have layers that you can remove as
needed to keep yourself warm and dry. If it's snowy out, you need to
keep yourself dry.

Avoid cotton close to your skin, since it just absorbs moisture.

Silk (or polypropolene) long underwear are good because they let your
skin breath and aren't bulky.

Waterproof (not just "water resistant") boots are a must in the snow.
I have a pair of winter boots with liners, but my regular hiking
boots would work fine for me in most conditions, I think, except for
very deep or very wet snow. Wear a pair of sock liners and a pair of
heavy wool or synthetic blend hiking socks to keep your feet warm. I
have gaters that are great for keeping snow out of the top of my boots.

We also have rain pants that work pretty well in the snow, going over
pants and long underwear without being too bulky. You can really come
to appreciate waterproof pants, since kneeling in the snow when you
stamp into a box is often unavoidable.

A winter parka can be fine, but make sure it's water and wind
resistant if you'll be out in the snow. And if you're exerting
yourself, a parka can trap heat inside and just make you sweat. I
often just wear layers, topped off by a fleece and a shell, except if
it's really *really* cold.

A hat is important since you lose a lot of body heat through your
head. Warm and water-resistant gloves are also key, and the problem
often is that you're often taking them on and off to stamp into
letterboxes, and digging in the snow which means a lot of melted snow
on your gloves, so I don't know the best solution here. We both have
glove liners, which are thin gloves that fit inside a warm pair, and
these work pretty well -- I can often handle stamping in while
wearing them (which I wouldn't be able to do in a heavy pair of
gloves). Maybe a pair of big mittens, the kind that open up and let
your fingers out, would be a good solution. I have some polar fleece
gloves that I like, but they're not waterproof, so I take an extra
pair with me. Even gloves that are waterproof can get waterlogged
after digging around in the snow, so a good pair of gloves is really a must.

We also take a small tarp with us, which is handy to spread out on
the snow or ground to keep us from having to sit or kneel directly on
the ground.

SD

From: Howling Coyote (coyotespaw@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-11-18 10:35:42 UTC-08:00
1. I agree: layers, layers, layers!!! My preferred brand is CuddleDuds, they carry several weights, light silk, heavier for winter, etc. I wear them skiing too, or just sitting around the house. I know JC Penney carries them, not sure who else.

2. Sock liners rule! I have a bunch of "wicking" liners that keep my feet warm and dry. Then I wear wool socks over them.

3. Pants - I don't even own jeans, they're way too cold in the winter. I prefer the lined warm-up pants, the kind that have the nylon outside and a soft cotton/flannel type lining inside. I've worn these skiing too if the weather is not TOO cold, of course you can always add that layer of long underpants underneath.

4. My Columbia ski jacket - great for cutting the wind.

5. I won't buy hiking boots anymore unless they are Gortex lined. It is totally worth it to have shoes that will keep you feet completely dry while hiking through a shallow creek. My boots are about 10 years old, and have duct tape around the top where my dog chewed them up when he was a puppy, but they are soooo comfortable I can't part with them!
