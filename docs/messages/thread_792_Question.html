From: Delirium (donaldson_home@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-10-21 09:27:31 UTC-07:00
Gross.

I, for one, am so violently opposed to the unbearable amount of marketing that we're barraged with everyday that I find it repugnant to attach it to a hobby like letterboxing. I've heard reports of corporate magazines suggesting that retail stores plant a box, just to use it as an avenue for more advertising. We are literally unable to get away from advertising (even my public school is no longer a commercial-free zone) without putting up a fight- I would hate to see letterboxing become a vehicle for more commercialism.

Maybe if there's public land near your intended site you could use that? Reference the nearby private property in the stamp and image, but plant it in an area that won't make you accept such an offer.

Cheers,
Delirium, who fights with her principal every day about the commercials in the school-provided student planners.

I have stumbled upon caches while out letterboxing--and have also
gotten reports of people finding caches while searching for one of
our plants (or seen notes from cachers in our logbooks). Unless you
know about the other hobby and check their websites for locations of
plants, you are not going to know if there is a box near yours. And
even if you would check their website, it might not do you any good.
If I checked the geocaching site for a certain area's caches and saw
all those coordinates, it wouldn't do me any good since I don't have
a GPS and have no clue where the cache is. Likewise, if a cacher saw
the clues for a letterbox, they might not have a clue where it is
since it probably doesn't list coordinates.

I don't think most letterboxers have the opinion that geocachers are
out there maliciously destroying letterboxes or purposefully taking
the stamps. It is just upsetting when a stamp is taken when you have
a big "Not a geocache!!" label on your box--and the person doesn't
take the time to read that and so takes the stamp that you slaved
over carving.

BirdWoman/Waltzing Pigs

From: xxxxxxxx (BrighidFarm@comcast.net) | Date: 2009-01-09 04:10:21 UTC-06:00
My main problem with geocaching is that in letterboxing, the clues, once
solved, generally take a person directly to the box. In geocaching, the
coordinates only take a geocacher to the VICINITY of the box. Some
geocachers, in their exuberance to find the box, upend too much of the
environment during their search. Which can be damaging to the environment.
To me, things then change from playing a game to vandalism.

I'm not saying that all geocachers are like that. But I've had some bad
personal experience when watching some geocachers attempt to find their
geocache boxes. Personally, I believe in disrupting as absolutely little of
the environment as possible.

~~ Mosey ~~

From: placedenfert1 (placedenfert1@yahoo.com) | Date: 2009-08-28 11:05:06 UTC
When the Great Lakes Gathering was held at Afton State Park in 2008, a local ranger contacted some of us to request that forms be submitted "after the fact". In that case, the boxes had been planted as part of the gathering, and left as permanent boxes, so they already had locations when we filled out the forms.

But I can tell you that the ranger who pulled the boxes (initially) and helped facilitate completion of the forms was really helpful as far as providing coordinates for the box locations. (That was another field on the form.)

If I recall correctly, you really do need to be able to give a specific location for the box, because they then approve the location. I went through a similar process in McHenry County, IL, and had to submit the form three times, suggesting different spots before they ultimately approved one.

So it would be a good idea to find out if there are any areas that the park considers off limits due to conservation or risk concerns. Those were the things that caused some of my previous forms to be returned, and that resulted in back-tracking on my part.

But I do think that the MN form requires an exact location.

Lock, Shock and Barrel
