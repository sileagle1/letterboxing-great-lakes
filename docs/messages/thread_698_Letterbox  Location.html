Letterbox Location

From: merlinandardea (rhesse34@hotmail.com) | Date: 2004-09-23 12:52:18 UTC
We have come around to the view that location is probably the most
important thing to consider when placing a letterbox. There are many
times we have wondered how a letterbox has survived due to the poor
location it's in. We've found a number of LBs with great stamps
and/or beautiful logbooks that seem to be just waiting to be stolen
or vandalized.
Well, that said what do we look for when placing a letterbox. A
place...
1. that is away from casual observation. A spot off the beaten path
that may require a bit of honest orienteering to find. That
way "curious folk" grubbing around in the brush are less likely to be
noticed by passersby. Gather your courage, get off the trail, and go
into the woods. (A - "Mosquitoes and chiggers and ticks - Oh, my!" M-
You forgot, "Poison Ivy and spiders and skunks --- Poor Baby!")
2. that hides the letterbox well. Grotty, little boys can be, and
usually are, anywhere and everywhere. (M - Having been one, I know.
A- No further comment.) A tiny part of a blue and/or white box
peeking out from under leaves is a beacon. (A- Now be honest, that's
helped us locate many boxes. M- Well, OK.)
3. that protects the letterbox. Rain and snow are obvious, but in
one park they were using prescribed fire as a management tool and the
LB was in a hollow log. (A- Luckily the LB was on the side of the
trail/fireline that hadn't been "managed". M- Old ecological
maxim: "Every community need a crisis.") We have also found boxes
that have been perilously close to streams and lakes. One with an
orange stamp pad had been flooded. It was winter and the inside of
the box was an orange popsickle. (M- Yeah, and I got orange hands
extracting the stamps.)
4. where it is easy to rehide the box well. Sad to say some LBers
just don't seem to spend the time to do a good job of rehiding the
box.
As to urban letterboxes... well, there have been a couple really
good ones (one here in Milwaukee, for instance), but most are
obviously existing on borrowed time. It seems a shame to spend all
the time scouting out an interesting area, dreaming up and carving
the stamp, writing and posting the clues, and getting all the bits
and pieces together and then basically throwing it all away with
careless placement.
So, we feel that location is directly related to the longevity of
a letterbox and the fun/satisfaction of finding it.
Merlin and Ardea
