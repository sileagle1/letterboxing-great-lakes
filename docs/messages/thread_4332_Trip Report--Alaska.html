Trip Report--Alaska

From: cdem_ltrbox (wp4@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2007-08-06 15:37:45 UTC
Just got back from a wonderful 2 weeks in Alaskabeautiful weather,
gorgeous scenery! Lots of wildlifebut not enough birds. I was
hoping to see 50 species so I could qualify for a "Wings over Alaska"
certificate, but I came up a few short. But I did add 15 birds to my
life list, so that was really good! We did manage to do some
letterboxing in the midst of all of our other activities (biking,
museums, kayaking, shopping, hiking, sight-seeing, mile-long zip
line.).

It seems like everyone talks about seeing the "Big Five"bear, moose,
caribou, wolf, Dall sheep (we saw everything but wolf). But we also
had some other critters we wanted to see. We decided to do a
letterbox series on the OTHER big five: sled dog, eagle, musk ox,
salmon, whale (and we did see all of these). I think we managed to
put them in some interesting or scenic places, but weren't able to
place them on a long hike. We did 3+ hours of hiking in Denali, but
that was off-limits since it is a national park. Plus, there are no
trailsyou just goso that would have made clues rather difficult.

We flew into Anchorage and rented a car for a week. While we were
there, we met Alaska Homeschoolmommy and her family for ice cream.
We've never met up with boxers while on vacation so that was nice.
On our way out of town, we tried to find a park with a series of
boxes. Unfortunately, the clues didn't give the name of a parkjust
said it was near something. We found the something, but couldn't
find the park. Oh well. We did find the Thunderbird Falls box at a
waterfall on the outskirts of Anchorage. Then on to Wasilla, where
we left the Sled Dog box near the Iditarod Museum/HQ. On our way to
Fairbanks, we stopped at Hurricane Gulch (gorgeous view) and
spontaneously decided to leave the Bald Eagle box there. But the box
wasn't put together yet. So I stood at the car with the trunk open,
putting the box together and stamping the image into our logbooks
while the rest of the family scouted out a hiding place.

Left our next 2 boxes at Bird SanctuariesI love being able to
combine 2 hobbies. We left the Musk Ox box in Fairbanks (they have a
herd at the university there). Hoped to find a brand-new series in
Fairbanks, but the rain came at just the wrong time for that. As we
were leaving Fairbanks, we stopped to see a section of the Alaska
pipeline. At the display, they showed a device called a pig that is
used to clean out the pipeline. Right next to this metal pig, they
had a little statue of a dancing pigso we weren't the only waltzing
pigs in Alaska. Then back to Anchorage, where we left the Salmon
box. We were able to find a few of Alaska HSM's boxesplaced during
a Midnight Sun gatheringwhile we were there. We got to the first
box and were looking through the logbook and saw the Doglvers stamp.
We were thinking "Wow, they must be up in Alaska this summer too".
Then we realized that Alaska HSM had launched a postal out into the
wild, but just kept using the logbook from the postal. So we weren't
surprised when we saw P. Granola in the next box.

Then it was on to our cruise. We placed the whale letterbox at Icy
Strait Pointour first port call and a good place to spot whales.
There was a nice little trail through the cedars. Actually, this
would have been letterboxing heaven. I have never seen so many
uprooted trees and stumps in such quantity. We were able to find the
one and only letterbox in 2 other port callsone in Skagway and one
at a totem pole park in Ketchikan. Now it is back to home and I need
to find the time to plant the 2 Underground RR/Quilt boxes that I
have all ready to go.

BirdWoman/Waltzing Pigs

From: (stampinpam@aol.com) | Date: 2007-08-06 23:49:55 UTC-04:00

Thanks for posting the details of your wonderful trip! I made my first (and
hopefully not only trip) to Alaska in May. Ours was an Inside Passage/Glacier
Bay cruise, so I didn't get to experience the beauty of inland Alaska. I
hope to make that my next trip! I, too, found the letterbox in Skagway. That's
the only one I could fit in.

Many of you are probably already aware that you can collect the National
Parks Passport cancellation stamps at national parks, monuments, trails, etc.
Most of the stamps look like postage cancellation images, with the name of the
site on the stamp, along with the current date. On my Alaska trip, I did seek
out some of the national park stamps. I was surprised that most of the ones I
found had images that differed from the traditional cancellation. For
instance, Misty Fiords in Ketchikan has an image in the shape of a shield, and within
the shield outline is a very nice rendering of a bald eagle flying over the
water. The Tongass National Forest image has a totem pole, a bear with a
salmon it its mouth, and mountains. Because you cruise the waters of Glacier Bay
when you visit, the park ranger who boards your cruise ship brings the stamp
with them when they come on board. (You do have to locate the ranger on a large
cruise ship - is that similar enough to letterboxing to call it a find? )
It's an image of a whale surfacing the water with the glaciers in the
background. And the Klondike Gold Rush NHP in Skagway has an extra commemorative
centennial stamp that shows the silhouettes of gold miners hiking up the steep
mountain pass, carrying their heavy equipment loads on their backs. In
addition to stamping my parks passport, I made extra images on blank pages in my
journal so I can use them for cards, scrapbooking, etc.

I know these National Park passport stamps aren't the same as finding a
letterbox, but for me it's still fun to collect them. And the unusual ones I found
on my Alaska trip makes them even more special as mementos of the trip.

Glad you had such a great time in AK. Now I'm ready to start planning the
next visit there, and it will include letterboxes, of course!
StampinPam
