From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-10-08 19:11:34 UTC-04:00
Sorry about your box... it is hard to lose one, especially a favorite. I think you bring up some really good points:

The hiding spot wasn't "that great" -- not necessarily the reason the box is missing, but we've all been there... having a place in mind for the box and getting there and not really being able to find a "great" spot so you just take what you can find and sometimes it works fine and other times it bites you.

But more importantly...

Your comments about finders not being discreet, boxes exposed and paths being worn to the box, etc. I have seen this so often and it seems increasingly more and more. I don't want to point the finger at *new* boxers because I know there are some very conscientious new boxers, just as there are careless boxers who have been at it long enough to know better.

We did some boxing in the U.P. the past week and a half and I really had to shake my head at some of the things we found. At one of my boxes, there was a well-worn path straight to the box, even though the hiding spot would have been accessible from many other routes. I am frankly surprised the box was still there. At a couple of boxes (not mine) that were hidden behind trees with the telltale pile of sticks, the sticks had been carelessly tossed in the general direction of the boxes, but no care had been taken to actually cover the boxes and they were laying completely exposed. A couple of my boxes had been placed inside large outer plastic bags (for no apparent reason -- the container was intact) and were completely soggy inside. One box we found inside a clump of stumps had been just set back in place with nothing placed over it, even though the clues very specifically indicated to be sure and cover the box well and there was plenty of appropriate covering material (bark, leaves, etc.) nearby. And the most unbelievable (get this) -- at one box a finder had stamped with fruity ink (it was very obvious from the smell -- blueberry maybe) and surprise!! the smell attracted a critter and the box had been chewed and what was left of the stamp was in crumbles.

At one time I was convinced it was just a matter of education and if we really put effort into educating people about letterboxing practices, then we would help to create more contientious boxers. But letterboxing has grown so quickly and so out of control -- more people letterbox who do not frequent the talk lists than those who do. Many people who access LbNA and AQ for clues don't ever take the time to visit any of the other informative pages on those sites, or even realize the information is available. I'm not sure that education is possible -- you can lead a horse to water, but you cannot make him drink.

One thing I do notice though is that the boxes that most often are found is disarray, at least for me, tend to be the easier boxes -- clues and/or hikes, and boxes in high-traffic areas where perhaps finders do not have ample time to discreetly cover the box when they put it back. So is the answer perhaps boxes in less accessible areas, longer hikes, more difficult clues? Of course, it stands to reason that a box with more difficult clues and a longer hike is *probably* going to see fewer visitors and thus the probability of being left exposed or having a worn path is far less likely. I know that some people don't have the time, inclination or ability to do longer hikes or tougher clues, etc. But for me it has become a matter or priorities. I would rather have fewer quality finders and increase the chances of my boxes surviving longer than tons of finders who have no clue what the word *discreet* means. I said to OldShoe just a couple of weeks ago that I am making my boxes much more difficult, both in terms of clues and hikes. Just ask him -- a couple that were planted last week are really tough. I realize that many people will pass these boxes by, or not even take the time to figure out the clues, because they require more time and effort than some want to put in -- seems like everyone wants instant gratification these days. But I am confident that there are enough true boxers out there who will look for and appreciate these boxes -- not to mention, the stamps are well worth the effort. I am also confident the chances of these boxes surviving are pretty darn good. I am even considering reclueing some of my existing boxes to use codes and puzzles rather than just straight-out clues.

I really don't know what the answer is, and what works for one person may not work for another. I certainly feel your frustration though.

SpringChick

Today is a sad day for me, for other reasons. I was contacted through
LbNA by a Muncie, IN (Delaware Co) news reporter who is planning to write
a piece on letterboxing and wants to know if I would be interested in
helping. I responded I might consider it if I could remain anonymous and
that no letterboxes would be exposed without owner's prior permission.

She responded today that she could not grant me that privilege (only
victims and special cases warrant that special treatment).

Given the fact that Muncie is the home of Ball State and the few boxes
that have been placed are now retired/missing/unknown, the likelihood that
new exposure would bring would endanger any new placements. This is also
the town were a geocacher placed something behind a downtown business near
a dumpster. A worried citizen called the police and the geocache was
detonated.

The box through which she made contact is Pickle Chiffon Pie. Probably,
geographically closest to Delaware County. If I don't participate, she'll
likely find someone else, and I can't think of many others that would have
the interest and longevity in our area to make sure the article doesn't do
more damage than good.

What do I do? If I agree, I may ask that my trail name not be associated
with my "real name." Maybe I ask her to solve SNICKERS, and then, and
only then, will I help. I think I'll ask her to check out the
Letterboxing Dissertation by 2bentriders. After all, it was done through
BSU.

Please post your comments. I am so conflicted.

~speedsquare

This is a very tough call. As you point out,if you don't work with the
writer, odds are that she'll find someone -- perhaps less experienced
and less informed -- to interview.

On the other hand, her requirement that you be identified by name
almost seals the deal in opposition to a cooperative venture.

There are more and more of these situations coming up.

At least two other boxes in the GL region are facing exactly the same
dilemma right now.

Can you talk with the writer "off the record"? (make SURE she agrees
that your comments are not for attribution!)Meet with her at some
coffee place and double check your instincts.
Tell her that you'd like to talk with her about letterboxing and that
you can both see where the story might go from there.
Perhaps during that conversation you can make the points about stealth
and mystery and then steer her to another boxer who is willing to be
outed.

Your instincts of fear for your own boxes can't be ignored.
Remember, you do not have to answer a reporter's questions.

Good luck

azobox

if you don't mind.

From: Kathi (krank@ameritech.net) | Date: 2007-10-10 04:40:49 UTC
I am in the same predicament, speedsquare. I too have been recently
contacted by a reporter for "The Doings" (I am not familiar with that
publication. She told me it is headquartered in Hinsdale, but covers
Darien, Burr Ridge, Willowbrook, Indian Head Park, Western Springs,
LaGrange, La Grange Park, Hinsdale, Clarendon Hills, Oak Brook, Oakbrook
Terrace and Elmhurst.) Here's the message I sent her. (She first
contacted me via LbNA's "contact the placer" for "On my Honor", a
LaGrange, IL box.) "Would it be possible to see what you end up
writing to verify the accuracy of your article? No offense intended,
but the amount of press letterboxing has gotten recently has introduced
a lot of people to the activity. While that is not a bad thing in
itself, many articles do not stress the need for researching the
protocols and conventions. As a result, people go out and misunderstand
what they are to do. Boxes are damaged from improper handing, or worse,
lost to vandals when not hidden as they were intended, ruining things
for all. Much of the press presents letterboxing as a "game" to be
played by parents and children, when in actuality, it is more of an
adult activity that children can participate in under the supervision of
letterboxing-knowledgable adults. For these reasons, I'm willing to
work with you on your article, but ask you be cautious of what you say.
Please do not show any letterbox hiding places in any photos you may
include, or mention where any boxes are hidden or even the titles of the
boxes. Do not show stamp images without the permission of the person
who carved it (a letterboxing etiquette thing - some people do not want
their images seen by anyone but those who have found the box). If
someone is interested in pursuing letterboxing, they need to find out
that information via the websites where they will first learn the
correct way to participate. That being said, if you have not already
done so, besides looking up letterboxing.org, I suggest you read up at
atlasquest.com. (check out "about letterboxing" and "code of conduct"
in particular under the "home" dropdown) There's a lot of excellent
information to be had. Let me know how else I may be of assistance.
--FungusWoman (oh, and please don't use my real name in your article,
just my trailname)"
I have not yet heard back from her (message was sent on the 6th).

============================

I know Ryan has some links directed towards press on AQ, but perhaps
what letterboxing needs is something similar to what I noticed
Bookcrossing does (this is off their October 4th "News and Whatnot"
e-newsletter). We need an official Letterboxing Press Secretary! (-:

"What To Do When The Media Comes Calling


As BookCrossing grows in size and becomes a bigger player in the world,
we'd like to try to channel interview requests through our headquarters,
so that consistent information is being given to the press. If you are
contacted by a member of the media, please encourage them to contact
Scott Sorochak, our CEO at scott AT bookcrossing.com or via a private
message through his BookCrossing shelf (redsoxbookguy
). We love that
our members are so excited to share the word, but want to make sure that
the most current information about BookCrossing goes out to the media.
If you let us know you sent them our way and an article results, we'll
give you wings for helping us out, as well. (And no, this is not an ego
thing with Scott we tattooed "humility" on his arm last
weekend...) "

Controlling the press is nigh unto impossible and the intentionally unorganized structure of letterboxing only makes that more difficult.
