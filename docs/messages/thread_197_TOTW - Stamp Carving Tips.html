From: Deb (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-05-11 12:27:13 UTC
Well, I think like many others, my first stamp (Chinook Pier) read
backward. I wish now I would have kept it, but at the time I just
was thinking how could I have done that? and quickly carved a new
one.

When I carve, I do the most difficult parts first -- for me this
usually means the lettering because I hate doing letters. Then I
move on in order of difficulty... Since it is more likely to mess
up on the difficult parts, this way I wouldn't have as much time
invested should I need to scrap it and start over.

Shading is always tricky when hand-carving. A couple of ways I have
had [moderate] success with shading are using various sized sewing
needles (can also use paperclips and other pointy objects) and
poking many closely-spaced holes into the area to be shaded. This
usually requires several test stamps to get the effect I am looking
for... Remember, you can always take more off, but you cannot put
anything back.

Another way of shading that I learned from Ruthann Zaroff, is cross-
hatching. Using the #1 Speedball tip (the small "V"), lightly drag
it across the area to be shaded in parallel rows, and then drag
perpendicuarly across these cuts. The trick is applying the right
amount of pressure -- too little and you don't get anything, too
much and you risk cutting away pieces of the carving material. But
when done correctly, it gives a very nice effect. One little trick
with this is to do the cross-hatching BEFORE you carve away the
areas around it. This gives a more even effect and also eliminates
the chance of hacking off a piece of the outline.

One last thing -- in regards to not being able to put it back if you
mess up or carve off too much... I have had some success using
super glue to fix boo-boos. I dab it onto the rubber with a
toothpick and then put back the cut off piece, being very careful
not to get glue on the surface of the stamp (ink will not adhere to
super glue). Not ideal, but really beats carving a new stamp if the
error is small and you have a lot of time invested.

SpringChick

Yeah, I hate letters, too. And I know that I read someplace to do the
most difficult parts first is wise for another reason. There is apt
to be more carving material surrounding the smaller parts earlier in
the process and thus they have more support alongside the very fine
cuts.

Another tip I learned from SpringChick at last year's Gathering is
this: When you think the stamp is finished, it probably is.
It's time to quit. I think we've all taken "one more tiny cut" that
we wished we hadn't.

From: debrocks54 (deb.maertens@stpaul.com) | Date: 2004-05-12 16:29:44 UTC
I cut away from the design when ever possible. I also try to
move/rotate the carving material as much as possible, instead of
moving the carving tool around. Doing it this way I can keep the
carving tool facing away from me (and my other fingers) and control
the design a bit more. I try to raise up the carving material on
something that it will stick to temporarily (like a small acrylic
block). The block can then be rotated around on the table.

I also use a strong light and magnifying glass. As I get towards the
end of the carving process, I do test prints using a darker ink
(Adirondack) that washes off the stamp when done.

Deb

If you're carving an eraser, a sawed-off section of a wooden paint
stirrer makes a perfect backing board for it. I simply use hot glue
(or as my wife and I call it, The Martha Gun!) to glue them together.
Most hardware stores will hand out paint stirrers for free as their
logo is printed on them.
