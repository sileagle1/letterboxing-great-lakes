Treasure among the trees

Group enjoys art of 'letterboxing'

By JANE NORDBERG
Gazette Writer
COPPER HARBOR Barbara Oxford takes a reading of 41 degrees, walks
41 paces into a forest, submerges her hand into the hole of a dead
log and gingerly removes her quarry, a plastic box.
The box contains a rubber stamp, an ink pad, and a notebook filled
with imprinted marks and messages from residents as far away as
Chicago and as close as Torch Lake.
"That was easy," she says, spreading the box's contents on the
forest floor before making her own stamp in the box's notebook, and
using the stamp in the box to document her discovery in her own
notebook. "Sometimes it takes me an hour or more to find it," she
says.
Oxford is a letterboxer, part of a growing breed in the U.S. Part
sport, part treasure hunt, letterboxing is said to have started in
England 150 years ago when a Victorian hiker inserted his calling
card in a bottle, threw it into a Devon pool and issued an
invitation to those who found the bottle to add their own card.
Recent articles in "Time" and "Smithsonian" magazines have spurred
the hobby's popularity. Since 1998, over 9,000 letterboxes have been
deposited in public places throughout the U.S. There are also
documented letterboxes in Africa and Central America.
According to the main letterboxing Web site, Letterboxing.org, there
are 46 letterboxes hidden in the Upper Peninsula, including four in
the Copper Country: two in Keweenaw County, and one each in Baraga
and Houghton counties.
Lured by the "Hwy 41 Letterbox" in Copper Harbor, Oxford followed
the directions on the Web site given by Tim Kuehl, also known
as "The Dragon."
Kuehl, who resides in Wisconsin, has placed 68 boxes around the
Midwest. During a trip last year to Michigan, Kuehl placed boxes at
Canyon Falls in Baraga County and two at Copper Harbor.
Anonymity is key; letterboxers take great pains to avoid being seen
discovering the boxes, and they carefully hide it again for the next
person to find.
Many, like Kuehl, take on personas to hide their real names.
Yooperann and Funguswoman are just two letterboxers who may have
Michigan connections.
Although the instructions Oxford followed were fairly
straightforward, she also likes the challenge of the solving more
difficult clues.

[The Mining Gazette online version ends here, but from our
EagleHerald print copy, I'm including the following additional
text. There may be more text in the Mining Gazette print copy.]

"Some people make you do math, word puzzles, or figure out lyrics
from certain songs," she said.
The inexpensive, accessible nature of the sport encourages a diverse
following. Its combination of solving puzzels while being outdoors
is attractive to families.
Other than a stamp, a notebook, and a compass, letterboxing requires
no equipment to start out.
