From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2006-06-08 09:36:53 UTC
Letterboxing.org (LbNA) and AtlasQuest are the two public letterboxing
clue sites -- where anyone can go and post their letterbox clues. In
addition, you will find clues (this is by no means an exhaustive list):

- Letterboxers' personal web sites and blogs (see Silent Doug's site
for a listing of letterboxer sites -- http://www.letterboxing.info/)
- Files, Links and Photos sections of letterboxing Yahoo! groups --
for instance this group (a list of other LB Yahoo! groups can be found
in the Links section of the LbGLK group or at Silent Doug's website)
- Posts at letterboxing Yahoo! groups
- Inside other letterboxes

There are two other places where you can dig through the archives and
perhaps find references to boxes in your area that for whatever reason
are no longer listed on the main clue site (but sometimes are still
active):

- The old letterboxing.org website... about 3 years ago,
letterboxing.org was completely redesigned as the site we know today.
At that time it was necessary for all letterbox planters to relist
their own clues at the new site. For whatever reason, some people
never did -- some were no longer actively letterboxing, others just
didn't take the time to do it or just didn't know they needed to,
etc. This site (http://lbna.dyndns.org/index.html) contains a
*snapshot* in time -- clues as they were before the new web site. It
can be fun poking around here -- notice on the home page, there were
only 4648 letterboxes listed at the time.

- The letterbox basement... This is a Yahoo! group
(http://groups.yahoo.com/group/letterbox-basement/) where the
webmasters dumped unclaimed letterboxes at the time of the transition
to the new letterboxing.org website. Each box is listed as a post to
the list and they are loosely grouped by state, although some are
randomly floating out of place. This is a bit more time-consuming,
but can yield hidden treasures.

It can at times be like looking for a needle in a haystack, but don't
exhaust yourself over it. The best thing to do is keep your eyes and
ears open and you will no doubt stumble across lurking clues every now
and then.

In addition, some clues are distributed via WoM (word-of-mouth). This
is kind of a catch-all for unpublished clues selectively passed from
one letterboxer to another via U.S. Mail, e-mail, or direct handoff
verbally or written. How do you get WoM clues? It varies from one
letterboxer to another. Sometimes it just takes time, participation
and proving yourself. People will sometimes plant a box or series and
then send out clues to other active letterboxers in the area based on
interactions they have had -- people they have met at gatherings, e-
mail correspondance and list posts. I know of one letterboxer who
sends a little WoM clue out to folks whenever they send him
informative *contact the placer* messages. I know of another person
who does not list their clues on any public clue site, rather
publishes a small clue book and sends it via U.S. Mail to people in
her area and remote contacts she has corresponded with. One person I
know has a series of unpublished boxes that he selectively distributes
clues to once he feels a person has reached a certain level in the
game -- they have proven themselves in his eyes to be discreet and
conscientious (yes, this is completely subjective, but they are his
letterboxes and he can elect to hand out clues based on any criteria
he chooses). Still others may list the name of an unpublished box
publically, but indicate that a person must request the clues. Many
people hand out WoM clues at letterbox gatherings -- not just for
boxes at the gathering, but other boxes in the general area or the
area where they live. WoM clues are not necessarily meant to be
exclusionary or *top secret* (although some are), rather placers often
use this means of clue distribution for boxes in sensitive areas or
boxes where they otherwise want to know or have control over who is
searching for their letterbox. If you have heard of an unpublished
box that you would like clues for, often just sending the planter an e-
mail and asking for the clues is all it takes.

SpringChick
