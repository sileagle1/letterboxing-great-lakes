Getting Permission for Placing Boxes

From: Mary Lou Toomey (toomey@genevaonline.com) | Date: 2004-07-07 21:57:46 UTC-05:00
I'm new to letterboxing and have had a lot of fun finding and, more recently, placing boxes. I've taken the kids with often (they are 7 and 9). Here's the thing - my husband insists that I should ask permission wherever I place boxes, including county parks and other public grounds. He says even if they're public, someone still owns or runs them who might object to having a letterbox placed there. In theory, I agree, but don't think it's a big deal. How do some of you handle this situation?

That's actually a good second queston of the week, so to speak.

Personally, if it is on public grounds, I don't ask. If it is on
private grounds, I definately ask. Either way, I also take into

even want it to be on their radar screen. That is why if I plant a
box in a state park here, I go to great lengths to make it so they
cannot find them via an internet search. I've gotten the impression
from other letterboxers in this state they basically feel the same
way. Am I right? Let me know!

Re: Getting Permission for Placing Boxes

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-07-08 10:24:26 UTC
That is certainly a subject with good arguments both ways.

I would have to agree with Tim... private land, there is no question
that permission should be asked and you should also note that in the
clues -- that this is private property and permission has been
received for letterboxing. Sometimes people can be hesitant to
tread onto private property looking for a box if they are not aware
of the circumstances.

As for public land... I have always been on the feeling that it is
better to ask forgiveness than permission. I view letterboxing as
an acceptable use of public lands and therefore do not feel the need
to ask permission before planting a box. Many time the folks in
charge of these places are not aware of what letterboxing is and
rather than take the time to learn about it, they feel it is easier
to just say "no" than risk setting a precedent for allowing
something they are not familiar with. In many cases asking
permission, actually forces them to make a policy -- which may not
always be in our favor. Whereas, just going about our business,
taking care to be environmentally conscious, nobody is getting hurt,
and most of these public lands remain without a specific policy one
way or the other.

Now... the situation gets sticky when the public lands have
specific policies. National Parks have a policy against letterboxes
and geocaches. State Parks in some areas have policies requiring
permission and/or placement fees. In these situations, you need to
use your judgment.

In any case, discretion is a key factor. As Tim pointed out -- if
we stay low enough below the radar, we can just peacefully co-exist.

SpringChick

Re: Getting Permission for Placing Boxes

From: azobox (jparkerg@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-07-08 11:50:59 UTC
My first reaction was to go into lots of pros and cons. Then I saw
that our colleague SringChick had outlined in good detail most of the
arguments that have bounced around the letterboxing world.
I would only add that there is a more radical (?) element who suggest
that since WE are the public, that "public" land should be open to
OUR use, including National Parks.
And so those folks feel that with a caveat of "leave no trace" and
embracing a practice of environmental concern, letterboxing should be
an acceptable activity in the parks (policies to the contrary
notwithstanding).
There seems little argument about private land.

Personally I find myself in SpringChick's camp on this one.
I have encountered park rangers who are happy to have letterboxes
in "their" parks, and who encourage letterboxing as another way to
get people outdoors and into nature.
On the other hand, as SpringChick reports, there are those, who if
asked, will default to a negative, fearful of creating policy or
precedent.

I suspect that enforcement of existing policies varies a great deal
even within a park system.

Obviously this is where the placement of some boxes where the clues
have limited distribution comes into play.
And as the Dragon alludes, the description of places becomes more
obscure.
