From: xxxxxxxx (BrighidFarm@comcast.net) | Date: 2008-12-03 16:38:28 UTC-06:00
Similar lineage here (on my father's side) up in the Ladysmith area, and
yes, used to always hear the word. From what my father said, it was a
Depression Era word.

When cows no longer produced enough milk for it to be worth keeping them,
they were slaughtered and, being old dairy cows and on the tough side, were
generally turned mostly into hamburger. Folks made their own bread and
butter and grew their own onions, so one of those sandwiches made for one of
the cheapest "dinners" around. Kids were often sent to school with those
sandwiches for lunch because there wasn't money for anything else. I'd hate
to have to be sitting next to them for the rest of the day, with that odor.

My grandmother also used to give them out to any "hobos" during the
Depression who happened to be riding the rails and stopped at the farm door
asking for a little food. My father was always proud that my grandmother
never turned anyone away. A body always at least rec'd a sandwich.

I don't know if that'd be a good thing or not tho. :-)

At one time, it was actually illegal to sell and buy margarine in Wisconsin.
Wisconsin was, after all, a dairy state.

When I was little, we used to drive down here to Illinois in the station
wagon to just over the state line, buy a huge load of it, cover it all up,
and bring it back up to northwest Wisconsin to all the family. Kind of like
all the folks from Illinois who cross over into Indiana just to buy the
BigBang fireworks.

Dad hated to drive but Mom always insisted that he drive the return trip
home because she was a speeder and was afraid that if her lead foot on the
accelerator got away from her and she didn't realize how fast she was going
and we got stopped...............oops!

State troopers were often on the lookout for margarine coming across the
border.

Times have changed.
