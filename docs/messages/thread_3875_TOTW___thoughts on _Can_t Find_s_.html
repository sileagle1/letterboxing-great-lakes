TOTW...thoughts on "Can't Find's"

From: tiptoetonto (tiptoetonto@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-03-19 14:12:58 UTC
We've all come up against a "Can't Find" in our own travels..and for
those that plant, an equal amount of contacts saying the box was
elusive and has to be missing. Both accounts conjures up
frustration....sometimes leading to sheer madness! We've been the
recipents and the accusers.

We had a tendency early on in our letterboxing "careers" to be very
quick at coming to the conclusion that the box was indeed
missing...We were certain we covered every angle, nook and cranny.
We dutifully contacted the placer expressing the regretable
news...sometimes leading to a box being noted as missing. We did
this more than once only to find out that humble pie doesn't taste
too good. The boxes were eventually found safe and sound.

This is when we contemplated changing our trailnames to the "Pathetic
Boxers" (which we still use as a nickname to our trailname!)...But,
instead we decided that this is just a fact of life with
letterboxing...We now may let a placer know we attempted it but
couldn't find it, but never use the word "missing"...We have learned
to always give the box the benefit of the doubt...not sure about you,
but quite frankly our searching skills can be questionable.

We say all this, having found over 500 boxes in the wild.
We've missed some of the basic and easiest (in others opinions)
boxes...and yet there have been boxes (like Funhog's Happy Javelina
in Tucson) that very few (except Pete & Wanda) were able to find that
we were able to find with ease. It all depends... As more seasoned
letterboxers, we still get stumped....and rewarded.

We have found that the higher our letterbox count goes,
our "letterboxing ego" becomes less...I guess one could say that the
letterbox has put us in our place.

We are wondering how the rest of you see it? What is your default?
The box is missing or ...are you missing...it?

From: Doodle & Deedle Bug (doodle_n_deedle@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-03-19 07:29:23 UTC-07:00
That we're missing it. We got to experience that just
yesterday! We were looking for a box, and somehow
ended up coming from the opposite direction that we
were supposed to go (but I guess that's what happens
when it's muddy and mucky and still some snow on the
ground in parts - the trails are a little hard to
discern; I believe we ended up taking a creek bed as
the trail we thought we were supposed to follow!). We
were finally able to recognize some of the landmarks
listed in the clues and retrace our steps, but darn
it, there were a LOT of 2 foot by 2 foot moss covered
stones underneath trees in that area, and we just
weren't able to find the one the clues were referring
to.

A lot of times, if we're certain that we MUST be at
the hiding place in question, since we always take our
camera with us we'll snap a few photos of the "hiding
place" where we couldn't find the box and email those
to the placer. That way, even if they don't get back
to us, we hope the photos help them determine if the
box is truly missing or if we were just waaaaaaaaaaay
off and then they get a good laugh.

Although I do admit that often, if we've tried to find
a box and were unsuccessful, and then later learn that
the owner has since marked the box as "missing," it's
a little bit of a reassurance to us - an, "OK, it
wasn't us, it was the box, we weren't completely off
mark with no idea where we were going!"

Doodle Bug.

From: Atom 118 (hersh118@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2007-03-19 10:04:08 UTC-05:00
Yea...I think we gave up with the taking pictures....We did it once only
to find we were WAY off...it didn't do much for our morale....so we've
stopped doing that.

If we are searching for a box, we still usually default to a "THE BOX IS
THERE".... however, when we were newbies we would wander around in
circles for a long time (and maybe throw a few fits) before
giving-up...now, we look around and suck-it up after a shorter while
(although we probably do a more efficient search that we did when still
newbies). When a trip proves unsuccessful (and particularly later when
we find that the box WAS there), we find it helps to remember some of
the tricker boxes that we HAVE found. Oh, and we still manage to miss
some 'easy' boxes...sometimes our experience works against us as we
over-think an otherwise straight-forward clue. I must agree with Doodle
Bug...we feel much better if others report a box missing that we
couldn't find.

If the box is ours, we tend to default to "IT MUST BE MISSING"....and we
are overjoyed if we find that it is actually still there. (BTW, please
don't tell us a box is missing just to make us happy!)

From: azobox (jparkerg@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-03-19 15:25:45 UTC
Oh Tiptoe, I think you've nailed it precisely.

"This *has* to be the spot"...therefore, since *I* can't find it, the
box *must* be missing.

There is a growing humility that parallels one's "F" count, I believe.
Another factor to consider is one's state of mind at those moments of
failure. More than a few of us, when coming up empty at the site,
have taken a break, had a bite to eat, or even just took a needed
water break, only to find ourselves with clearer heads and a new
perspective on where the box might be.
Lo and behold, suddenly the clues seem much more concise, the
landmarks make sense, and (sometimes) 'There's the box'.

I was recently corresponding with a friend in the East who, with
others, was a first-finder on a box that had been in place for six years!
I'll bet more than a couple of previous seekers just figured it was
missing.

My default is most often, "I guess I just can't find it."


azobox

From: Coffee Beans (coffeebeans06@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-03-19 11:10:39 UTC-05:00
In addition to what Atom has said, I wanted to add that we often just
jump to conclusions with the clues. For example, as soon as we see "a
stump on the right side of the trail next to a pile of sticks" like the
clue says, we assume that we're at the correct stump and the correct
pile of sticks. However, the clue might also say something like "just
after a slight incline in the trail," which we've overlooked (hey, it's
hard to hike and read at the same time! :P) So we'll be digging around
in totally the wrong spot for about 10-15mins, and just when we are
ready to give up, one of us will reread the clue and be like, "hey, the
trail has to incline first and look up ahead -- it's inclining!" So
then we run ahead and find the real stump with the real pile of sticks
AFTER the incline.

Reading and rereading the clue is key since many times the clues are
subject to interpretation. What one person refers to in the clues as
"sacrificed trees spanning the river" might be a couple of logs that
fell/were chopped down and now lie across the water, OR it might
actually mean the wooden bridge that's up the trail a bit further. Even
if the clue isn't that detailed, things still might sound totally
perfect where you're at, but if you walk ahead another couple hundred
feet (or even half a mile), you might find an even BETTER place that
also matches the clues perfectly. Another thing I've noticed to keep in
mind is that the distances between reference points in clues can vary
quite a bit depending on the planter and/or just the length of the hike
-- sometimes reference points are every 100 feet, but other times they
can be every quarter mile.

All in all, as we've found more boxes, we've gotten much better at
reading and interpreting clues and therefore finding more boxes... now
that's a great cycle to get stuck in! :)

Coffee Beans

As a relative newbie (about 50 finds), there have only been about 7

missing something and couldn't find them. 2 we were sure were
missing.. But, in all of the above, when we've contacted the placers
