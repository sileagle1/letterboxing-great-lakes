This afternoon I sent an e-mail to both Katrina Cravy
(who I worked with when I was an intern at Fox 6) and
to Vince Condella expressing our concerns with giving
letterboxing such exposure on the local news. I
explained the negative effects this "over" exposure
can have on the hobby and asked them, for the sake of
the letterboxers who would like to preserve this
hobby, not have their boxes go missing, be vandalized,
etc. to please reconsider doing the segment.

Who knows if it will do any good, but at least they'll
know there are some letterboxers out there who are not
happy to see letterboxing have such publicity.

Re: This just in....

From: junk4joann (junk4joann@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-02-09 04:33:53 UTC
LibraryGrrl here. :)

I thought the news piece was just fine. Honestly, it was fine. She
showed mostly her own Sig stamp being stamped and the box was her box.
There were maybe 6 people, or so, in the group. It was educationally
done and it didn't expose anyone's boxes. No names of the park
location were mentioned. I think some legitimate people may think of
checking out letterboxing so that they can use a compass and count paces.

If you're interested in my larger opinion, read on.

This is my first time getting in on this annoying thread topic, but
I've passed it up a few times now... so I'll just say everything I
want to say and that will be that. :)

Regarding hearing about letterboxing in public:
Most of the people I know who letterbox are in this hobby because of a
piece done on public radio, which led to a friend finding out about
the hobby, which led to her recommending it to us. The piece, which
you can still hear online, was nearly identical to this one in the
sense that they followed some staged clues to a box planted by the
interviewee. Seems fine to me. No radicals are going to jump on this
hobby-wagon.

Regarding secrecy, in general:
The act of letterboxing does require stealth to a certain degree, but
it is not exactly a secret society...unless of course the Knights
Templar were actually letterboxers... which would be pretty funny. I
bet the great pyramid eyeball conspiracy of Masons is actually a
dollar bill clue to a giant letterbox!

It's just a stamp in a box (a metaphor(?) for your enjoyment):
This reminds me of when I first became a librarian. Every new book was
my baby, needing my protection. But now I simply wish to share the joy
of books and reading with as many people as I can. I want people to
take the books home! I know that they'll use them as a coaster. I know
the dog will use them as a chew toy. It is possible that before that
happens someone will actually read it... and become a Reader some day.
I choose to think of my stamps the same way. At first they felt like
my babies that needing protecting, now I want to share them. It seems
as though letterboxing sometimes feels like a baby needing protection.
Or a possession to be kept behind glass. I am suggesting that as
letterboxers we shouldn't fear exposer. We might just want to share
our hobby in the hopes of someone become a capitol "L" Letterboxer.

People, especially kids, need guidance:
Mostly I'm in the woods by myself finding a box. And that's just fine.
If someone wants to show a group of kids a worthwhile hobby that I
love, that's super. At least they're not standing outside a school
with a baseball bat calling their friends to a brawl on their cell

I think there is a difference between articles in
various publications and those broadcast TV...even the
article on NPR leaves the listener with a sense of
mystery. Articles in magazines, while fairly widely

what the magazine normally has to offer. ie. a hiking

stealth is neither said or implied (the large

finding their own box...but we have to remember that
most of our (the group) boxes are listed on one of the
two big sites for letterboxes. I'm sure that we will
get many new letterboxers from the exposure that could
be good for the hobby/sport/obsession. But with each
exposure it does sometimes seem that there is a rash
of missing boxes afterwards. Could be coincidence..I
don't know. Even if more people are aware of the
hobby...such as the FBI as someone mentioned, it
doesn't mean that they will respect it. So I guess we
all just walk the tightrope of harder clues to keep
the boxes visited less easily, but hopefully often, or
easier clues that keep the boxes easily accessible but
also more susceptible to damage from either
carelessness or vandalism.

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-02-09 22:22:12 UTC
I am a little befuddled at her response to all of this and felt the
post at the big list was kind of a sympathy ploy to garner support
after-the-fact and exaggerated the backlash she received from this
list. It really is too bad because I did not think the comments I
read here were nearly as negative and nasty as she said, rather
there were some very valid and insightful concerns and opinions
expressed, from all perspectives.

I noted that in her post she made a point to say that she had
stressed being discreet, however I have watched the story several
times now and have seen no mention of being discreet or rehiding
boxes well at all. Publicity is a double-edged sword for
letterboxing and along with that people have very valid concerns
when it happens, particularly when it happens so in-your-face
visually.

While many people do not especially like publicity, they do realize
it is reality and the best we can do when it comes knocking is to
handle it responsibly, to offer experienced letterboxers who stress
the aspects of education, being discreet and rehiding boxes, etc.
But anyone who actively seeks out publicity has to expect there are
going to be many people who are less than supportive and
appreciative of that action, particularly when that person is
somebody that very few people know and things such as being discreet
and rehiding, etc. are not stressed, or in this case, even mentioned.

SpringChick
