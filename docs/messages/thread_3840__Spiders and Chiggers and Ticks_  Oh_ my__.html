"Spiders and Chiggers and Ticks! Oh, my!"

From: Merlin and Ardea (rhesse34@hotmail.com) | Date: 2007-03-14 16:19:33 UTC
I came across this reprint of an article in the Bird Watcher's
Digest recently and thought I'd pass in on.
******************************************************************
"Among the things I put in my pack for a day's birding, botanizing,
or just hiking are bug spray, sunscreen, requisite maps, water,
first-aid kit, snacks, water, and a lint roller. Lint roller? Well,
although I usually do get muddy, scratched, sweaty, poison-ivied,
begrimed, and chemically marinated, fastidiousness has nothing to do
with it. A lint roller helps with ticks.
The kind of lint roller I carry is covered with wide strips of
perforated masking tape, and there are many brands available at
drugstores, discount stores, and health and beauty supply stores.
The first time I tried using this tool, I'd emerged from four
hours of bushwhacking through shrubby Pine Barrens woods near
Lakehurst, New Jersey. Rolling the lint brush along pant legs,
shirt, and bare skin, I came up with several hundred deer, dog, lone
star, and unidentified ticks in a few passes.
Take whatever other tick-avoidance measures advised - long pants,
tucked in (I also duct tape mine), etc. But I haven't had a case of
Lyme disease in the five years I've used this device (and I had five
cases prior to then).
Using the lint roller in addition to taking precautions will
reduce the number of ticks brought home, into your car, or carried
on your body for too long. Peel the tick-infested strips off and
dispose of them in the garbage; the ticks can't extricate themselves
from the adhesive.
(This tip from Peg Brancheau )of Milford, New Jersey, was
originally featured in New Jersey Audubon Magazine)"
******************************************************************
The time for ticks isn't far away...
Merlin

From: xxxxxxxx (BrighidFarm@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-03-14 20:09:07 UTC-05:00
With ticks, "several hundred" might not be much of an exaggeration.

Ticks aren't like fleas. They don't do a whole lot of hoppin' around.
Ticks don't have much in the way of travel expenses to turn in to the
company.

Ticks lay masses of eggs. Depending on the species of tick, ticks can lay
anywhere from a hundred eggs in a bunch to a few thousand in a bunch. When
they hatch, they generally don't move around all that much. They often
climb a bit and lie-in-wait for a sucker to come along and give them both a
free ride and a free meal. Which means that if a person wanders into a
place where a couple of hundred ticks have been laid, quite a few of those
ticks might move quite quickly to take advantage of the free ride and free
meal ticket.

My personal best so far was in Rusk County, WI. 54 ticks in one outing. I
picked up 53 ticks. My dog picked up 1 tick. She came down with Lyme
Disease. I didn't. But she picked up her 1 in an entirely different area
from where I picked up my 53. And the person hiking with me also picked up
only 1 or 2, so I obviously just managed to wander into a nest of 'em.
Maybe that's why there seem to be so few boxers in Rusk County. :-) The
ticks got 'em.
