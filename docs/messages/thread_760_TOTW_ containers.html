Yup, me too...I'm a 50 ml centrifuge tube and/or 100-125 ml specimen cup
user myself. I've taken to camo taping the lids since I realized how
visible light blue, hot pink, and orange caps are. I sometimes throw a
strip of tape down the sides of the container itself to prevent reflection.

Logbooks become an issue...I have a large oval shaped punch which I use to
make little logs for the specimen cups. For centrifuge tubes, I use a 3x11"
strip of paper accordion-folded to fit inside. Unfortunately, a popular box
can fill up fast. That's okay for those near my house, but distant boxes
become more of a problem.

The other down side becomes lack of room for hitchhikers!

I bought some of those stain resistant Rubbermaids (similar to the blue
seal, but it's silvery grey, and the rest of the lid and bowl of the
container are clear hard plastic). I hope those work as well as the blue
seals. That's the kind I had used for Hershey's Hike, which as I noted
earlier was found all wet inside. Granted the lid was not on tight, but was
that due to weather fluctuations or the way it was attached?

The cheapo Dollar Tree container I was all worried about at Bluffalo (which
WI Hiker graciously applied an extra baggie to this summer) turned out to be
actually quite a nice box. Go figure! I must admit, though, hearing others
confess to using them make me feel better. I was under the impression that
one HAD to use Rubbermaid to be considered a "real" boxer!!!

I'm so glad to hear they work well. I snagged some a few weeks ago after
my students did a lab on hydra, figuring they must be reasonably
waterproof since they ship marine specimens in them. You've given me all
the more reason to look forward to getting live specimens for the kids!
I washed them out and left them on my patio for a few days and so far no
interest from the wildlife or the dog, so I guess they don't smell
interesting. Now I just have to carve some stamps.
For microboxes, I like to use Fisherbrand centrifuge tubes. I actually
borrowed the idea from catbead. We use them in my lab and store samples
in the -80 freezer with them, so I know they hold up in the cold. We
also run them through the autoclave, so I suspect they hold up in heat
very well, too. The only down side I know of them is that too much
pressure on the sides will shatter them.
http://www.catbeadmischief.com/microbox.html
has details on them, including ordering.
I haven't made too many normal size boxes and none of them have weathered
the winter yet, so I'm not sure what my favorite is. I think I will
continue to favor microboxes, just because they are easier to hide. One
of my boxes got vandalized and I replaced it with a microbox, in almost
the exact same place, and it has been untouched by vandals, but found by
4 letterboxers.

I have been using the Krylon Camo Paint. Have not tried the one made

way to camo the boxes, that I have seen done on several boxes here in
Pennsylvania. The boxes are wrapped in a camofluage burlap with a
rubberband around that. Wal Mart sells the stuff for about $6.oo for
a big sheet. It does a very good job of hiding the boxes and holds up
very well. It is made to use outdoors. Talk to you later,
