TOTW - Organization

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-10-05 15:38:23 UTC

Having been involved with letterboxing for a couple of years now, I
am accumulating quite a lot of stuff -- a variety of carving related
stuff, a stash of possible letterbox containers, a collection of odd
little memo books that could be used for box logs, clues to boxes
placed, clues for boxes yet to find, park/trail maps, books about
hiking trails and out-of-the-way interesting places, info on possible
box locations, notes about possible boxes/series, bits and pieces of
ideas for clues and puzzles, filled box logs... not to mention 3
filled personal log books, a photo album holding index cards stamped
with images of boxes I have placed and another album of WOM clues and
stamped postcards that letterboxing friends from other areas of the
country have sent to me. This doesn't even include the assortments
of art paper, book-binding materials and stamp pads because I had
most of those things in my art supplies before letterboxing.

Anyway, the question... how to organize all of this stuff so that it
1) doesn't take over my house and 2) is easily findable when I am
looking for it?

So for this week's TOTW, I am interested in hearing some of the
tricks that other people use to keep their letterboxing-related stuff
in order.

SpringChick

Re: TOTW - Organization

From: Timbertoes (toomey@genevaonline.com) | Date: 2004-10-05 19:00:28 UTC

Like Bess, I am lucky enough to have a sewing room at my house.
Well, it started off as a "sewing room" on our house plans, but
became a scrapbooking room before the house was finished and, because
we put our family computer in there, the computer room. Then
letterboxing came along so the sewing machine and the scrapbooking
stuff got pushed aside. Along one wall I have shelves from floor to
ceiling stocked with plastic see-through Rubbermaid boxes in various
sizes. To the box side facing the room I attach those plastic
sleeves you use on the binding of three-ring binders and on the
inserts I write what's in the box. In them I keep sewing projects,
kids crafts, art supplies, etc. - and because they're clear I can see
what's there. One of the bigger boxes holds my letterbox materials
(containers, labels, logbook materials, camo duct tape, olive-drab
spray paint and wood backers for future rubber stamps). By my work
table I have a plastic drawer set that holds carving materials,
tools, half-completed stamps, and anything I use often. In a file
cabinet by my work table I keep three folders: Placing Letterboxes,
Finding Letterboxes and Letterboxing Misc. So far the system is
working alright, but as an obsessive organizer I change things around
all the time. Case in point: since I began letterboxing in June '04,
I have changed the pack I keep my stamp, stamp pads, personal logbook
(only one so far) and brush markers in FIVE times! One of the packs
never even made it to the trail before I found something better! How
sad is that?

I find the most important thing in maintaining quick access to any
files or supplies is to file things the way you think. Seriously.
When looking for something I've often thought to myself "Where would
I have put a (insert currently misplaced item here)?" Most people
are consistant in how they think, so if it made sense to you a month
or even a year ago, it probably will today. Try it and see!

Timbertoes

Re: [LbGLK] Box Log Books (was TOTW - Organization)

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-10-06 06:17:04 UTC-04:00
What you use for the log books in your boxes is a totally a personal
decision -- as simple or elaborate as you want. The most important
consideration is the size of your box, and paper without lines is nice, but
again that is more a preference than a requirement.

I have seen boxes that use the little 3"x5" spiral pads of basic lined
paper, others that use strips of adding machine tape stapled together at one
end, index cards (blank or lined) -- spiral bound or held together in a
variety of other ways, a wide assortment of purchased unlined paper
pads/books and then handmade journals. I enjoy bookmaking and can never
seem to find the right size book for my boxes, so that is why I make most of
mine, but it is certainly not necessary. As you can see from the list
above, even art quality paper is not a requirement, although some of the
thinner papers tend to allow ink bleed-through..

A lot of people buy the small sized Strathmore spiral-bound sketch pads and
then recover or embellishment the cover a bit -- adds a touch of creativity
and gives a nice quality paper without having to start from scratch. A few
options:

400 series 4"x6" spiral-bound pad (brown cover) - 24 sheets/80 lb.
Mister Art (www.misterart.com) for about 1.50 each
400 series recycled 3.5"x5" spiral-bound pad (green cover) - 100 sheets/60
lb. Art City (www.artcity.com) for about 1.80 each
300 series 3.5"x5" spiral-bound pad (yellow cover) - 100 sheets/50 lb.
Art City (www.artcity.com) for about 1.80 each.

SpringChick

Re: [LbGLK] Re: TOTW - Organization

From: Doodle & Deedle Bug (doodle_n_deedle@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-10-06 07:09:34 UTC-07:00
One of the downsides of pre-printing the clues is that if the placer changes clues since you printed the sheet you might not realize it, which makes as you've been doing, SpringChick, printing as you go along, almost the better way to go on one hand, and actually, we're starting to lean more towards that method.

When we first started letterboxing we were a little overwhelmed by all the boxes listed for just our area, and of course we wanted to get them all, so I had originally printed all those clues to help us keep track of what we'd found already & what was still out there. Of course, then people keep adding boxes, which means more clues to then print off before you've found all the already-existing boxes....it's a curse, I tell you! :)

Sometimes if we're not sure which boxes we want to hunt for we'll take the entire binder with us, and then pull the clue sheet out once we get to the destination. This is also good if the first search was shorter than we anticipated, because then we'd have all the clues with us to find another box in the area with the time we had left. This was how we handled our Michigan trip. I mapped out all the boxes we'd considered finding on a map, and placed the clues and directions in a binder. The clues where then all placed in the binder in order of how we'd come across the locations if we started at point A and headed towards point B. We had almost 50 sets of clues, and as I've reported, since we were only there for 4 days didn't find all the boxes we had clues for, but that set-up really helped us determine, "OK, we just found this box, so which box would it be best to head towards next?"

We haven't been taking the entire binder with us recently, more-so because we've gotten into the habit of pre-planning our trips. We've found the majority of boxes that are within a short drive of our homes, so we've now moved on to the locales that will take at least half an hour drive for us to reach and a little more planning rather than spur of the moment, "Let's go hunt!" We're going boxing this Sunday, and have to be done by 4 pm (Deedle has band practice at 5), so I went online to see what in our area we haven't found yet that would fit into that time-frame and came up with 4 different "trips" we could do, some multi-boxed, some just one box with a longer hike.

What I'm leaning more-towards, rather than the binder, is a map. That way I can star on the map where a box is, and then when we've got time to box determine what searches would make a nice trip for the time we've got to box, & then print off clues that way.

A binder is good, though, to store those mystery clues that take some time to unravel. This way you can keep them next to other clues to boxes in the area for the day you can finally make that track.

Hope that helps!

Doodle.

Like SpringChick said, what you use as a logbook is a personal decision. Occasionally I get a little irritated when I find a box that has one of those little composition books (lined paper). But then I remind myself that the image I'm taking away from the box is on a paper of my choice and that if that person doesn't mind lines showing through the image I leave than it shouldn't bother me either.

As for how to make log books, that's also a personal choice (obviously driven by your personal level of experience in bookbinding and how much effort you want to put into it). I'm fond of spiral bound 3x5 cards (yes, they're lined, but only on one side). Last time I went looking for logbooks I couldn't find a good price on spiral bound note cards, so I decided to make my own. All it took was a hole punch and some binder rings with a piece of cardstock cut to size for a cover. Takes about half an hour per 50-sheet book. It's easy for me and they don't look horrible.

As for those strathmore sketch pads, the covers can easily be flipped so that the cover becomes the inside cover. Just flip the front cover over to the back and play with the binding a little to remove it, turn it over and reattatch it and you have a nice blank cover to play with. This would also work with any pad of paper that was not continuously bound. If my explination doesn't make any sense, go stare at the wire spirals of the pad long enough and you should be able to figure it out (sorry, I'm not great at explaining things).

From: merlinandardea (rhesse34@hotmail.com) | Date: 2004-10-08 03:13:12 UTC

We organize our clues with what's evolved into the "Red Dot
Method". After printing out the clues for a letterbox, we locate it
as close as we can on a map. (A map can tell you almost everything,
except how to fold it up.) "Avery" makes 1/4" adhesive, red dots
(and other colors, too) in a roll of 450: pull the paper strip and
out pops a dot. A dot is placed on the map at the letterbox location
and numbered. The number is also written on the clues. Once there
are enough dots to warrant an expedition, we're on our way. Of
course, the dots are also very helpful in planning your route and in
knowing which clues to take along.
After a trip the dots for the letterboxes we found are removed from
the map. The dots are pretty sticky and hard to remove sometimes,
but they come off easily using a Q-tip lightly moistened with paint
thinner or something similar. It's probably a good idea to try the
solvent on an unimportant part of the map first to make sure it
doesn't smear the ink.
The red dots work quite well for us, but it does require some
preparation time. And I suppose it confirms our image of
being "dotty" letterboxers.
Merlin and Ardea
