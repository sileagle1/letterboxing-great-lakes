Well, I was one of those who was interviewed at length but whose
comments and observations did not make the final cut. My sense is that
the writer DID "get it" but he to wrote me that, "It got cut
tremendously from the length I had originally turned in". The writer
was deeply backgrounded on a range of letterboxing variations and I
sensed that he understood how differently letterboxers may approach
the game. He expressed intrigue and curiosity about mystery boxes,
encrypted clues and word-of-mouth boxes. He clearly understood the
inherent conflict between the secrecy of letterboxing and the effect
of publicity on the public places in which boxes are placed.
He and I corresponded just before and after he took a cold, wet and
snowy Lb search with his photographer for a GR area series (with a the
placer's permission). When he finished his hunt he was pretty psyched
up and excited about his adventure and he seemed to feel a real
respect the deeper subtleties of letterboxing. In fact, he said that
he'd probably go back and finish the series when the weather was better.

Having said that, I think the piece is so incredibly lightweight and
fluffy that it'll not likely generate much of any new interest in
letterboxing.
I don't see content that would pique any curiosity in a casual reader.
As was said by another, the nuts and bolts of "what" and "how" are
barely mentioned.

It simply takes more than a few paragraphs to even scratch the surface
of letterboxing. All of us who have tried to tell someone else what
this game is all about know that it takes several rounds of questions
and answers to convey even the mechanics of letterboxing.

He tried; unfortunately, he didn't succeed.

azobox

Re: GR Magazine

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2005-03-03 19:04:40 UTC

I didn't really feel like it was one versus the other -- that
BindleBabe and I were pitted against each other, or that it was in
any way derogatory toward either of us. I just think he was trying
to show there is something for everyone and you can make it as easy
or complex as you want it to be -- a spectrum I would guess intended
to lure hesitant newcomers as well as geeky puzzle solvers and die-
hard outdoor types.

It only seemed logical to me that the reporter would pull the
comments he did from his conversation with me and use that in regards
to the more cryptic, mysterious aspects of the game. Although I try
to offer something for everyone (especially when things were just
getting off the ground here in West Michgan) and have several
straightforward letterboxes and shorter hikes, there are also a lot
of mystery boxes, more vague clues and tough hikes. Not to mention,
I tend to talk a lot about the "underground" aspects of letterboxing,
hoping that people will see past the tupperware and get to the heart
of the game -- but I do realize that not everyone shares that
perspective and passion. On the other hand, it makes sense to me
that the reporter pulled what he did from his conversation with
BindleBabe as she offered a different perspective to what he had been
hearing from me and she has worked very hard to place boxes that are
more accessible, putting the game within reach to many people who may
not be able to do a long hike, etc. One is not better or worse than
the other, one is not right and the other wrong -- just different
approaches. Everybody looks for something different and letterboxing
can meet that diversity of interest.

While I think he did a good job of pointing that out, I would have
liked to see less "she said," "she said" and more case-in-point,
trail talk and hands-on letterboxing talk from the author, who did go
on a successful letterbox hunt before writing the article and has
indicated he will probably go again once the weather gets nicer.

I do agree that the article as it was presented, probably won't lure
too many folks into letterboxing. Perhaps that is a blessing.

Such is editing.

SpringChick
