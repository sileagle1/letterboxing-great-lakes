From: pgranola2002 (p2jd@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2006-10-15 19:21:38 UTC
Hello to all, newcomers and old timers alike!

If you would please take a moment to ponder my words it would be
most appreciated. During the summer, in the span of 3 weeks I lost
5 boxes...this was a hard hit Two were lost due to animal
interventions and three it appears from human neglect. Since I have
invaded the animals rightful territory I can not be dismayed at
their natural behavior, however, I have become saddened by the
actions of my fellow boxers.

I replaced all the boxes, and within 2 weeks I found one of my boxes
totally exposed when I went to check on it, and another kind hearted
boxer alerted me to the fact that another one of my boxes was just
left sitting out in the open.

When I have been out boxing I have also found boxes that are not
well re-hidden, the boxes are not snapped shut, they have zip lock
bags that are not sealed shut, or have log books that have been
filled up because one family has each child fill an entire page
leaving the book with no where to stamp in.

I am here simply as a reminder that we all must be considerate to
the placers. I know the exhileration of finding a box and wanting
to move on to the next one before it gets dark, but we are ruining
the trip for those who come behind us if we are not careful.

For those with families: if you are letting your kids put the box
back when all are stamped in, then please double check that they
have done it correctly, and exactly as the placer had it hidden. I
box with my grandson and I always check before we leave a site.

Also, please consider having all members of one family stamp on one
page. It is wonderful to have so many new, excited boxers and young
families joining us, but that means the pages fill up more quickly,
and when trying to get a logbook into a small container there is a
limit to how many pages will fit. It is not always easy to get out
and change out a log book for the placers. Consider having each
member stamp only a part of their stamp, or write their names on one
page. I was recently at a box where the logbook was filled up
because one family used up 5-6 pages and there were no pages left in
which I could sign. Please look ahead and see how close to full the
logbook is before filling all the pages.

This is a great hobby when everyone does their part...

Thanks for your time and please think about my words before you send
me hate mail!!!
P.Granola

From: tiptoetonto (tiptoetonto@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-10-15 22:36:28 UTC
We can't believe P. Granola beat us to the punch...probably a good
thing...we weren't as gracious. I wrote a letter to the boxing
world at large on Friday evening after finding the last 13 of 15
boxes we've found exposed and badly re-hidden, also with many pages
scribbled on...the last entries in all of them were unfortunately
children. I canceled my letter thinking it was a bit harsh and
afraid we would come across as "family haters" - which we clearly
are not.
I think the exposure brought on by national magazines (like the
recent Family Fun article) has brought many new boxers with small
children quickly into the hobby without really explaining the
importance of stealth, care for the environment, and respect for the
boxes to be left better than when first found.

In all of the cases mentioned above we also found very worn, torn
and trampled vegetation surrounding the boxes hiding places. It is
no wonder that park rangers are becoming more and more annoyed with
our hobby and want to control our activity.

We have boxed with children and it is agreed this is a super family
affair, but we also have experienced that our very own nieces and
nephews in their enthusiasm tear into the plant life like opening a
present at Christmas. Once they have stamped in they are rearin' to
get onto the next stop and the last thing they care about is
rehiding it better than they found it. We have had to stiffle their
enthusiasm more than once and always insist on either replanting the
box ourselves or double checking their job.

Letterboxing gives parents an excellent venue to teach about good
stewardship regarding the environment, as well as, the gift that
someone gave in hiding a box to find. It is an opportunity to teach
that being alert and quiet will potentially reward the child to hear
sounds and see wildlife that are only possible when watching and
listening. Far too often we have watched as children are allowed to
run off trail beating anything in their way with sticks and tearing
up plant life all to the chuckle of the parents thinking it a fine
opportunity for their children to be...just children. Not to sound
too teachery...it comes naturally to us I'm afraid, but parents
please make sure you teach your children to letterbox
responsibly...and still have a great time in the woods!

Another issue that came our way that took us back a bit was an email
that came via "contact the placer" questioning the availability of
one of our boxes. They informed us that they wanted to take a group
of 20 to look for it next week and was it still active. Our first
reaction was...YIKES! Twenty people searching for one box on a small
property certainly will make sure that everyone for miles will know
that something is hidden at that spot! We emailed our concern to
which they responded they would be careful! I don't think we've had
20 people letterboxing in one group at the biggest gatherings! We
are concerned that 20 people at once stamping into a box no mtter
how careful they are is going to compromise the area and the box.
Having some letterboxing nightmares...
Tiptoe & Tonto

From: Carol Osada (cjosada@hotmail.com) | Date: 2006-10-16 02:18:57 UTC-05:00
Greetings tonight from Beetle

Recently, I lost 2 letterboxes too. But I placed them before the Family Fun Magazine article appeared. So I tend to think that there is always some degree of risk involved in placing a box. But I cringe when I read about all the permanent boxes that have dissapeared within the last couple months.

I agree with Oprah - 'People do better when they know better." So ,when the Girl Scout National Office posted a new badge for older girls called Hi Tech Hide and Seek on their Studio 2B web site, I thought it was a good idea to begin teaching letterboxing workshops for GS adults and older girls. By teaching them the right way, I thought that would insure that boxes would not be damaged or missing. And, I have meet some great new friends who have become letterboxing addicts (like the rest of us.) Now, they place boxes and some of them have an F count greater than mine in only a short time.

However, after this weekend, I've got a whole new opinion on this subject. I hide a set of 4 boxes with hand carved stamps at a Girl Scout campout gathering on Friday. After specific verbal and posted written instructions, only a few troops went searching for the boxes. On Sunday morning, I went to collect these temporarily placed boxes. Two were gone!!! A third box was not put back properly; it was just left on the ground a few feet away from the original hiding plae. The fourth one was put back properly too. But the two of the four boxes I found were a mess inside. The inky stamps were not wrapped in the paper I provided nor were the baggies sealed not were the boxes closed tightly. The log book was an inky mess. People stamped over other previous stamps. But they did listen when I told them to make room and conserve the pages in the logbook by stamping several people on one page and to use the back side of the sheets of paper.

Young children just don't get it and neither do some adults.

My thoughts are mixed tonight. However, I may no longer be conducting letterboxing workshops in the future.

---Beetle

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2006-10-16 21:59:21 UTC-04:00
I am personally am not a fan of the "F" count clue option at AQ as there are many people with significant numbers of finds (and plants) who simply choose not to log their finds online, at AQ or letterboxing.org. My feeling is that clues posted to a *public* letterboxing web site (AQ and/or letterboxing.org) should be available to anyone who visits that site. But, AQ is Ryan's site and he can do as he pleases with it. As there are so few people in my area that even use AQ, I really have not been impacted much by it.

All that being said, I am a proponent of WOM (word-of-mouth) clues. I know there are a lot of people who feel this is exclusionary, but not all boxes are for all people and if somebody plants a box that they feel is best suited to a particular type of letterboxer or letterboxers who are more seasoned or even more familiar with a particular geographic locale, then distributing clues via WOM and selectively via e-mail is a good way to get them to your target audience. There are plently of publically available letterboxes that nobody is left without something to look for just because they may not be privy to a WOM clue. Besides, they are fun and in my mind, one of the most intriguing aspects of letterboxing.

On another note... I have read the posts regarding missing boxes with interest and just want to throw out a suggestion. I hate to point the finger blindly at families and people who letterbox with children because I know there are many people who do so very responsibly. But having letterboxed with my young grandson, it is certainly much more of a challenge to make sure all the bases are covered... and to remain stealthy in the process.

I still believe that the most common reason that boxes go missing is because of poor placement. And that is not to say anyone is a bad planter because sometimes you have no idea that a hiding spot is a bad spot when you place a box. I am reminded of one box I planted out in the middle of a woods inside a hollow log and time after time that thing was found laying out in the open, contents exposed. Apparently the hollow log that was my *perfect* hiding spot, was home to some sort of critter and he just was not going to let that box rest inside that log. Sometimes I find boxes that are located in places where it is nearly impossible to properly hide because they weren't hidden very well in the first place.

In general (and of course there are exceptions to every generality), the boxes I have found exposed, carelessly repackaged and otherwise mistreated (both my own boxes and those I have hunted) are those that are relatively easy to find, in terms of distance, clues and hiding spot. Now I'm sure most everyone knows that I am a huge supporter of longer hikes and more difficult clues and I do realize that is not for everyone... but, I have to say that my boxes that require one to solve puzzles, climb sand dunes and hike 2-3 miles very rarely see this kind of mistreatment. Likewise, boxes that I have struggled for when finding, in terms of clues or hike, are usually in top condition when I find them. Perhaps it is because of all the effort it takes to get there, that finders treat the box with more respect, perhaps because of the types of people who are doing these kinds of boxes, perhaps it is just because the volume of visitors is less... But it does seem to provide a level of security against the more typical letterboxing problems we are seeing lately.

So in general, my feelings about improving the odds for your box to survive:

1) Make the clues a little more difficult than the standard 1-2-3 follow the directions variety
2) Put some distance between the parking lot and your letterbox
3) Be really, really picky about the hiding spot

(did I mention that you need to be really picky about the hiding spot?)

SpringChick

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2006-10-20 06:24:00 UTC-04:00
As indicated in a previous post, I don't think it is fair to blindly point at families or new letterboxers as the reason for all missing boxes. There certainly are many reasons that letterboxes go missing, including animals and poorly planted boxes, and there most certainly are many families that letterbox responsibly. However, that fact being acknowledged, it is difficult to attribute scribbled logbook pages and unbagged letterbox contents to animals. While it is true that any letterboxer or muggle *could* have scribbled logbook pages, it seems reasonable to assume it was a child. Now, it may not have been *your* child, but I think people were speaking in generalities about the increase in these types of situations, as well as missing letterboxes, each time letterboxing articles in family-oriented publications bring in a new wave of letterboxing families with young children.

While on the surface letterboxing appears to be a fun family activity, by nature it is not really conducive to boisterous, impatient children as a very large aspect of the game -- which many people do not immediately understand -- is stealth. While is is certainly possible to letterbox as families and with children and I know of several families who use letterboxing as a successful teaching tool for their children and *do* letterbox responsibly, there are many who do not. It is not just about taking a walk in the woods and following some clues to find a letterbox -- it takes extra effort on the part of a parent to teach children how to be *responsible letterboxers* and not all parents are willing to put in this extra effort.

If your family happens to be one of those that takes the spirit of the game to heart and letterboxes responsibly, the letterboxing community welcomes you and appreciates your efforts as a parent to teach your children the importance of respecting nature and other people's boxes, repackaging and rehiding properly and being stealthy while letterboxing so as not to inadventently compromise a box location.

SpringChick
