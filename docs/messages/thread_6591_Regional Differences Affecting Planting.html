Regional Differences Affecting Planting

From: sunnysideupm (drewes@WI.RR.com) | Date: 2010-05-10 00:52:48 UTC
Wow - what a "duh!" sort of surprise I got when planting in the U.P. - the trees and terrain are different enough from the Kettle Moraine in SE WI to take me aback. The evergreens and birches, plus the relative lack of rocks, do not provide the same hiding opportunities that I am used to at home. Here we have lots of deciduous trees that age/decay/rot in ways that provide lots of hiding holes, plus we have lots of ROCKS. I think I adapted fine. And I look forward to finding and planting again in the U.P. very soon!

- Sunny

From: Wisconsin Hiker (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2010-05-10 02:43:51 UTC
Well, I have to say I now have a slightly different opinion of those tree/log hidey holes. As you say, they certainly age/decay/rot. What we didn't realize when we first started planting was how QUICKLY they deteriorate! If you saw my post on the "big board" a few weeks ago, you'll know what we experienced when looking for some 7+ old boxes in Virginia. Whole logs were now gone! In the past we've also discovered that some other boxes in WI and IL(including one of own) were quite deeply buried by "tree rot debris".

Although rock piles present a challenge to us when hunting, I think a few well-placed rocks are even better than trees. I have seriously contemplated hauling some in when we are planting...

Wisconsin Hiker

From: Kathi Krankoski (krank@ameritech.net) | Date: 2010-05-11 15:28:55 UTC-07:00
Have to share this story, which is very tenuously indirectly related to letterboxing.

After the Boxacon gathering, Mr. FW and I stayed overnight at the home of an old HS friend who lives in Waukesha. As he toured us through his highly-wooded property, we came upon a headstone way in back near the property line. I recognized his grandmother's name on it. He explained that for whatever reason a new one was placed at her burial site, and he decided to take the old one and put it here just for "fun", next to a couple of marked dog graves, I might add. But the funniest part of the story was when the adjacent property was for sale and a realtor was showing it toa potential buyer and they stumbled upon an apparentmini human and pet cemetery in the neigbor's back yard!
