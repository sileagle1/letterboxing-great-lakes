The type of carving material that is best is largely a matter of personal preferance. I am a strong believer that there aren't many "bad" carving materials out there...just that some are better for certain applications. Harder material will support more detail, but is more difficult to carve. Softer material is easier to carve but may not be a good choice for very fine detail (like the Rose Gardener or March Hare stamps from the gathering). Personally, I don't have the patience for uber-fine details, so I don't really mind a slightly softer material. My advice is to go ahead and try a couple of different materials if you can to see which you like best.

Now the more important question for you is, did you get the white speedy-cut or the pink speedy-cut? Most people don't like the white stuff as it tends to be crumbly (a property I have used in a few carvings to my advantage). I used the white stuff for many of my earlier carvings. My Fish Tank series doesn't see much use right now, so it has worked fine for that. However, my first sig. stamp made out of the white stuff had many "added character" marks by the time I retired it from things banging against it in the back pack. The pink stuff is a much harder material and seems more durable than the white stuff.

Good luck on your first stamp! And if you are a bit dissapointed with your skills the first few times around...just remember that it takes practice to work up to some of the intricate masterpieces you have seen.

From: ruhlette (ruhlette@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-06-12 15:39:50 UTC-07:00
My two cents worth ...

My first carving experience was the expensive Master Carve when I made my
first sig stamp. Very crude, straight lines and so simple. I posted an
image at Atlas Quest if you want to see my first "masterpiece."

I realized carving stamps could get expensive and then I found the pink
stuff. Like that and made a few small stamps. Then I found the gray
material at a specialty art supplies store. It took me nearly a year to
use it all up, but it was highly affordable. Easy to cut and the material
is still going strong (see my favorite PT). Also made the mistake of
buying that crumbly white stuff, but I have a gorgeous celtic knot image I
will redo on PZKut soon.

I found all of them easy to work with once I figured out how much pressure
to use.

I do love PZKut, and I will probably use this exclusively now. I
purchased 1 sheet of white and 2 orange several months ago, and I have
made one stamp so far. Yes, that's correct, just one ... The Rose
Gardeners. It does make fine detail work easier. I also used a
magnifying lamp for that stamp. I think I spend about 8 hours making that
stamp (most stamps were a breezy 30 minutes or less). I also felt much
more comfortable with the exacto knife using PZKut. I was petrified of
the knife prior to carving rubber stamps.

It is a process and I hope you try all types.

speedsquare
