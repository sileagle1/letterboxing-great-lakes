`Letterboxers' are on a quest for a rubber stamp


http://www.chicagotribune.com/entertainment/chi-0610110383oct12,1,4772502.story?ctrack=1&cset=true

By Kathy Ceceri
Albany Times Union
Published October 12, 2006

ALBANY, N.Y. -- Any hobby can become addictive.
But to folks such as Turtle Run, Mookie and
others, the thought that any boulder or patch of
woods they pass might be hiding a "letterbox" can be excruciating.

Compared with most people's idea of buried
treasure, the average letterbox is usually just a
plastic food storage container -- doesn't look
like anything special. All it contains is a
rubber stamp (often hand-carved), an inkpad, and
a logbook and a pen. The fun is in the design, and in the finding.

Using clues posted on Web sites such as
www.letterboxing.org and www.atlasquest.com, avid
letterboxers travel around the area and the
country to collect stamps from boxes with names
such as "Snakes on a Grave," "Where the Elf
Hides" and "The Sagamore Canoe" for their own logbooks.

They can be planted anywhere from a chink in the
side of a brick office building to the top of a
mountain, and may involve a 15-minute walk
through a park, a quick stop at a highway rest
area or an all-day hike to locate. Once found,
letterboxers mark the box's logbook with their
personal stamp, often leaving a comment about the hunt.

Although the letterboxing craze in America is
only a few years old (the activity got its start
150 years ago in England), it's growing. One
Albany area group is hoping to expand
letterboxing's popularity even more with a
program called "questing," which helps children
learn about their community through the creation of letterbox walking tours.

A frequent letterbox creator is Martha Starke,
also known as "Mookie," who has taught
stampmaking and clue writing to kids at her home
in Greenfield, N.Y. Starke is now applying for a
grant to do a questing project with the Saratoga
Mentoring Program of Catholic Charities, which
matches children from single-parent homes with adult volunteers.

Two years ago she held a questing workshop for
students of the Waldorf School of Saratoga
Springs, N.Y., who were studying geology and
printmaking. The Waldorf quest investigated the
nearby Petrified Sea Gardens, a private preserve
featuring the fossils of ancient sea creatures.
Starke showed the students exploration techniques
such as sound mapping, in which they simply sat in silence and listened.

"We heard birds and running water -- and
construction vehicles," she says. "That's how we
learned that the Sea Gardens are owned by a quarry."

History soon became as important as geology. The
students discovered that weddings have been held
there, and some people have had their ashes scattered there.

"Then you decide on the story you want to tell," Starke says.

What's everyone's take on this article? Me
personally, I don't think this article would cause a
sudden influx of "one-boxer-wonders" - my take was
that this article focused more on the "feel good"
human interest story about working with kids through
this "questing" program. As far as sparking interest
in taking up letterboxing, it gave me an, "Oh, that's
nice" feeling, not a "I want to run to the store, buy
a stamp, print off as many clues as possible and get
out there NOW" vibe. I'm sure there are SOME people
who will read this article and want to learn more, but
I don't know if we'll get the influx of new boxers
that we have from other articles (like the Family Fun
one) which went into more detail about the actual
hunting.
