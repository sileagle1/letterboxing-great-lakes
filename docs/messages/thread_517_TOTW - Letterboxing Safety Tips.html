Re: TOTW - Letterboxing Safety Tips

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-08-09 12:38:44 UTC
There are so many things I could include, but will stick with these
three items:

1) Take the time to put together a basic first aid kit for your
pack. I just use a small rubbermaid container with basic supplies --
bandaids, first aid ointment, ace bandage, aspirin, tweezers, etc.
Sounds so common-sense, but easy to overlook in the excitement of
heading on to the trail.

2) Never post to this or any other online location the specifics
about an upcoming letterboxing adventure. It may seem innocent
enough, but posting that you will be at XYZ park looking for the ABC
box on Sunday afternoon is too much information to make public,
especially if you are letterboxing alone.

3) If you do letterbox alone... let somebody know where you are
going either by leaving home an itinerary or checking in while you
are out. When I am out alone, I take my cell phone with me, not only
to have along for an emergency situation that may arise on the trail,
but also I use it to leave a record of where I am. When I get to a
letterboxing destination, I call home and leave a message on the
answering machine of the park I am at, the box(es) I am seeking and
the time. Same thing when I get to the next location. This way if I
don't show up at home at the end of the day, somebody will be aware
of the last place I was at.

SpringChick

Re: TOTW - Letterboxing Safety Tips

From: lesann847 (l-fischer@northwestern.edu) | Date: 2004-08-09 13:09:02 UTC
Well, as one letterboxer told me, those hats with the mosquito
netting over them make letterboxers look fierce enough to scare
off anyone with bad intentions :-). The hiking staffs are good,
too.

I do a lot of letterboxing on my own, and security is a big issue
for me. I used to have to walk through very rough parts of the
mean streets of Chicago and often had to take good care of
myself there to ward off trouble. It was a couple of years,
however, before I could get comfortable by myself on the trail.

The way I began to gain confidence was by applying what I had
learned in the city as a lone woman pedestrian. In the city, I
walked with my keys in my fist and a whistle close at hand. On
the trail, I don't use the keys because I'm juggling a compass
and a camera and the clues and my water bottle, etc. I do carry a
whistle that can be heard if I hurt myself or if I need help.

In the city, I learned to walk with more confidence than I
sometimes felt--always look like you know where you're going.
On the trail this is less easy if you're looking for a letterbox, but
you can walk tall until you get to the general area, then take out
your camera as if you're searching for the perfect shot.

My mother told me not to talk to strangers for good reason.
Letterboxers are a friendly, trusting lot. Women, in particular, are
taught to always be polite. If you're alone, particularly if you're
female, you can't afford to indulge in friendly conversation with
others. This is against our natural inclination, but it's necessary
to keep yourself safe. You've got to be tough and you've got to be
rather cold.
Just one example: some of you may recall that when I was
planting boxes for the Great Lakes Gathering this year, I locked
myself out of my car. First safety tip: don't lock yourself out of
your car; carry an extra car key in your pack. Second safety tip: if
you are so foolish as to lock yourself out of your car, please have
your cell phone with you so you can call for help.

Other than standing next to the car in the pouring rain, there was
no place to wait and still see the car and the arrival of the
locksmith. You'd think I had a neon sign flashing over my head:
POTENTIAL MARK HERE. Two different creepy guys--no they
weren't being helpful, they were being creepy--drove up while I
was waiting for the locksmith (who only took 20 minutes from the
time I called).

One backed off quickly when I narrowed my eyes and coldly said
my husband was simply using the facilities. Safety tip: avoid
letting people know you're alone. The next guy wasn't taking go
away for an answer. My first instinct was to run to the outhouse
and lock myself in; that did not sound very pleasant, I might
miss the locksmith or other sources of help, and I wouldn't be
able to tell if the creep went away. So I just began walking
briskly to the road in and toward the park exit. There were a few
cars going by, the locksmith or ranger might be coming that way,
so it was relatively safe. As I walked I said some really creative
curses ( I never swear, but I rehearse them before setting out so
I don't stumble over the words) and made rude gestures I never
otherwise make, and generally acted like a volatile, dangerous
crazy woman, and the guy took off.

I guess my point is, just think about what you would do in certain
situations before you get into those situations. Think about how
you will act, what you will say, and what you will do. Visualize it in
your head ahead of time, and it will be easier to make safe
choices when you need to.

Acorn

This is an important TOTW, especially with so many new letterboxers
joining the ranks.
Obviously, a short, urban box letterboxing jaunt is not the same as
an out-in-the-wild box search but sometimes a letterbox trek turns
out to be more strenuous than the clues might have suggested.

A common thread among hikers is "hope for the best and plan for
the worst", hence the general agreement on The Ten Essentials.
(This list is adapted from many references).

1. Flashlight and spare batteries. If you're delayed getting off
the trail, it's easier with a light source.
2. Map. Websearch for maps of the letterbox area. Consider using
Terraserver or USGS maps.
3. Compass
4. Food & water. `Nuff said. (A chocolate mini Hershey's is a
regular reward for us when letterboxing).
5. Appropriate clothing (rain gear?)
6. Sunglasses
7. First-aid kit
8. Pocket knife
9. Waterproof matches
10. Candle or firestarter

The important comments already shared by others on this list about
personal behavior, especially when `boxing solo, can't be overstated.
Cell phones, whistles, notification of locations being visited, etc.

Above all, common sense.
