From: Drucilla Prince (wwlilsis@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-08-04 23:07:43 UTC-07:00
The idea and practice of limiting one's letterboxing clues to those who have a certain find count is not only perceived as unwelcoming to a new boxer, but is ludicrous to think that it would limit a would be vandal. Let's get real people!
Planters need to take the time and make the effort to hide the boxes well. Boxers need to take the time and be both more descrete on the path and RECOVER the boxes well. I have experienced both of these problems which I sure contributes to majority of discoverd and vandalized boxes out there.
Staff is presently researching a variety of hexes, curses, jinx and enchantments (think King Tut's tomb) for box protection.

I think one thing it helps cut down on that might be on the uprise are the
bouts of intentional, pre-meditated vandalism. The people that read an
article or watch a blurb on the TV news or hear about it from a friend, and
think the vandalism is a wild, crazy, really cool idea. I would think these
people would be much more likely to take the easiest target they can find --
the boxes where, first, finding the clues is the easiest, and second, where
following the clues are the easiest, and third, where the amount of work in
hiking activity is the easiest. But number one on my list if I was one of
those vandals would be finding the clues in the easiest way in the first
place. If there are clues that are restricted and clues that are not
restricted, the unrestricted ones will get hit first.

I do a lot of cemetery photography. The increase in vandalism in the past
decade has just been amazing. And, obviously, not amazing in a pleasant
way. And these old stones from back in the 1700s and 1800s are
irreplaceable.

But most cemeteries are not locked up with 8 foot high electrified fences.
They're sittin' ducks of targets. And as letterboxing comes out from under
the radar and gets better known out there by the general public, and the
fact that every Tom Dick and Harry nowadays either has a computer or can
stop by their local library to use one.............letterboxes might start
to look as ducky as tombstones.

Speaking of curses and enchantments, maybe Rowling could come up with
something for letterboxers.

~~ Mosey ~~

Re: restriction of letterboxes

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-08-05 14:04:14 UTC
The alternative to making your box less of a spontaneous target would
be putting some distance on the hike and clues that go beyond "three
steps from the trailhead, find the box next to the big rock."

I would daresay that most would-be "just for fun" vandals are not
likely to hike 5 miles or take the time to decipher code just to have
a little fun with a letterbox. Not that all boxes need to be long
hikes or cryptic clues, but think back to the days of Mapsurfer and
Legerdemaine -- clues that took some thinking, hikes that involved
distance, etc. Many of these boxes are still in place -- years after
they were put in place. While any box can go missing due to
accidental discovery or poor rehiding, or fall victim to animals or
weather, the increase in intentional vandalism is directly related to
the increase in no-brainer clues and drive-by type letterboxes and of
course, increased publicity.

I have always maintained that the best defense against intentional
vandalism is a well-placed letterbox and well-written clues. Even
then nothing is fool-proof and making clues less accessible by placing
restrictions or limiting distribution is certainly another means of
adding some insurance. While I am not opposed to WOM clues and
alternate means of distributing clues and think this can be a fun,
exciting aspect of the game when used as the exception, I do think
that resorting to this as the *norm* for disseminating letterbox clues
goes against the original philosophy of making letterbox clues
accessible to "any person who walks into a library and sits down at a
computer."

There is a very fine line between protecting your boxes and exclusivity.

SpringChick

From: Carol Osada (cjosada@hotmail.com) | Date: 2007-08-05 23:56:24 UTC-06:00
I remember some of my most challenging searches and those boxes are still in place. On box took me three yearly vacation trips to find and when I found it, it was sooooooo worth the challenge!

Another box I found required 6 months of web surfing and inquiring about local area history from older residents in that town. I even visited the town's library. Of course, I must have sounded like a historical buff, but I certainly never let on that I was looking for a letterbox. I finally found that box too. And yes, it's still in place.

Sometimes I feel a 'need' to find a box, so I look for a quick and easy one. But there are times when I 'need' a challenge and I enjoy the mental process. Then, there are times when I feel a good hike will help to loosen up my joints and some exercise would be good. But exercise for the sake of exercise is....well, let's just say I 'need' more motivation and a letterbox is the answer.

Beetle
