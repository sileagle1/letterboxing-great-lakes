Serious Question

From: beckers410 (Mathw613@aol.com) | Date: 2007-05-17 01:02:52 UTC
Not that any of the question posted on this board are not serious, I
just have one that has been weighing heavy on my heart for the past
24 hours.

A true artistic genius and pioneer letterboxer from the Quad Cities
was killed last August.

Several of us have formed an attachment to her legacy through the
hobby of letterboxing. We keep her boxes up by replacing baggies or
boxes that have critter damage.

Two of her 18 boxes have gone missing. I didn't have the pleasure of
finding these boxes before they disappeared. I tracked down the image
of one of the boxes and re-carved it, thinking that it would just be
re-planted for others to find.

I guess I didn't think out the possible ramifications of my actions
as I really hurt someone's feelings in doing so. The other
letterboxer feels, "that as her boxes go missing, they should just be
retired."

I seriously didn't mean to hurt any feelings, and if I had known I
never would have re-carved the image.

To me, in my mind I have watched over these boxes as if they were my
own. If one of my own boxes goes missing I would re-carve the image
and put it back out for others to find, thus I did just that for this
box.

I figure that with the number of people on this board I will have
opinions from both sides of "should the stamp of a deceased
letterboxer be re-carved and replanted or should it just be retired?"

I see the folly of my ways, especially not asking this question to
those close to the situation before I just jumped in and did it, I
regret my actions. I'm just looking for some introspective feed back.

Thanks,
Hart x6

From: xxxxxxxx (BrighidFarm@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-05-16 20:40:38 UTC-05:00
I know you really meant well, but I'd vote amongst those who wouldn't care
for having that done. If it were just a case of replacing baggies and doing
general maintenance, that's one thing. But by re-carving the stamp, it's no
longer her artwork. It's now your artwork in her box, using her clues. So
folks are now not finding her artwork, but rather are finding your artwork.

Personally, what I would have done was to retire that box since obviously
she, herself, can never replace it, and then maybe carve another box with a
theme that you thought she might have liked and plant it in the area of the
missing box in her memory. That way, boxers would legitimately be looking
for your artwork but the box would be *about* her. You could possibly
consider including an image of the stamp from the original box if that was
OK with her family.

On the other hand, if she was close to her family, and her family gave you
permission to do it and encouraged you to do it, then I could understand it.
But if you primarily just took it upon yourself to re-carve the stamp, I
wouldn't have felt it was the correct thing to do. I would fall firmly on
the side of retiring the box and finding some other way to show how you felt
about her and respected her contribution to letterboxing.

~~ Mosey ~~

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-05-17 04:46:29 UTC-04:00
I would have to agree with Mosey. I certainly understand your effort and think your heart was in the right place. It really isn't one of those things you think about, rather just do because it seemed right at the time and possibly almost a tribute to this person. I myself have recarved a missing stamp from another person's box that I had adopted (dropped out of letterboxing, not deceased) in an effort to keep the box alive.

But in retrospect, I think that taking care of boxes planted by another person should be limited to general maintenance and once a critical piece of the box is unavailable, the box and clues should be retired since at that point, the box would no longer be the creation of the original planter. With the abundance of letterboxes available in most areas of the country, there really is no need to perpetuate a box unto oblivion, rather they are planted, they exist for a finite length of time and then they are gone, often to be replaced by a new box with a fresh theme and clues.

SpringChick

Re: [LbGLK] Serious Question

From: (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2007-05-17 03:07:12 UTC-07:00
Hi Hart!
There has been quite a bit of discussion on the "big board" recently on a similar topic. Here are excerpts from some emails I sent to someone earlier this week regarding this subject:
I personally believe that if a box is gone, it's gone. No reason to have it continue indefinitely. In some sense it is a "limited edition". I'd rather see people placing new boxes of their own than "recreating" someone else's.
I view letterboxing as a dynamic, not static, environment with some boxes disappearing and new ones appearing. I don't have any desire to see every box maintained into perpetuity.
I'll do maintenance on a box (baggies, logbook, container, etc) but I would never recarve a stamp to a box that wasn't mine. If the stamp is gone, the box is done unless the owner decides to replace it. I have adopted one box that exists and the owners were unreachable by LBNA. I simply adopted it so the clues were still accessible to people, since the box was still in place. If the box disappears, I'll delete the clues. I wouldn't recarve someone else's stamp. I don't plan to replace all of my own boxes if they disappear and would have no desire for anyone else to replace them for me.
Wisconsin Hiker
