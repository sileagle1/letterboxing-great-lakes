From: Debbie Kotlarek (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2008-12-04 22:38:05 UTC-06:00
Being from Wisconsin, we always celebrated St. Nick's as kids. However we
always hung up STOCKINGS on DEC 5; didn't use shoes. In the Milwaukee area
it is definitely a given (but I know of several people who moved here and
had no idea what their kids were talking about when they came home from
school saying everyone else had gotten something from St. Nick the night
before...!)

Nowadays the kids seem to get a big variety of things, including small toys.
However when we were kids, our stockings were filled only with fruit, candy
and nuts. This was our first introduction to pomegranates a kazillion years
ago - St. Nick left some in our stockings one year.

Wisconsin Hiker

P.S. We always tried to make our Halloween candy last right up to St.
Nick's, when we would get more goodies.

I'm wondering if the prevalent celebration of St. Nick's Day in Wisconsin partially stems some from religious practices brought to the area, since he was a saint and all. St. Nicholas is, among other things, the patron saint of Byzantine Catholics, which was the religion of many Eastern European immigrants. Despite the large number of Byzantine Catholic churches around Cleveland, though, we don't have area-wide St. Nick celebrations like they do in Wisconsin. Although we knew of the tradition, my brother & I never left our shoes out for St. Nick. But, at our religion classes Christmas party each year, the party was held as close to St. Nicholas Day as possible and our priest dressed up as St. Nicholas to pass out presents. We were also given a cookie with a picture of St. Nicholas somehow glued onto the cookie. Oh, those cookies were horrible!

Doodle Bug.

From: Sara Lancaster (sr_lancaster@yahoo.com) | Date: 2008-12-05 06:25:45 UTC-08:00
Im of Dutch descent (Sara of Leapin Lizards) and it was that side of my family that introduced me to and celebrated St. Nicks Day. Saint Nick is the common name for Nicholas of Myra, a Bishop of Myra in Lycia of Anatolia (or modern-day Turkey). You can read all about it in David Sedaris hilarious essay Six to Eight Black Men. Highly recommended.

When I was little, my sisters and I would set out our wooden shoes (which Grandma and Grandpa had gotten for us in Holland, Michigan) on Dec. 5. On the morning of the 6th. wed find them filled with candy and small giftsthink stocking stuffersand sometimes even an ornament for the Christmas tree. As a kid, I honestly think I enjoyed St. Nicks Day more than Christmas. We dont have any Little Lizards, and John doesnt have a pair of wooden shoes, but St. Nick still seems to make it to our house every Dec. 6. :) I just have to make sure John doesnt leave out his running shoes. (Eeww.) Hang the stocking instead, honey.

Yes, being of Dutch decent, we also celebrated St. Nick's Day by
setting out wooden shoes from the Wooden Shoe Factory in Holland, MI.
Somewhere along the way, we stopped doing it though.

Another thing that we had as children that I don't see much any more
are Advent calenders -- very fancifully drawn ones with glittery snow
and little doors and windows for each day during the month of
December. I got them for my girls when they were very young also. I
think this is also a Western European thing -- they sell some very
nice ones at Bronner's, the big Bavarian Christmas store in
Frankenmuth, MI (online at http://www.bronners.com/).

From: Katrina Lustofin (drkaty@gmail.com) | Date: 2008-12-05 12:11:31 UTC-05:00
I loved the Advent calendar when I was a kid! We had a fancy paper one
usually that a Sunday school teacher or someone gave us, but loved even more
the one my mom made. It was made of green felt in the shape of a Christmas
tree with 25 red pockets that each contained a Bible verse plus three little
treats (cos there were three of us kids). Think miniature candy canes or
Hershey kisses. It was terrifically exciting to be the child allowed to
open the pocket and read the verse. It also made a nice Christmas wall
hanging. Now you've got me wondering what happened to it.

Despite being of German descent, we only celebrated Nikolaustag the year our
German exchange student stayed with us.


Katy
