--- In LbGLK@yahoogroups.com, "SpringChick" wrote:
> Okay, maybe I am doing something wrong over at the LbNA site, but
how do you keep people from entering status updates on your boxes?

I don't think you can disable it. Some people like to keep track of
counts of their boxes using LBNA so I think you can always make a
notation about whether you've found or attempted a box. The
advantages of this feature to me personally are:

1. We can change the settings within LBNA to only display the boxes
we haven't found yet, thereby generating a shorter list of boxes to
consider.

2. A lot of people rarely, if ever, sent me messages via "Contact
the Placer", so at least with the status change I know that the box
is still there. If someone just changes status to "Found" I'm
assuming that the box is OK.

3. I like seeing info about when a box was last checked/found and
have included it in the bocy of all my clues. I would update the
date whenever I got an email that someone had found the box. By
allowing the automatic display of the last status update, that
information is a bit more current, since it shows info from people
that perhaps would never send me an email. (However keep in mind
that there is nothing that guarantees that people are actually
putting in accurate information.)


Nonetheless, like Azobox & SpringChick, I also prefer to get more
info via an email message about the status of my boxes. Thanks to
those that do this!

Wisconsin Hiker

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2005-06-28 18:09:14 UTC-04:00
So, why might you want to keep people from entering status reports? Is it
because of the inconvenience of getting so many emails from earlier finds
right now? I can understand that. Would be interested in any other reason
as well-I learn so much form these discussions


It is my personal opinion that publicly viewable status reports do not belong on a website whose purpose is to provide a clearinghouse of clues. Rather it is my feeling that a status report is more appropriately a private exchange of information between box seeker and box planter. All too often clues are inadvertently given away when people disclose their trail and others come along and follow their footsteps. And then there is the accuracy of the status report themselves. Over the past 3 years I have received countless "can't find your box, it must be missing" e-mails. More often than not, the box was safely in the hiding spot. Giving seekers the ability to log a status of "attempted" on a letterbox can discourage future seekers from looking for the box. Many people would rather spend a Saturday afternoon seeking out a half-dozen boxes that have been recently confirmed active than taking a chance on a box that was not findable by previous seekers (even though the box may be safe and sound).

There are some letterboxers who display box status in their clues; there are others who choose not to display box status at all. There are some boxers who will give you the last known status if you contact them; there are other highly respected boxers who choose not to answer any questions regarding status, feeling that detracts from the underlying philosophy of the game. Traditionally public status disclosure and logging have not been a part of the game and there are alternatives for those who wish a more generous level of disclosure -- AtlasQuest, personal web sites and status information provided within individual box clues. There should not be a need to water down the integrity of the original LbNA clue site by adding it there.

That being said, I know that I and a smallish group of staunchy old curmudgeons are in the minority on this and I can accept that the added functionality of status reporting on LbNA is a feature welcomed by many, many people. As such, I can agree to disagree, especially since Choi has made it possible to disable the public display of status logs if a planter so desires.

However, my primary beef with the status logging is not that it exists, rather that it seems to have inadvertently become a substitute for contact the placer messages or private e-mails to planters about box finds and/or status. These individual contacts were very useful tools -- not only did they provide specific information as a means of helping a planter keep tabs on their boxes, but also they were a communication between two letterboxers -- sometimes only a stone's throw away from each other, other times clear from the other side of the country, and as such, they facilitated correspondence and community.

SpringChick
(trying desperately to hold onto some of the old-school ideals of this game)
