From: Carol Osada (cjosada@hotmail.com) | Date: 2007-08-06 22:27:58 UTC-06:00
That brings up a good question: why geocaching and not letterboxing? I guess geocachers prefer their hobby and we prefer letterboxing. Seldom do you meet someone who does both; like Cinemaboxers. But GS camps usually stress 'stay on the trails,' so I'm not sure why either. I think the clue writing would be part of the activity fun. Also, the GPS units cost money and to give only one unit to a group isn't much fun if there is a large troop and the others just tag along and wonder about till one or two people sharing the GPS unit pin point the location and find the box. I've seen groups trampling an area marching around through all sorts of wild flowers and poison ivy too, with only one goal....find the box.

As a letterboxer first who has tried both hobbies, I guess I feel that letterboxing is more environmentally friendly...and we should keep that in mind when placing boxes.

The Chicago council is discussion plans for a geocaching program. Whatever?

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-08-07 23:16:48 UTC-05:00
LOL Doodle...what's really funny about your description is that it is
exactly how I thought Boy/Cub Scouts would feel about letterboxing, until we
did a letterboxing-themed parent/pal weekend with Cub Scouts back in
April. Like you, I've staffed numerous Cub and Boy Scout camps and thought
I knew the market well enough to know it wouldn't be well-received, which is
why I never bothered to ask the Council about putting boxes on camp until
there were already caches.

I think it's all how you present it as to whether boys get excited about
letterboxing. If you tell them it's art, they'll turn their noses up, if
you tell them the art side is a necessary part of a treasure hunt game,
they'll go with you on it. At our event, all the Cub Scouts received
hand-carved stamps by someone else. 150 boys - 30 at a time - made carving
tools seem like a bad idea, but the boys were terribly upset they didn't get
to play with the sharp instruments - errr, I mean carve their own
stamps. They did make their own logbooks. We told them the stamp and the
book were tools for the rest of the events for the day. After they had
those two things, they were good to go. I was even amazed at how many of
the Boy Scout staffers came to my station during their off-time to make a
logbook and carve a stamp for themselves.

I think what ultimately made it a huge success was that the boys realized
they were on an even footing with any of the adults there - they were
learning compass skills and other navigation skills all day that helped them
be able to read clues for themselves and figure them out as well as any
grown up. In fact, if they found all 13 boxes, there was a 14th for them -
with spoken clues only. They came to me and I said the clues to them in
riddle form, and they had to remember it and figure it out. The boys got it
long before the dads did! I just don't think they could've gotten the same
sense of satisfaction from geocaching.

I see the bit about the tech-y side of geocaching, but when you can't put a
GPSr in every boy's hands, it starts losing its fun value. And then when
most the caches planted aren't trade caches (only about 20% in our area have
trades, and none of them on scout camp do), what's the fun in all that
hunting, just to write your name and move on to the next?

And all this reminds me - I have a few of those boxes that still need to be
hidden in the general wild. The logbooks still have the stamp entries of
the boys in them and each box has a FF prize in it. I wonder if any of you
Great Lakes Scouter/Letterboxers would be interested in taking a box and
planting it near YOUR scout camp?

Re: Geocaching on Scout Facilities (was Re: [LbGLK] Geocaching class)

From: Carol Osada (cjosada@hotmail.com) | Date: 2007-08-08 01:08:43 UTC-06:00
I agree! Letterboxing is more of an artistic endeavor. That's what the Girl Scouts like about it. However, I've been teaching and stressing this is for older girls, not Brownies and Daisy Girl Scouts. They just can't keep a secret about the location of a box. They are too young to understand how to use a compass. Most of them can't even read yet.

But give the older girls the opportunity to design a signature stamp on paper with a lead pencil, transfer rub it to a piece of rubber, and hand them a Speedball cutter, and they get really excited! Logbooks can be simple; blank index cards with punched holes tied with fancy yarns seem to work well. Some girls (and some adults) get hooked instantly, while others will only attempt to find one box and then give up.

But I have been telling leaders they can use this type of activity with younger Scouts at camp, but I recommend that they call it a treasure hunt and not mention letterboxing or the Internet where the clues are posted for more boxes in their communities.

Most councils want to know what is being placed on their property which is a good idea. Then the caretaker is aware and doesn't clean them up. But one bit of advice is this....clues to boxes should be part of a camp program with a check out binder containing clues and information about the hobby. I still might not mention
LbNA or AQ because you can't control the age of the scouts using the binder.

But NEVER post the clues and the locations to the Scout camps on the internet!!! Most camps are not even listed in the phone books because Scout camps are private property and we are more concerned about intruders on the property while troops are camping. Best to be cautious.

But it's good to hear that BS enjoy this hobby like the GS do.

As for caching....those units are expensive an compasses are more practical for groups of Scouts.

Beetle
