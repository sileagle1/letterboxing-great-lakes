Before we have a new TOTW, I thought I'd put my 2 cents into this
week's topic...

Much like SpringChick's granddaughter, I cannot go much of anywhere
anymore without noticing good locations for hiding letterboxes. (Do
we need professional help? :-)) Usually I have an idea for a box or
series, then go to the location that would be ideal for the context
of the box and find an appropriate spot to hide the box. Usually
this is in a tree just off the beaten path. Unfortunately, that
doesn't always work, especially with new forests that don't have
large "holely" trees. Then I usually go for a mound of rocks or in
a rock wall.

I always keep in mind to avoid low places where a box might get
flooded (although I forget about low places that might get wet on
the way TO a box, 'eh Moofie & Blaze? Oops!) or washed away. I also
cover my boxes very well with loose brush and/or rocks so they
cannot be easily seen or take by animals. So far this has worked
very well. Only one of my boxes have gone missing, and I'm still
not sure why - either it when down the unused sewer pipe it was in
(although I had blocked it up behind the box) or I was swiped by
hoodlums.

locations topic.

I tend to try and theme my boxes, either playing off the location or
within a series. My favorites are those where the location and the
theme compliment each other, thus allowing for more imaginitive
clues.

Trying to determine which comes first -- the location or the box
theme is kind of like asking which came first -- the chicken or the
egg. I think subconsciously I have this mental list of place and
park names in the back corner of my mind and ideas for boxes come
based on that (yes, I know, it is a sickness).

Occasionally I come up with a box or series, having no idea where I
am going to place it, but that is rare and I then find myself trying
to make something fit, and usually being less than satisfied with
the end result.

In seeking hiding places, I prefer not to place boxes right at
ground level as it seems these are more susceptible to critters.
Rather I try for standing tree stumps, crooks and hollows, natural
or architectural crevices. But sometimes you have to take what you
can find and lying hollow logs are often easier to find than
standing ones. Rock piles are great, but are few and far between
here in Michigan.
