Ah yes, a good mystery.
And what makes the mystery a good one?
Mystery letterboxes are indeed a special breed and need special
attention by both the placer and the seeker. As Acorn suggests, the
personality of the placer can come even stronger to the surface with
a good mystery box. How are the clues presented -- puzzles? very
cryptic text clues? graphic images only? vaguely described locations?
total minimalism? they all certainly contribute to the complexity
and the allure of a good mystery box.
I've planted easy mysteries and difficult ones. Some have sat for
awhile before they even had a nibble. Some were solved in a flash. As
the placer I'm always interested in what tipped off the finder. Where
in the clues did he/she "get it"? Sometimes I think I'm being very
clever with a mystery box clue and then I discover that the seekers
never gave it much thought, or they figured it out right away. Or
maybe they moved on in the clues to something else they could get
their teeth into. I like to give hints that *may* reveal something.
Or may not.
Oftentimes the only mystery may be the location of the park. I recall
an email I got from a seeker who demanded that I tell her where to
start her hunt for a mystery placement of mine. She was mighty
unhappy when I didn't comply. (I sent her a link to Silent Doug's
glossary: Mystery Letterboxes). She was not assuaged, and she let me
know it.

On the very practical side, I'm frustrated when I'm aware that
another boxer has (perhaps inadvertently) given away a glimpse into
my mystery's location by either their online boxing reports or by
their own box placements. Landmarks or identifiable attributes of a
place given by another boxer as part of their clues to another box
may reveal a key element of *my* mystery. I guess the only way to
avoid that is to carefully research boxes in your area and make sure
you don't shed light on another's stealth. Experienced placers know
how much thought, time and energy goes into creating a successful
letterbox. I've discovered that it may take even more to create a
good mystery box.
I do offer hints to seekers who are stuck if I sense they have put
some thought and energy into the search. Not all placers will offer
help

I quote from a section of Mapsurfer's site:
--------------------------------
"Please do not e-mail me asking for hints and help
[Snip]
(Despite this gentle request, I still get plenty of these request-
for-help e-mails, so please don't be offended when I don't respond --
it's nothing personal, just the way the game is played)."
--------------------------------------
So you see, the mysteries range from here to there and the spinners
of those mysteries can hold the answers pretty close to their vests.
The really good ones require the legerity of a good puzzler and the
tenacity of a determined tortoise.
And *that* could be a clue. Or not.

I recently corresponded with a letterboxer whose clues I admire. She
admitted the joy in discovering a mystery box after *seven hours* of
search through prickers, gorse, and knee-high rushes. The good ones
are worth it, but you have to want it.
And sometimes a regular letterbox holds those same challenges. Just
because you can't find it doesn't mean it isn't there.

> because there is so much work that goes into developing a
> good mystery, the creator just shines through in every element of
> the box.
>
> For my personal taste, a great mystery box includes a fun story
> or framework, some research I can conduct before I get out in
> the field, clever clues (I don't mind having to return a few times--
> right TT? ;->), and of course a good stamp. It's an added bonus
> for me if the creator includes puzzles, ciphers, codes, rebus,
> anagrams or other forms of play.
>
> The "quality" of the stamp is less important to me than the
> process by which I found it. Nonetheless, I delight in skilled
> carving, or clever jokes embedded in the stamp.
>
> My sometime letterboxing partner (and dear husband), is more
> goal-driven than process-oriented. Sometimes, that's just what I
> need to finally track down the mystery. We were boxing in
> another state, I had done the preliminary research to find the
> right site. I was, however, given wrong information by the local
> tourist board and we were continually misdirected by locals
> throughout a long day of tracking down the mystery box. After
> making a circuit around the entire county, we finally located the
> box two blocks from where we began that morning. It was a little
> too much "process" at the time, but we still laugh about it and
> how he just wouldn't give up.
>
> I'd love to hear other stories and comments on mystery boxes.
