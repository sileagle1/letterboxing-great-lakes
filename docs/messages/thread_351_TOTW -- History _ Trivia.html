From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-06-28 11:27:02 UTC
One of the aspects of letterboxing that I enjoy most is learning
about new places, and learning new things about old places. In
researching cities, parks and historical sites for possible box
placements and/or solving mystery locations, I have uncovered all
sorts of interesting historical bits and trivia I would otherwise
never have discovered.

This week's topic of the week: What are some of the most
interesting bits of trivia or history that you have serendipitously
stumbled upon while letterboxing? Is this something about your own
city/state/area or another place?

SpringChick

From: Delirium (donaldson_home@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-06-28 04:52:29 UTC-07:00
Oh! I LOVE this one! Like my mom always says about me, "You've got a brain like your father's- full of trivia and no common sense" *grin*

Every location I've planted a box holds some little gem of history for me. From Hines Drive and it's hidden little trails, to my "Coyote" box, which was carved because I found it so funny that coyotes would be living in the middle of such an affluent community.

But my favorite isn't a park I planted a box in- it's where I found one. Asylum Lake, in Kalamazoo, has a truly creepy/interesting history. Please correct me, those who are more familiar, but what I remember is this:

The current place is called "Asylum Lake Preserve". It originially was one of the first pieces of land owned by an African American in the Kalamazoo area. It then changed hands, and was owned by the Michigan Asylum for the Insane. The "Asylum" name stuck even after Western bought it, which is who owns it to this day.

And as a pretty big fan of zombie movies, it's v.v. easy to imagine escaped asylum patients turned zombies crashing out of the woods to come eat the brains of innocent letterboxers.

Cheers,
Delirium, who has now managed to freak herself out just a little bit.

Delirium, you just gave me a great idea for a new letterbox. If I ever make it back down to Athens, OH, I'll have to plant one at The Ridges.

"The Ridges" was the nickname for an insane asylum on the edge of town. Ohio University aquired the property I forget how long ago...within the last decade. OK, maybe I just spooked myself too much to hide a box there. But anyone else in the area, feel free to!

Re: TOTW -- History & Trivia

From: debrocks54 (deb.maertens@stpaul.com) | Date: 2004-06-28 17:32:29 UTC
This is one of the many things I love about letterboxing as well.
I've learned a lot and traveled to some interesting locations and
hope to do much more. I especially love finding places close to home
(Minnesota), that I've never ever heard of or been to before.

One of my favorites is Matt the Rat's "Grasshopper Chapel" box. It's
the first box I ever found so it holds a fond place in my memory for
that reason but, aside from that, the location and the Chapel has a
really neat story behind it.

It's a sweet little chapel, in a secluded, wooded clearing, at the
top of a hill, that commemorates the advance of a locust plague in
April, 1877, near Cold Spring, Minnesota. When it appeared that the
creatures would inundate the town and decimate its crops, residents
gathered for a day of prayer sanctioned by the governor. Amazingly, a
snowstorm descended, freezing the insects and preserving the town.
Officially called Assumption Chapel, most people refer to it as the
Grasshopper Chapel. I probably would have never heard of it or
visited it if it hadn't been for Matt's box. So, thanks Matt!

Oh, and by the way, Matt has carved the perfect stamp for this
location!

DebRocks

I've thought quite a bit about this over the past week.
While creating my box, "I Spy", I learned a lot about Great Lakes
shipwrecks, especially the sinking of the paddle wheeler Alpena.
A curious fact came to my attention after I'd created and placed the
box. (check out the clues to get the gist of the situation).
I learned that the nameplate from the paddlewheel box on the doomed
ship finally washed ashore just north of Holland, MI almost 30 years
after the ship sank. I later learned that the beach near Holland was
named Alpena Beach for the ship that was lost in 1880. Another eerie
experience I had was reading an eyewitness description of the storm
from a sailor who saw the Alpena in her final hours from another ship.
He was interviewed about 1921 and excerpts were reprinted in a local
newspaper.
By the way, the wreck has still not been found.
Despite the fact that my clues were only peripherally connected to
the wreck of the Alpena, reading that account of a sailor who lived
through that storm brought a sense of reality to me that I would not
have expected.

A thought provoking TOTW.
