Letterboxing. It would be fantastic if anyone could share with me what

From: doubledragonletterboxer (M8KC5008@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-11-06 18:15:12 UTC
The first question I must ask is do you have a box planted and did
you carve it yourself? It isn't that we want to exclude people from
joining in on the fun, or prevent new people from joining the LB
world. After all we were all newbies at one time or another. We just
want people to understand how much effort goes into making a box,
especially when you carve the stamp yourself. I think, at the risk
of sounding corny, we all feel that a peice of ourselves goes into
our art work that may take days to create. And when we, as the
placer, find out that our box is distroid by the carelessness of new
boxers, it hurts to know someone cares so little for our creation.
We just want people to understand that you need to rehide the boxes
well, and to understand how you would feel if you just spent three
days on a stamp just to have it go missing in 2 weeks and you have
to carve it all over again.
Double Dragon

From: Doodle & Deedle Bug (doodle_n_deedle@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-11-06 13:47:24 UTC-08:00
Well, since I'm on a roll...

Gleening from recent past posts on this list, here are
some highlights:

As I said in my previous post, resist the urge to
scream "I found it!!" if there are others around.
However, if you're alone on the trail, by all means
break out into the Happy Happy Joy Joy dance.
However, be forwarned - my husband Deedle's Rule Of
Letterboxing #1 is that no matter where you are, as
soon as you get ready to stamp in someone else will
appear. You can be letterboxing on the top of Mt.
Everest, positive that you are the only one around,
and as soon as you have everything spread out in front
of you to begin the stamping in process, someone will
come walking by with their dog.

ALWAYS bring water with you. And if these are
families with kids, especially if the family choses to
do a longer hike, it probably wouldn't be a bad idea
to bring some snacks too. However, before grabbing
the letterbox, make sure that anyone who was touching
food wipes their hands. The animals have good noses
and the food scent might transfer to the box.

The #1 Letterboxing Crime of late has been the poorly
rehidden box. If little Johnny has been given the
duty of rehiding the box, have Mom and/or Dad watching
to make sure that no colored corners are poking out.
As much as you might think you're doing the next
letterboxer a favor by letting a corner of the plastic
baggie poke out of the hiding place (and I admit,
Deedle & I used to think that way, too), it's more of
a chance for a non-boxer with ill intent to find the
box and wreak havoc instead. After hiding a box,
Deedle & I both make sure that we cannot spot it from
the trail, but that the rehiding also doesn't look
suspicious. That pile of rocks in the middle of the
field might also perk up the interest of the non-boxer
with ill intent.

If boxing with kids, teach them to treat the land with
respect. They are in the animals' home, so teach them
to stay on the trail, and if they need to leave the
trail to tread lightly, try not to trample plants.
And large sticks are meant to be used as walking
sticks, not to attack every tree they pass. And if
they're screaming as they walk, they'll miss the
opportunity to see animals in the woods.

This goes along with searching FOR the box. Treat the
land with respect, especially when searching for the
letterboxer. Sometimes it's just not there, and in
these cases, try not to leave the forest looking like
you were digging up land minds in your effort to find
the letterbox.

When logging in, since some of those logbooks don't
have much room, encourage the families to use one
family stamp or have individual stamps small enough to
fit on one page. And with stamps, if they can't clean
the ink off the stamp (blotting on a wet paper towel
is all that's really necessary to clean it), encourage
them to wrap the stamp in a paper towel to keep ink
from spreading to the logbook and container and
baggies.

Also, and this is probably the most important aspect
of letterboxing with children, teach them that they
won't always find the box, but that's just part of the
game. It's a metaphor for life. And just because
they didn't find the box doesn't mean it wasn't worth
the hike. Encourage parents to make it more about the
trip than it is about the outcome. Although, when we
don't find the box, Deedle & I have learned that ice
cream works wonders to console ourselves with. :)

As for the LbNA website, I think it's OK to share the
letterboxing.org address with your homeschoolers,
since technically that IS what the website is for.
And especially because they have a whole section on
that website dedicated to "getting started" which is
important for newer letterboxers to read. The issue
some of us have had with this recent article is that
we didn't post our clues on letterboxing.org so that
they can be included without permission in an article
that includes pictures of what the stamp image looks
like and a shot of the hiding spot. That's just,
well, like telling someone the ending of the movie
they're on their way to see.

Boy am I long-winded today!!

Doodle Bug.
