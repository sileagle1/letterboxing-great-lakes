Yes. Letters are tricky. Some of my stamp images are fairly intricate and somewhat impressive...but I HATE doing letters because I'm rarely happy with how they turn out. From: Lizz (lizz.glossop@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2008-02-12 18:07:42 UTC
I had trouble with letters on my 1st carving, and still do have
trouble.....
But the thing that has made the most difference and that no one
mentioned is getting a magnifying glass, a big one! Wow what a
difference! I got one that is on a base, from the Ott Lite series
(wait for a 40% off sale or coupon!!! otherwise too pricey.)
The other item that has helped me with carving is a LazySusan
clipboard. I double stick my caring medium to the board, and spin as
needed. I think they are still available from Alextamping.com. I don't
think they come with the clip anymore, but you don't need that for
carving.
I also use micro wood carving tools.... I get them at a wood carving
show. But you can probably order them online. There's a WoodCarvers
store in Peoria IL. if that helps any.

I'v only cared about 7 stamps so far, so i am by no means an expert,
These are just the things that have helped me so far.

Petrified Peep Eater.

ps I also keep a needle handy for very fine lines and stabbing and
removing waste pieces.

From: (Mathw613@aol.com) | Date: 2008-02-12 21:18:01 UTC-05:00
I haven't heard of the LazySusan idea. I just have my stamp on either a
piece of paper, or, more often than not on my pad of tracing paper -- either one
spins easily on my table top.

Congrats on the first carve, beware though IT'S ADDICTING!! Soon you will
try to one-up yourself as far as detail etc.

Lettering, well, I'm not sure that I've *ever* heard anyone say that they
"love" to carve letters. They seem to be the most dreaded part of any stamp
image to carve for veteran carvers as well as those new to carving. Just take
your time, carve out the centers first, and use *really* shallow cuts.

One of the reasons I hate carving letters, (aside from the point already
mentioned that "everyone knows what letters look like, therefore they can spot
errors easily") is that letters are spaced so closely to each other, there's
really very little room to work.

Just keep at it . . . carve carve carve! And with all that carving, enjoy
enjoy enjoy!!

All I can say is, once a very wise stamp artist gave me some excellent advice I have used to this day. Only once did I not heed her advice, and sure enough, that was the one stamp I screwed up. Her advice actually referred to carving a person, but can be extrapolated to apply to letters or other detailed work. She said "Start with the eyes." They are the hardest, and if they look bad, it affects the entire image, not to mention the time you will save if you have to start over. So start at the smallest most critical details...the eyes, the letters, the insides of the tinest letters, and continue from there.

Oh, and thanks again, SpringChick!

Re: [LbGLK] Re: My first carving!

From: SpringChick (letterbox@comcast.net) | Date: 2008-02-14 06:49:04 UTC-05:00
To add to what Fungus Woman has said about carving details (letters, eyes, etc.) first... Carving the inside of the letters before the outside makes a difference especially with small letters because once you carve the outside, the area is more flimsy and getting an exact carve on the inside is more difficult. Also, to help get letters uniform height (assuming of course that the font you are using is meant to be evenly spaced), before beginning individual letters, I carve a straight line across the top of all letters -- actually two lines, one that runs across the top of things like "o" and "n" and "r" and then a second that runs across the top of caps and letters with tall extensions like "l" and "f" and "d". I then do the same thing on the bottom of the letters. This helps to keep them an even height. You would think that wouldn't be difficult to do since you have the outline to follow, but when carving individual letters, it is easy to stray a little and end up with some letters that are a little taller than others.

Also, it has been said many times, many places, but holds very true when carving letters -- kind of goes with the lazy susan idea -- to get nice smooth curves on letters (and your images), instead of pushing your tool, keep the hand with the carving tool still and rotate the piece of carving material as needed. You will get a much smoother cut that way.

The reason to carve details first? Does it really make a difference in the difficulty of the carving process? No, but you get the most tedious parts out of the way and also, since these are the things you are most likely to mess up, if you do really mess it up, you can start on a new stamp without throwing out hours of time that might have been spent had you done the other areas first.

SpringChick

From: Kathi Krankoski (krank@ameritech.net) | Date: 2008-02-14 20:56:34 UTC-08:00
Once my husband was having a magnetic sign made for his truck, and they were using those peel-and-stick letters on the magnet material. I noticed that the round letters looked too big compared to the rest, and as I saw them being placed against guide lines, much like SC describes, you could definitely see the "O"s, "G"s, etc were larger. The signmaker explained that visually they will look too small when viewing the completed words if they actually were the same size, so they do make those letters a bit larger. Most of the time it won't make a big difference in a stamp, but it is something to keep in mind.

--klk/fw
