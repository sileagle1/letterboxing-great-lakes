We did our very first hunt/find today!

From: (KarenBeid@aol.com) | Date: 2007-03-06 01:32:29 UTC-05:00
Hello everyone!
I am new to the group and to letterboxing in general. I thought I would let
you know we just got signed up on the LbNA and AQ sites on March 2nd and I
got stuff this past weekend to try my hand at carving a signature stamp for my
hubby (CMB) and myself (FlyGirl409). I found some basically blank square
ready made stamps on clearance and thought this would be wonderful to save the
trouble of mounting my own so got one for $2 to try. Used this to carve CMB
a stamp and found it extremely difficult to carve the rubber stamp that was
already there so I am glad that I only got the one as I will not being doing
it that way again! The stamp turned out decent in my humble opinion, though a
bit rough even for a first attempt. I had played at wood carving a bit many
years ago, but never anything else. I wondered if stamps could be carved of
just wood, but figured not or someone would have said it worked online
somewhere.

I bought some gray linolium to carve many stamps from since CMB does wood
working and has tons of scraps to mount them with. Found it much easier to
carve but was not so crazy about the way my first stamp came out and I think it
is too big judging by the size of the log book in our first find tonight.

We live in Indianapolis so we decided to go look for Quack is Back in Broad
Ripple since we knew the area fairly well. We followed the directions which
were excellent though must note the name of the creek in the clue is
incorrect and might lead out of town letterboxers astray. Other than that we found
it easy enough and stamped in. The hand carved stamp was very nice! I was
not sure what most write near the various stamps in their personal logbooks, so
just wrote the name of the Letterbox, the date and the city and state.

I think this one has hubby hooked now and I will be able to get him to
traipse the countryside in search of many more in the future though he was very
skeptical about whether it would be lame or fun before doing it. I think he
still thinks I am nuts for getting so excited about collecting impressions of
rubber stamps but he is willing to humor me for now so I won't complain.

We are planning a short trip along I-70, I-57 and I-64 the weekend of St.
Pat's Day and used the trip planner on AQ to help us map out the routes and
such. I think there are over 50 we could find but doubt we will get to finding
most of those as it takes some time to get to them, find them, stamp up and
put everything back.

Can anyone share with me what they have in their travel letterboxing pack
for such adventures as I want to make sure we are well prepared. Tonight I
just took both our stamps 3 different ink pads so we had a choice, the
directions to find the box, a pen and log book. I was glad I had baby wipes in the
car to clean the stamp before we used it and clean ours after stamping in. Not
sure that was really the right thing to use but figured if it is safe for a
baby's bottom it wouldn't hurt the stamps too much. Also wondering what
folks carry the needed supplies in on the trail too. So any input would be
greatly appreciated.

Glad I found the whole letterboxing thing ad can't wait to get a few more
finds under my belt so I feel comfortable enough to try some plants of my own.
The teens even acted a bit interested after they saw the stamp in the log
book and want to try carving their own now, though they acted like I was nuts
the first time I mentioned it and neither would come along tonight on the hunt.

Karen aka FlyGirl409

From: Scoutdogs (DogScouts@hotmail.com) | Date: 2007-03-06 10:35:11 UTC-05:00
Congrats on your first find! Sounds like you had the items you needed, but I would suggest adding a compass with a turnable housing (that you know how to use), water, flashlight (trust me), and some thick gloves for reaching into places you can't see.

Also, instead of baby wipes, wet some paper towels and place them in a baggie. Baby wipes can leave a scent on the stamp that can attract critters. Also, be sure to wash your hands before you go finding letterboxes if you have just eaten, so you don't add food oils t the outside/inside of a letterbox. This can also attract critters (same with perfume or scented hand lotion.) Critters are curious and if they think there's any chance the box or it's contents might be edible, they destroy the box.

Always be sure to hide the box well, completely from view with no clues to it's location. You want the hunt to be as challenging for the next person as it was for you. And don't want any non-letterboxers to see the box or physical clues and get curious. Especially intoxicated teens (if those are a problem in the area) that probably won't respect the letterbox.

For carving your stamps, I would suggest PZ Kut grade B white and a #1 speedball tool from www.Stampeaz.com Myself and many others really like these products. Goldilocks, you've already taken care of finding products that are too hard and too soft LOL Now get some PZ Kut and find out what's "just right."

Have fun! If you want to see some of the stamps I've carved on PZ Kut, take a look at my picturetrail below.

From: Coffee Beans (coffeebeans06@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-03-06 10:03:49 UTC-06:00
Welcome to the LbGLK and to letterboxing! :) It sounds like you are off
to a great start. As you find more boxes, you'll get a better idea of
how people generally carve their stamps. Atom118 and I started out
making our stamps from a polymer we use at work, but after a few weeks,
they fell apart. Carving takes some practice, but it sounds like you
and your husband have some artistic wood carving skills, so I'm sure
you'll both get the hang of it. :)

I just saw that ScoutDogs and Atom responded to your message, and I
agree with them about what to include in your pack -- certainly a
compass is a must since many clues involve taking bearings, a pen or two
to write with, ink pads, maybe a set of markers since some stamps are
colorful, a couple extra baggies in case a box should need some
maintenance, snacks, water, garden gloves for digging around and
reaching into hidey holes, small flashlight, and maybe some bandaids or
other light first aid items.

Have fun!

From: Wisconsin Hiker (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2007-03-06 16:22:03 UTC
We carry one other thing that was recommended on a list a few years
ago - duct tape. No, not a full roll of duct tape! Just take a
small/short pencil and then start wrapping duct tape around the body
of the pencil, winding around around to create your own "mini roll"
that takes up very little space in a pack. It proves very useful to
repair a crack in a box you might find (if you have no replacement
box along) and we've also used it to mend a stamp or two that were
broken in half. We simply lined up the pieces and then added a duct
tape backing to keep them together. Not the sturdiest "mounting" but
it works.

Wisconsin Hiker

From: vanman021657 (fholbein@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-03-06 17:18:56 UTC
FlyGirl409,

Congrats on your first find. Just wait until hubby (CMB) finds out
that that this is a great hobby to use a GPS with. Every single LB
that I have found I have marked the coordinates in my GPS unit. I
also have a photograph of every single LB that I have found. I've
started a blog that I've yet to update, but that too will list all my
LB's.

As for what to place on your logbook next to your stamp, it's up to
what your preferences are. Myself, I put the name of the
Placer/Owner, the name of the LB, the location I.E. xy park, or xy
woods, the closest city, the date, the day of the week and the time.
(oh, and the stamp too)! Sometimes if it's rainy, snowy, extremely
cold or hot, I'll make that comment as well. I started out using 3 x
5 index cards and just recently abondoned this as I was getting far
too many stamps that exceed 2 x 2 or 3 x 3 sizes.

A couple of other items that I'd like to mention for your bag. Take
an old rag or two. These come in handy for wiping out moist and damp
boxes. My traveling partner took off her socks and wiped out a
soaked box once is how we learned this lesson.

You mentioned baby wipes and someone else mentioned damp paper
towels. I've found that alcahol wipes work pretty good too, plus
they serve as a first aide item.

Also a note pad to help you keep track of your mental notes.
Especially when you find an HH and you've been out collecting several
boxes and can't remember which one you found the HH in or replanted
it in.

Can't stress enough about a good compass, and if you don't already
know how to use it, to learn quickly as this will aid you in finding
your treasure or going home empty handed. Also don't forget that a
lot of places use the pace method which equates to two steps. Keep
in mind they are talking about their pace and not yours, so you have
to allow for that.

Most of all, have fun and get the entire family involved.


VanMan/ Fred

From: (Mathw613@aol.com) | Date: 2007-03-10 22:17:20 UTC-05:00
We went out today, Tiptoe and Tonto we had the same experience (minus the
tight rope act) -- mud mud mud. Shriveled up feet and everything. We found 5
boxes today and had a GREAT time . . . snow, ice, mud and water.

This brings me back to this topic of what to take with you while
letterboxing. So far we find the box and walk it all the way back to the car to stamp in
and then I run it back and hide it in its place again.

In the future we will encounter stamps to far to run back to the car and the
question of "where do you stamp in?" comes up when all around you is nothing
but ground, leaves, dirt and mossy fallen trees (I can't bear the thought of
placing my log book in the dirt).

I've come up with an idea and that is bring an old checkers board with you
on your hunt (or scrabble or any other board game). When you get to the stamp
open it up and put the printed (or top) side down on the ground, fallen tree
etc. Put your log book on the board to stamp in -- so your log book doesn't
get dirty.

When you are done fold the game board in half and the dirt will be on the
inside, thus nothing in your bag would get dirty.

Hart x6

We also use a backpack/stool combo. If the one that
Wisconsin Hiker linked too is a little out of your
budget, check on Ebay - there's usually a variety
being sold there. Be forewarned, though, that the
quality might not be as good as from the Army surplus
site. Our first backpack/stool combo purchased from
Ebay for about $10 plus shipping kept falling apart on
us, and it was awkward to wear because of the
placement of the stool. But our new one is much more
comfortable - it's also one where the backpack can be
used with or without the stool.

For stamping in, we purchased a mini clipboard from
the local office supply super store. Since we use
index cards for our logbook, we just clip some index
cards on the board and then make our impression, and
we use the stool to lay the box and our stamping
supplies out on.

Doodle Bug.
