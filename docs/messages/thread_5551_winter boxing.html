From: Coffee Beans (coffeebeans06@yahoo.com) | Date: 2009-01-15 15:28:29 UTC-06:00
I think there are several more obvious issues to keep in mind with
winter boxing -- landmarks/trails being obscured due to snow and the
snow making trails towards boxes/caches very easy to spot -- but
something in particular that is maybe less obvious: the cold temps make
the plastic containers MUCH more susceptible to cracking/breaking. We
have on several occasions found boxes frozen in place inside
logs/stumps, so although we could see and touch the box, there was no
way we could remove the box from its place without damaging the box
itself. We accidentally broke one of our own boxes a while back while
trying to chip away at some ice on one side of the box -- it wasn't
major damage, but just a small crack is all it takes to make the insides
susceptible to water during the spring thaws, regardless of baggies.
And what a waste of a good lock-n-lock!

Coffee Beans

--- In LbGLK@yahoogroups.com, "pugnasties" wrote:

> We have not been winter boxing but I've been thinking about it (not

Here are a few we've gleaned from winter hiking websites and some blended with our own
letterboxing experiences. I'm sure that there are plenty of additional tips...So letterboxers
keep this topic going. It looks like we are going to have to adapt to winter letterboxing for
a long time here in the Great Lakes!

1. When choosing , look letterboxes for clues that explicitly state the box is hidden "in"
something - a stump, a hole in a tree, a cave, etc - less chance of finding a frozen box or
damaging a box if you have to dig. When finding a box entombed in ice, don't risk
damaging it by chipping at it and cracking the box. As disappointed as you will feel at not
getting that tricky box, it is better than laying awake nights with the guilt of contributing
to a ruined box!

2. Warm socks with some wool in them - NO COTTON. Waterproof boots! If you XC ski
or snowshoe - bring 'em! (Just be careful when counting paces or steps!)

3. Bring the right gear and hike with other people. Let someone know where you are
going and when you plan to return is a good idea any season.

4. Hiking is more difficult in winter than summer and often takes considerably more time.
Snow conditions underfoot, weather conditions, and navigation challenges on poorly
defined trails and above tree line can make for arduous going and take longer than
expected. Hikers need to set a turn-around time and stick to it, leaving the box if not
found to another day. Having a trail map of an area may help to find those snow covered
trails. Be careful around lakes, ponds, streams - don't assume them to be frozen - aka
soaking wet feet!

5. The right clothing/equipment can make or break a trip. Knee-high nylon gaiters keep
snow out of boots. Snow pants work well too. Trekking poles are useful for stability in
deep snow. Ski goggles, a Balaclava, neck warmer and face mask provide full face
protection against winter winds. For hands, fleece gloves with thin liner gloves and a
waterproof shell are standard.
Short winter days require starting out earlier in the day or use a headlamp with an LED
light and extra batteries, just in case you don't make it out of the woods before dark.
Clothing in winter is all about layering and controlling body temperature and moisture as
conditions dictate. Only synthetics will do NO COTTON....think about wet, cold blue jeans!

6. Carry hand and toe warmers-they come in handy! Put them next to your camera or
flashlight in cold weather to keep them working, too.

7. Also important to stay well-hydrated with drinkable (non-frozen) water throughout the
day. A Thermos of hot liquid helps ward off the afternoon chill. Plenty of high energy
snacks tucked into the outside pockets of your jacket or backpack for easy access is a
good idea, just remember to keep hands clean in order to not transfer good smelling
scents to potential box eating critters. Also, remember that most winter activities usually
use up to twice the energy of a similar summer event.

8. Duct tape! wrap some duct tape around your water bottle. It's easily accessible, you
know exactly where it is, and it doesn't take up space in your pack. Good for patching
ANYTHING that is broken; from blisters to cracked boxes!
Happy Trails
Tiptoe & Tonto

From: Debbie Kotlarek (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2009-01-15 21:00:33 UTC-06:00
Good recommendations from Tiptoe & Tonto. Here are a few more we can add:

1. Keep your ballpoint pen inside your mitten so it will be warm enough to
use!

2. If you want to take photos, keep your camera near your body (inside
pocket) so it is warm enough to use.

3. Those backpack/stool combinations are a better place to sit than the
snowy ground,

4. If the hike isn't too long, it might be better to take the box back to
the warm car to log in, then hike back to return the box to its proper home.

5. Umbrellas work well for snow in addition to rain. If flurries start
when you are logging in, use the umbrella to protect all the various
logbooks.

Wisconsin Hiker
