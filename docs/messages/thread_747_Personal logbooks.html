Re: [LbGLK] Personal logbooks

From: SpringChick (springchick@letterbox-mi.com) | Date: 2004-10-09 17:56:38 UTC-04:00
I do mine way #1 -- I use a special little leather log book (am just
finishing up #3) and stamp both finds and placements in chronological order.
Sometimes I will get them a bit out of order if I am doing a series and
don't quite finish but know that I will be back soon for the others -- I
will leave room to keep them together. And sometimes I will carve up a
bunch of stamps and log them as placements before they are actually placed.
Sometimes I do 1 stamp per page, sometimes 2, depending on the size of the
stamp, if they are related and how much I feel like writing -- I always put
the box name, the date, the placer and when it was placed and sometimes
other notes about the area, the trail, the weather, the clues, etc.

In the beginning my intent was to do a scrapbook type journal -- although it
probably wouldn't have been nearly so coooool as Del's -- but found I much
preferred spending my time carving stamps and making boxes than prettying up
my own log book. I also tried using blank 4"x6" index cards at one time --
when I finished up with log book #1 -- with the intent to slip them into a
photo album and make notes on the back of each one. I did this because the
perfectionist in me liked the idea of getting a second chance at a stamp and
also because it doesn't force the chronological thing and I could keep
placements in one section and finds in another and keep series together,
etc. But it didn't work for me -- I just ended up with a pile of stamped
index cards that somehow didn't appeal to the journal lover in me like
paging through my little leather book did. So I abandoned that method and
began little leather journal #2.

But... here is the anal part -- I didn't want my journals to have a gap and
there were about a dozen boxes I had found and stamped onto index cards
between journal #1 and #2. Fortunately they weren't any further away than
Kalamazoo and Saugatuck, so I went back (it was January as I recall) to get
them stamped into my new journal -- yup, climbed all 200+ stairs at Mt.
Baldy again (actually the 3rd time since I didn't find the box until the 2nd
time) and went back down to Kalamazoo at which time I discovered what a
creepy place Blanche Hull Nature Preserve is after dark (it's the whole RR
track thing -- I have a phobia).

I will have to say that Delirium's log book is the nicest I have seen and I
wish I had the discipline to keep such a nice record of my letterboxing
experiences.

SpringChick

>
> Oh! A question about what to do with the images you collect! Can I
answer this one? Can I? Pleeeeease?
>
> Ok, I think there was a topic of the week a while back about personal
logbooks, how did people arrange theirs and what did they do with those
images. If you search the archives, there's answers in messages 304, 300,
and 287 (although I seem to remember more). On the "big" letterboxing list
there will be many, many more ideas.
>
> I've personally seen three different ways to archive your found images
(though I'm sure there's more):
> - keeping the images in a book reserved just for the purpose, where you
stamp in, then record as much or as little as you like. My mother-in-law
does it this way, and she'll record the name of the box, the placer, the
date, and a little blurb about the trip.
> - keeping the images in a scrapbook style, which is what I do. I stamp in
on a piece of cardstock (good for me, since it usually takes me a couple of
tries to get a good image), then when I get home, I mount the image in a
book that's too big to carry around "in the field". I'll add pictures, and
a narrative.
> - index cards. The image is stamped on one side, and information about
the box is recorded on the other side. The cards are then filed in a card
box.
>
> Like I said, I'm sure there's more ways out there to record your finds,
and I think that after a very short time, your personal logbook becomes more
personal than a 7th grader girl's diary- and in my case, just as cluttered.
>
> Cheers,
> Del
>
> Gabrielle Coil wrote:
> >1. What do you do with the collected stamped images.

> >I mean - it sounds really cool to go on the treasure

> >hunt, but is there anything that anyone does with the

> >images you get??
>
> Good question. Being new to the hobby myself I really haven't thought
this one through at all. I suppose I'll be one of those people that just
has a collection of relatively useless images to look at from time to time
for some good memories and maybe to help introduce others to the hobby. At
the very least, it'll be something to harass my family with at family
gatherings and holidays.
>
> >2. Stamps...Is it ok to use the pre-made stamps

> >instead of carving my own?

>
> A lot of people really like to find hand-carved stamps in the boxes they
find, but I think most would agree that they're always thrilled to just
succesfully find a box regardless of whether the stamps are hand-made or
store bought. Personally I carve my own because I like to carve stamps
(which I didn't really know until I tried it) and because it's much cheaper
than buying stamps. However, if you have neither the time nor inclination
to carve I see no problem with using a stamp you already have (and aren't
very likely to use again).
>
> >3. Stamps in a box. I am seeing on the posts that a

> >lot of the stamps relate to the location of the box.

> >Is that something like, If I place a box in a forest I

> >would use a leaf stamp, if in a garden a flower stamp,

> >if in a cemetary, a headstone stamp? Or can they be

> >all totally unrelated - like "I placed this box on

> >Halloween I'm using a witch" type of thing?

>
> Letterboxing is young in the US and we're kind of making the "rules" up as
we go. Even in the UK where it has been fairly well established there
aren't really hard and fast rules. Some people think it's important for a
stamp to relate to it's location somehow while others don't really care. I
fall into the later category. So far, my stamps have very loosely related
to their location. My hidden village series is placed where it is because I
could find all of the elements they represent (sort of). My Kame Kasuri box
is placed on a trail where I came across a turtle while scouting for hiding
spots but it's more coincidence than planning. And my fish series is being
placed near water but the more important factor was that I really like the
park I'll be placing them in...not that any of the species in this series
live anywhere near the lake they're being placed near anyway. Some of my
other stamp ideas (such as Aina's name in Japanese characters) will have
absolutely nothing to do
> with their location. I think most would agree that you should plant
boxes where you feel they should go and that the journey is ultimately more
important than the box's actual content.
>
> >4. Clues.
>
> The kinds of clues you write are entirely up to you. You can make them as
easy or as difficult as you want to. If someone doesn't like how your clues
are written, they don't have to look for your box...and that's ok. My clues
(so far) have all been pretty easy and straight-forward because I'm not
terribly imaginative when I write them. They are also written that way, in
part, because the hobby is very new to this area and I feel that it's
important for new letterboxers to be succesful when they are just starting
out.
>
> >5. Leaving Clues. Do you leave clues in

> >Letterboxing.org only or are there other places I'd

> >need to post them? Is that where I would mention that

> >you need to bring your own stamp pad and other

> >notations on perhaps where you would park, etc?

>
> Letterboxing.org is probably the fastest way to get your clues out there
for people to see...but there are certainly other places. Some people have
their own websites they post clues on, you can post them to any letterboxer
lists you're on, or you could even make them word of mouth clues that don't
get posted anywhere. And yes, I always mention notations in my clues (where
to park, how to get to the location if it's hard to find, bring your own
ink, trail hazards [such as steep hills, terrain unsuitable for small
children or wheels, poision ivy, ticks, road crossings, etc], any rules
specific to the location [like if dogs aren't allowed], and an approximation
of trail length and/or how long the hike will take).
>
> Hope that answers your questions.
