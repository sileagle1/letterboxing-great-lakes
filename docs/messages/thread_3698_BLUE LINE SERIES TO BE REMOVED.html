From: Barb Funk (mmfunk99@greenapple.com) | Date: 2007-02-07 21:26:58 UTC-05:00
I have been struggling with whether or not to speak up about the boxes
that have been placed recently inside stores. Please know I respect
that everyone has their own perspective and so I kept quiet. I guess,
with the recent situations in Chicago and Boston, I would feel better if
I softly spoke up now. (Better later than never?!)

As a retail general manager of a large store, I can share the
perspective that if a customer, employee....or heaven forbid, child
found a letterbox that I was not aware of inside my store, I would have
had an immediate responsibility to evacuate the building while calling
911 and calling mall security.

It isn't even a decision I would have to think about because we are all
trained to follow the above guidelines. All I am respectfully
suggesting is that everyone who has recently placed a box inside a store
without permission, please reconsider the above and consider that
employees are trained to be on the lookout for shoplifters and other
suspicious behavior.

Some companies still use a physical attempt to stop shoplifters. Some
professional security carry firearms. It scares me to think a
letterboxer (of any age) might get into a really bad situation because
an alert employee (or a camera being watched by an employee in the back
rooms) saw suspicious behavior. For anyone else in retail management,
I'm sure you have had similar experiences. Those not in this business,
may not believe what retailers see on a weekly and sometimes daily
basis. And if a letterbox is wrapped in camo-tape....

In some ways, I know there isn't much difference between hiding a
camo-taped box out in the woods v. inside a store. I guess the only
difference is that inside a store or other public place there is a much
higher concentration of people and more of a chance to induce panic
and/or media attention.

Again, all I am suggesting is that the placement of boxes inside stores
without permission be reconsidered. I am counting my words as carefully
as I can via email and am sincerely concerned that someone might either
seriously scare someone or might get into trouble with police authorities.

Trying to softly suggest some things to consider....
Trailtracker

RE: [LbGLK] Re: BLUE LINE SERIES TO BE REMOVED

From: xxxxxxxx (BrighidFarm@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-02-07 21:49:12 UTC-06:00
There's no reason you shouldn't voice your opinion on how you see it from
your perspective. You certainly *should* speak up.

I can't see any sense in wrapping any box inside any establishment in
camo-tape. Camouflage tape is to camouflage a box in a woodsy, foresty type
setting. Camo-tape isn't really going to camouflage a box in an indoor
setting unless the box is hidden amongst the camo-tape. :-) But I can
picture some placers using camo-tape on an indoor box just because "that's
the way it's done." :-) No need for baggies with an indoor box either.

Mine was in a 35mm film canister. (I went over and pulled it tonight --
I'll probably go with the golf course idea.) Big box stores offer film
developing, so I figured if an employee found it, the most they would think
is that someone was dropping off their film, set it down while looking at
some merchandise, and then forgot it and lost track of where they set it.
If they opened it, they'd find the stamp and logbook.

And the reason I do it word-of-mouth is so that I can totally control who's
going to go looking for it to know that they're the sort of boxers who are
extremely unlikely to demonstrate any "suspicious looking" activity.
There's nothing to "shoplift" because they're not leaving the store with the
box. I would think that no security person would attempt to use deadly
force on a potential shoplifter. Is it even legal to use deadly force on
someone who's suspected of shoplifting?

But yep, someone could still panic or get upset when they find it, and there
could be trouble.

I remember a thread quite awhile back, I think on the big list, about using
magnets to attach boxes to outdoor electrical boxes, which is what happened
in New Hampshire, if I'm not mistaken, with the geocache.

And there was also the discussion awhile back of using magnets to attach
letterboxes underneath collection mailboxes in cities. I can't remember
whether that was on the big list or the urban boxing list.

But I know, as a Postal employee, that we're trained to immediately contact
the police, the hazmat folks, the Postal Inspectors, and also frequently the
FBI, if we see any type of unauthorized box attached to or inside of or
sitting under or next to any USPS collection box or if we see anyone acting
suspiciously around a box. It's taken so seriously because we've already
had bombs go off that have seriously maimed Postal workers and things like
poisons placed in envelopes have already killed Postal workers. Not to
mention that any mail already deposited in that box might end up destroyed
as the bomb squad goes about "removing" the suspicious item -- sometimes
they don't bother using a lot of finesse with those types of jobs. So
federal charges could end up filed against both placer and finder.
Destruction of federal property. Destruction of the mail.

Funny thing is, when I was a collection route driver, I never did really
worry about bombs or poisons except once when someone deposited a really
suspicious package in one of my collection box. It got turned over to the
police and then to the Inspectors, then to the FBI, and I never did find out
whether it was a threat or was benign. Mostly I worried about idiots who
put live rats down the box chutes, live cats down the box chutes, dripping
ice cream cones down the box chutes, used condoms down the box chutes, live
snakes down the box chutes. I only found one package that was actually
suspicious. I probably found a hundred or so of gross-but-not-suspicious
stuff. But the only thing that ended up with the Postal Inspectors and with
the FBI was that package. They didn't care about the cats, the rats, the
snakes, the ice cream cones, or the condoms. Those they considered *my*
problem. :-) Not the problem of Homeland Security.

Barb, I certainly agree...........best for each placer to rethink where each
of our boxes is placed in today's climate and be sure we're comfortable with
where we've placed those boxes.

~~ Mosey ~~

Trailtracker, you make some very very points here. Thank you for sharing your perspective with us. I know there are some people who get kicks out of hiding and finding the urban /indoor boxes and that is the way that they choose to play the game, but I am just not stealthy enough, or at least I don't feel I am and thus no doubt, make myself look suspicious in these situations. As one who has had an unnerving experience with the authorities regarding a questionably placed urban /indoor letterbox, I long ago swore off all but the most innocuous of these types of boxes. I have to admit that my first reaction after my NYC /FBI experience was "geez, it is just a letterbox." But there really is no way to know that at first glance and the policy in most places is not to investigate, rather to immediately contact authorities. It is indeed sad that world events of the past several years has made us a paranoid society, but for better or worse that is reality and there has been a huge attitude shift to "better safe than sorry" which has prompted organizations to instruct their employees to immediately take precautionary steps when they see any suspicious behavior or items that do not belong in their environment.

I asked myself why I was attracted to letterboxing in the first place -- sure there is the challenge of finding boxes, but outside of that, my primary attractions were beautiful locations with great hikes (a store?) and FUN! Confrontation with the authorities over a letterbox is not, in my opinion, fun.

I am glad to hear that the Indoor Letterbox project was cancelled.

SpringChick

From: xxxxxxxx (BrighidFarm@comcast.net) | Date: 2007-02-08 10:02:52 UTC-06:00
Ooooooooooh, I would never ever EVER want any type of job cleaning
hotels/motels. Those folks deserve big tips just for taking the job in my
opinion. Hotels/motels couldn't survive without 'em and it's got to be
disgusting at times.

They've never told us that we couldn't say anything. The window clerks are
supposed to check for the same things when they take in a package, and are
supposed to ask senders a list of particular questions. The collection
carriers, unfortunately, don't have the benefit of having the sender right
in front of them face-to-face, so we have to be even more careful.

The package was "Unibomber Style." It was very obvious.

The address it was being sent to was made from words cut from
magazines/newspapers, pasted on, and then covered with clear shipping tape.
The return address was a false address (but was hand written). I happened
to realize right away that the return addy wasn't legit since I'd done the
section of town where the return address was supposedly located so I knew
there was no such office building with that address. It was addressed to a
person at a university in Arizona, which wasn't a good sign either.

It wasn't, however, ticking or anything. :-) But from what I understand,
modern day bombs don't need to be "ticking." I guess the ticking stuff is
mostly in old movies.

So there was no second guessing for me as to whether or not I should
consider this a "suspicious" package. I called the supervisor right away on
my cell phone and was told "Don't touch it! Don't touch it!" I told 'er,
well gee, I wasn't about to. Not My Job Man. :-) I did like I was
supposed to and sat in my truck at a good distance from the package and
stopped anyone who came along to mail a letter in that box before the police
got there.

For all I know, it might even have been a Postal Inspection "plant." They
do those types of things sometimes to check up to see that we're following
the rules and regs the way we're supposed to. But we're never told the
final disposition of any of these cases.

The Service is now in the process of putting video cameras in a lot of Post
Office lobbies and window lines. We just got one a couple of months ago at
our place. The camera is connected to the offices at the big sectional
center. Serves two purposes actually. It can identify when lines get
really long and window clerk supervisors don't do anything to get things
moving along faster (something needed at some of our facilities). And it
can identify people who might need to be identified later should there be an
attempted hold-up or someone mailing a package that really did turn out to
be dangerous where someone got killed or maimed. Like banks, we'll now be
able to get some idea of what the person looks like.

So don't pick yer nose or scratch yer butt while you're standing in line at
the P.O. -- you just might be on Candid Camera. About all ours has showed
so far are things like four year olds throwing temper tantrums when they
find out the P.O. doesn't sell candy.

I don't know why we don't put in candy. Like Fry's Electronics. Fry's
really should have nothing to do with selling candy. But in the long walk
thru the check-out line to the cashier, the aisle is just lined solid with
candy on both sides. The P.O. is missing a great source of revenue here. I
never get out of Fry's without *some*thing to eat. And they've got that
little cafe in the middle of the store? It has niiiice turkey and avocado
sandwiches? We could use one of those too -- a little cafe in the lobby.
Diversification, that's where it's at these days. Look at Costco and
Walmart -- they sell lots of food a person can go home and
cook...........but just in case a person is into instant gratification
eating and not interested in going home and cooking.....there's a little
place where a person can hotdogs, soda, pizza, etc. Even IKEA has a little
food court inside their store. And no matter how lousy the food is, folks
are always in those places eating it.....particularly folks with children.
And look how many people drag their children along to the P.O. We need to
give these four year olds what they want! They don't want stamps. They
want a hot dog! They want a Milky Way!

With senior citizens in mind, we did put out a bench. But what did the
Postmaster do? Put it outside the building. So that in the summer when
it's 110 in the shade, or when it's pouring rain, or right now when it's -15
without the windchill, seniors aren't exactly going to be interested.
*No*body is exactly going to be interested. I was told we couldn't put it
inside because it might encourage folks to "socialize" in the lobby. Well
maybe if they were "socializing", they wouldn't notice how long they were
standing in line. Frankly, *I* think we should line the wall leading up to
the window clerks with one loooong bench. Sit down and relax while ya wait
and just scoot yer butt along as the line moves forward. But nobody's
buyin' my ideas.

Stepping off my Postal soapbox now...............
