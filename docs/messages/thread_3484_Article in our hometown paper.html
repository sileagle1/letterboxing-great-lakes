Letterboxing sees modern-day revival
Published Thursday, December 21, 2006





By MARY JOHNS
EagleHerald staff writer

Parks, often tucked away in quiet neighborhoods or behind commercial areas, are places of great wonder.
There lie hidden treasures: homes for all manner of wildlife and evidence of changing seasons.
There are other, less obvious treasures in many parks and other public places, known only to those who practice the 19th century pursuit of letterboxing.
Letterboxers hide small, weatherproof boxes in public places, usually parks. They post clues online as to the boxes' whereabouts.
Inside the letterbox is a rubber stamp and a notebook. People who search for letterboxes -- and anyone can do it -- must bring along a rubber stamp, notebook, pen and compass.
When they find the letterbox, they add their own stamp to the book and stamp their book with the stamp that was inside the letterbox. In each book, a notation is made: when and where the box was found.
Letterboxing has its roots in 1854 England, but it has been revived in the past seven or eight years in the United States.
Tom and Jill Hemr are former area residents who got involved in letterboxing when they lived in Detroit.
"We found out about it in the winter of 2003," says Tom Hemr. "We attended a gathering of letterboxers, the first one in Michigan, and we met several people there."
The Hemrs learned more about letterboxing -- and they got hooked.
Letterboxing is a bit stealthy -- in a good way -- and that adds to the intrigue.
"You kind of do it undercover," says Hemr. "You don't do it in front of anyone."
For that reason, letterboxers are reluctant to advertise exactly where they've stashed their letterboxes. Suffice it to say there are some hidden locally.
In most cases, the weatherproof boxes are hidden in the stumps or hollows of trees. The boxes are usually sealed in plastic bags to further protect them from the elements.
"Also, you try to hide it so it is not exposed," says Hemr.
Letterboxers are encouraged to be neat and considerate when they do their stamping, carefully wiping off the stamp in the box.
Stamps are a big part of letterboxing. Hemr says that when people start this hobby, they are often content with ready-made stamps. But as they get into it more, they often create their own stamps.
"They're usually hand-carved," he says. "Hobby stores have kits that allow you to carve your own."
Letterboxers track the boxes they've found and the friends they've made.
The hobby attracts a mix of people, said Hemr. It's often a family activity.
"Kids can do it, too," says Hemr.
There's a code of conduct for letterboxers, and of course, the hobby had its own jargon. To learn more, access www.letterboxing.org.
